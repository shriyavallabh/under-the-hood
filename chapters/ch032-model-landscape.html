The model landscape

Six names, four questions, one map. After this chapter, every model announcement lands in a slot you already have — instead of on the guilty pile of things you meant to look up.

~25 minChapter 32 of 123
The story

You have just moved to a new city, and a colleague asks the only question that matters: "So — where do we eat?" A fool tries to memorise every restaurant. A local learns the shape of the food scene instead. There are three famous kitchen houses downtown; you can only eat at their counters, because their recipes are locked in a safe. Across town there is a marvellous open shelf: kitchens that publish their full recipes, so anyone with a decent stove can cook the dish at home, tweak the spices, even serve it commercially. And within every kitchen there are tiers — the twelve-chef tasting-menu experience for the anniversary, and the fast, reliable counter meal for an ordinary Tuesday.

Nobody sane eats the tasting menu daily, and nobody proposes marriage over a counter snack. Knowing the city means knowing the houses, knowing which recipes are public, and matching the occasion to the tier. The LLM market is exactly this city. The kitchen houses are OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. The open shelf holds Llama, Mistral, and Qwen. And you are about to become a local.

Where we are on the map

A quick recap, because this chapter stands on the last two. In what is a model you learned that a model is not code — it is a big file of learned numbers, produced by training and loaded at prediction time. In LLMs vs deterministic models you split the word "model" in two: the deterministic kind (a fraud scorer that gives the same answer every time) and the generative kind (an LLM that writes). This chapter zooms into the LLM half only and answers the question your colleagues answer with brand names: who actually makes these things, and how do the offerings differ?

Here is the liberating secret: you do not need to track individual models. Version numbers churn every few months and any list would be stale before you finished reading it. What stays stable — for years now — is the structure of the market. Learn the structure once and every future announcement just updates one cell of a table you already own.

Four questions beat a hundred names

Every model that exists, or will exist, is located by four questions:

1. Which house made it? A handful of organisations train serious LLMs, because training one costs somewhere between a nice house and a small airport. Each house has a recognisable style, a naming scheme, and a product line — like car makers.

2. Can you download the weights? The weights are the learned numbers themselves — the modern, giant cousin of the PKL file your data-science colleagues pickle to disk. If the maker publishes that file for anyone to download and run, the model has open weights. If the file never leaves the maker's servers and you may only talk to it over the internet, the weights are closed — you rent conversation, you never hold the recipe.

3. Which tier is it? Every house sells a range, from small-and-cheap to what the industry calls a frontier model — the most capable model anyone can offer at that moment, priced accordingly. Same brand, very different machinery under the badge.

4. How does it reach you? Either as an API — you send a web request to the maker's computers and the answer comes back, which is what the first chapter of the next act has you doing yourself — or by self-hosting: downloading open weights and running them on hardware you control.

Hold those four questions and the vocabulary below, and the rest of the chapter is a guided walk.

Open vs closed: what you are actually trading

The open/closed split sounds ideological, but for a working professional it is a plain commercial trade. Closed weights buy you the strongest models with zero infrastructure: the frontier tiers from the big three houses have led most capability benchmarks most of the time, and using them is one HTTPS request. The costs are dependence — their prices, their outages, their deprecation schedule — and the fact that your prompts travel to someone else's computer, which some regulated data simply may not do.

Open weights buy you control. The file is on your disk; nobody can raise the price of running it, retire it while your product depends on it, or read the customer records you feed it. A large bank that must keep client data on premises is not choosing open weights for the vibes — it is often the only lawful option. The bill: you provide the GPUs, the serving stack, the monitoring, and you accept that the very best open model usually trails the very best closed one by some months.

Careful

"Open weights" is not "open source", and it is not "do whatever you like". Most open releases publish the weights but not the training data or full training code, and each carries its own licence — some truly permissive (Apache 2.0), some with acceptable-use terms or scale thresholds. Before anyone builds a product on an open model, someone must actually read its licence. In a bank, that someone has a legal team on speed dial.

Small vs frontier: the tier ladder

Inside every house, the same ladder: a small tier, a middle tier, a frontier tier. The names differ — mini and full, Flash and Pro, Haiku, Sonnet, Opus — but the trade is identical: capability against cost and speed. And the gap is not subtle. It is not 20 percent; it is one to two orders of magnitude in price between the bottom and top of a ladder.

That ratio drives one of the most common patterns in production systems, one you have almost certainly operated without naming it: tiering. Route the flood of easy, repetitive work — classify this ticket, extract these fields, summarise this thread — to a small model, and reserve the frontier tier for the genuinely hard calls: multi-step reasoning, delicate writing, agent orchestration. The working rule is worth memorising: use the smallest model that passes your quality check, and let evidence, not brand anxiety, push you up the ladder.

Tip

When someone says "just use the best model for everything", translate it to the restaurant version: "let's cater the office canteen from the tasting-menu kitchen". The food would be wonderful. The invoice would end the experiment within a month.

Fine-tuning vs prompting, in one paragraph

One more fork you will hear debated: should we prompt the model or fine-tune it? Prompting changes only the instructions you send — the weights stay untouched, so it is instant, reversible, and free to iterate; everything you have built as an operator so far is prompting. Fine-tuning is additional training that actually nudges the weights using your own examples — it needs curated data, compute, and evaluation, and it produces a new model variant you must then version and maintain. The professional rule of thumb: exhaust prompting first (and retrieval — giving the model your documents at ask-time — which a later act covers properly), because it solves most problems at a fraction of the effort; reach for fine-tuning when you need consistent form rather than new facts — a house style, a strict output format, a specialised tone — or when you must make a small cheap model perform a narrow job that currently needs an expensive one. Fine-tune for form, prompt for facts, and be suspicious of anyone who proposes fine-tuning before showing you the prompt they gave up on.

Keeping the map without chasing every release

Model launches now arrive weekly, each with a triumphant chart. Trying to follow all of them is a hobby, not a job skill. Here is the low-effort maintenance plan.

Track houses, not models. The six or so names in this chapter change slowly — years, not weeks. When a headline lands, first ask "which house, which district?" and most of the meaning arrives free of charge.

Only re-open a decision when an answer changes. A release deserves your attention only if it changes an answer to one of the four questions for a system you run: a tier gets dramatically cheaper, an open model reaches a capability class that was closed-only, a licence changes, a model you depend on is deprecated. Everything else is spectator sport.

Read the model card, not the launch thread. When a release does matter, the model card gives you sizes, context window, licence, and intended use in five minutes of primary-source reading — better information than an hour of commentary.

Treat leaderboards as weather, not law. Public benchmark rankings are useful for spotting the rough capability class and nothing more; models can be tuned to shine on famous tests. The only leaderboard that matters for your system is your own quality check on your own tasks — a discipline this course builds up later, and the true final answer to "which model should we use?".

In the wild

The entire landscape usually condenses to a single string in a config file. Skim any agent repository and you will find it: model: "gpt-…" or claude-sonnet-… or gemini-…-flash in a YAML or .env file, or a Hugging Face repo path like meta-llama/… in a vllm serve command — that last shape is the tell that a team is self-hosting open weights. When a merge request changes just that one line, the diff is one line but the decision is this whole chapter: house, weights, tier, and road all at once. You are now equipped to review it.

Check yourself

1. A colleague says a new model is "open weights". What exactly is being offered?

Open weights means the weights file itself is published. Training data and code usually stay private, and a licence still applies — which is why "open weights" and "open source" are not the same claim.

2. A regulator rules that customer data may never leave your organisation's own servers. Which question of the map does that settle first?

An API call, by definition, transmits your prompt to the maker's servers. If data cannot leave the building, the delivery road is decided before any debate about brands or tiers begins.

3. What distinguishes a "reasoning" model line, like OpenAI's o-series?

Reasoning models trade time and tokens for depth: they think through hard problems step by step before producing the answer. Every major house now offers some version of this fast-line/thinking-line split.

4. Your team's summariser works, but the frontier-tier bill is climbing. What does this chapter's working rule suggest first?

Summarising is exactly the narrow, high-volume work the small tiers exist for, at 10 to 100 times lower cost. The rule: smallest model that passes your check — and let evidence, not brand anxiety, push you up the ladder.

5. A splashy new model launches tomorrow. When does it deserve more than a headline's worth of your attention?

Track houses, not models. A release matters when it flips a decision you own: a tier gets far cheaper, an open model reaches a formerly closed-only capability class, a licence shifts, or your current model is deprecated. Otherwise it is spectator sport.

Key takeaways

  • You do not track models; you track a structure. Four questions locate anything: which house, open or closed weights, which tier, API or self-hosted.
  • The closed counters are OpenAI (GPT line plus the o-series reasoning models), Anthropic (Claude: Haiku, Sonnet, Opus), and Google (Gemini Flash and Pro). The open shelf is led by Meta's Llama, Mistral, and Qwen — with crossovers like Google's Gemma and OpenAI's gpt-oss.
  • Open weights means the file of learned numbers is downloadable and self-hostable — it does not mean open training data, and a licence always still applies.
  • Tiers inside a house differ by 10 to 100 times in price. Use the smallest model that passes your own quality check, and route easy volume down the ladder.
  • Prompting changes instructions and costs nothing to iterate; fine-tuning changes the weights and costs data, compute, and maintenance. Fine-tune for form, prompt for facts — and exhaust prompting first.
  • A release earns your attention only when it changes an answer to one of the four questions for a system you run. When it does, read the model card, not the hype thread.
