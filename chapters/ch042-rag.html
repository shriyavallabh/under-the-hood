RAG: retrieval-augmented generation

The model is brilliant but has never read your documents. RAG hires it a librarian. By the end of this chapter you will have built the entire pipeline — embeddings, similarity search, prompt stuffing, grounded answer — in pure Python you can run right here.

The story

A company keeps forty years of records in a grand old archive room — every policy, every contract, every memo. In the reading room sits a genius consultant who has read every published book on Earth but not one of these files. You ask her, "How many days of paid leave do I get?" She has two bad options: guess from general knowledge (confident, plausible, quite possibly wrong), or read all forty years of files aloud before answering (ruinously slow and expensive).

Then the librarian steps in. Months ago she went shelf by shelf and wrote an index card for every folder — not the folder's title, but a fingerprint of what it is about — and she arranged the cards so that similar topics sit near each other. Now she hears your question, works out where it lives in that arrangement, walks straight to the right neighbourhood, pulls the three most relevant folders, and drops them on the genius's desk: "Answer from these, and say which page." Thirty seconds later you have a correct answer with a citation.

The librarian is retrieval. The card catalogue is a vector store. The drop-on-the-desk is prompt stuffing. The whole arrangement is RAG — and it is the single most common way enterprises put LLMs to work.

The problem RAG solves

Quick recap from tokens, embeddings and context: a model's knowledge is frozen at training time, squashed into its weights as a blurry statistical summary. It has never seen your leave policy or yesterday's incident report, and it cannot look anything up on its own. Ask it about your handbook anyway and it produces the most plausible-sounding text — a polite description of a hallucination.

There are three honest ways to give a model knowledge it lacks. Retrain it (fine-tuning — slow, costly, and as we will see, wrong for facts). Paste your entire corpus into every prompt (the meter from the previous chapter starts spinning like a fan). Or fetch only the passages relevant to this question and paste just those. That third option is retrieval-augmented generation — RAG — a pipeline that searches your documents for the best-matching passages and stuffs them into the prompt so the model answers from evidence instead of memory.

Fix one thing in your head early: RAG is not a model feature. There is no rag=True switch on any API. It is plumbing — ordinary code wrapped around the same chat call from your first API call. The model never knows a retrieval system exists; it just receives a prompt that happens to contain exactly the right paragraphs.

Embeddings: filing by meaning, not by keyword

The librarian's index cards are the clever part. An embedding is a list of numbers — a vector — that positions a piece of text as a point in a huge "meaning space", such that texts about similar things land near each other. You met the idea in chapter 30; RAG is where it earns its salary. "Reimbursement limits for travel meals" and "how much can I claim for food on a work trip" share almost no words, yet their embeddings sit practically on top of each other. That makes this semantic search, not keyword search: it matches what you mean, not what you typed.

To measure "near", the standard tool is cosine similarity — a score for the angle between two vectors: 1.0 means pointing the same way (same meaning), around 0 means unrelated.

Two practical notes. Embedding models are separate from chat models — small, fast, and cheap, which is why embedding a million documents is routine. And vectors are only comparable within one model's space: embed documents with one model and queries with another and the similarity scores are meaningless — a classic production bug.

The whole system is two halves that run at different times. Ingestion happens offline — nightly, or whenever a document changes. Query time happens in the second after a user hits Enter. Step through it:

A vector database stores each vector alongside the original chunk text and metadata (source file, section, permissions), and answers "which stored vectors are nearest to this one?" in milliseconds across millions of chunks. Ingestion done — all before any user asked anything.

Note

Steps 1–3 run offline on a schedule; steps 4–7 run per question in about a second. This split is why RAG scales: the expensive reading of the whole archive happens once, and each question pays only for a lookup plus a small prompt.

Chunking: the unglamorous decision that decides everything

Chunking — how you cut documents into passages — sounds like a formality and is actually the highest-leverage knob in the pipeline. The tension is geometric: one embedding must summarise one chunk. Make chunks tiny (a sentence each) and every vector is razor-sharp, but the retrieved text arrives stripped of its surroundings — the table of amounts without the heading saying which grade of employee it covers. Make chunks huge (twenty pages) and each vector becomes a blurry average of many topics, retrieval turns vague, and every hit drags thousands of irrelevant tokens into the prompt — the meter again.

Sensible defaults that survive production: chunks of a few hundred tokens; 10–15% overlap between neighbours so a sentence straddling a boundary is not cut in half; and best of all, structure-aware splitting — cut along headings and paragraphs, and prepend the document title and section heading to each chunk so an orphaned passage still knows where it came from.

Careful

When a RAG bot gives a weird answer, almost everyone's first instinct is to blame the model. Look at the retrieved chunks first. Nine times out of ten the model answered perfectly reasonably given the wrong or mangled passages it was handed — a retrieval problem wearing a hallucination costume. Debugging RAG means debugging the librarian before the genius.

Why grounding cuts hallucination — and where it does not

An ungrounded model sits a closed-book exam: it reconstructs answers from compressed memories, and when memory runs out it improvises fluently. RAG turns it into an open-book exam: the right passage is physically present in the context, the instructions say "answer only from this, cite the source, say when it is missing", and models are far better at reading than remembering. Citations do double duty — the user can click through and verify, converting "trust me" into "check me", which is the difference between a demo and something a compliance team signs off.

But keep your scepticism. RAG reduces hallucination; it does not abolish it. If retrieval fetches a plausible-but-wrong passage, the model produces a confident wrong answer with a citation — the most dangerous failure in the genre. Stale index, stale answers. And a model can still occasionally blend memory into the evidence. Mature teams evaluate the halves separately: "did the right chunk arrive?" (retrieval quality) and "did the model stick to it?" (faithfulness).

RAG vs long context vs fine-tuning

Modern models accept enormous contexts — hundreds of thousands of tokens — so why not paste the whole handbook into every prompt? For a small corpus and a handful of questions, honestly, that works. At scale it collapses for three reasons. Cost: the full corpus is re-sent and re-billed on every question, while RAG sends a few chunks. Focus: models get measurably worse at using facts buried in the middle of a huge context, whereas retrieval hands over exactly the relevant paragraphs. And ceiling: no context window holds ten million documents, but a vector store shrugs — and can filter by permissions, so each user's retrieval only touches documents they may see. That last one, access control, is quietly the biggest enterprise reason of all.

Fine-tuning is the other pretender. Retraining a model on your documents adjusts its weights — excellent for teaching behaviour: tone, output format, domain vocabulary, house style. It is a terrible way to store facts: knowledge goes in blurry, cannot be cited, cannot be deleted, and is stale the day after training. RAG keeps facts outside the model, where they update the moment you re-index an edited file. The industry's rule of thumb: fine-tune for behaviour, retrieve for knowledge — and plenty of systems do both.

What production RAG code looks like

Your toy and a production system differ in three swaps: hand-made vectors become calls to a real embedding model, the DOCS dict becomes a real vector database (FAISS, Chroma, pgvector inside Postgres, Pinecone — different clients, identical verbs), and fake_llm becomes the chat call from chapter 34. rag_service.py — the shape you will meet in real repos
chunks = chunk_markdown(handbook, size=500, overlap=60)
Ingestion starts with chunking: ~500 tokens per passage, 60 tokens of overlap so no sentence is beheaded at a boundary. The unit is tokens, not characters — both the embedding model and the prompt budget are metered in tokens (ch41).
vectors = embedding_model.embed([c.text for c in chunks])
One API call embeds the whole batch — the square-bracket expression is a list comprehension (ch8). Embedding a million chunks is an evening, not a project.
store.upsert(ids, vectors, texts, metadata)
"Upsert" = insert-or-update: re-ingesting an edited document overwrites its old vectors instead of duplicating them. Metadata (source file, section, access group) rides along with each vector.
# --- everything above runs offline, on a schedule ---
The ingestion half typically lives in a nightly job or fires when a document changes. Users never wait for it.
def answer(question, user):
qv = embedding_model.embed([question])[0]
Embedded with the SAME model as the chunks. Mixing embedding models between ingestion and query is the classic silent killer: no error, just garbage similarity scores.
hits = store.query(qv, top_k=4, filter=user.access)
Top-k nearest neighbours, filtered to documents this user may see. That one filter argument is why security teams approve RAG and veto "paste everything into the prompt".
context = format_with_sources(hits)
Each chunk is labelled with its source ("[expense-rules.md, section 3]") so the model can cite, exactly like your toy did.
prompt = GROUNDING_RULES + context + question
Prompt stuffing is string concatenation — that is the whole secret. GROUNDING_RULES says: answer only from the context, cite sources, say "not found" when it is not there (ch37).
return chat_model.generate(prompt), sources(hits)
One ordinary chat-model call, plus the source list so the UI can render clickable citations. Everything upstream existed only to make this prompt good.

One forward pointer: in Act V retrieval often stops being a fixed pipeline step and becomes a tool the model chooses to call — deciding for itself when to look something up and re-searching when the first haul is thin. People call that agentic RAG; you will recognise it as this chapter plus a loop.

In the wild

RAG is, by a wide margin, the most common enterprise LLM pattern: nearly every "ask our documentation" assistant, HR-policy bot, support-deflection widget, and legal-research tool is this exact pipeline. You will spot it in the artefacts: a pgvector extension appearing in a Postgres migration, a nightly ingest job in the scheduler, an "embeddings" line item on the API bill next to the chat models, config keys named chunk_size, chunk_overlap, and top_k, and merge requests titled "increase k to 6" or "switch to heading-aware chunking". And when someone in a review says "the bot hallucinated", you now know the first question to ask: what did the retriever return?

Check yourself

1. At answer time, how does the model actually receive the retrieved passages?

There is no side channel. Retrieval happens in your code, and the winning chunks are concatenated into the prompt string. The model just sees a prompt that happens to contain the evidence — RAG is plumbing around a normal chat call.

2. Why must the query be embedded with the same embedding model used for the documents?

An embedding is coordinates in one specific model's learned meaning space. Compare coordinates from two different maps and "nearby" is noise. Mixing embedding models between ingestion and query is a classic silent production bug.

3. You re-chunk your handbook into giant 20-page chunks. What goes wrong?

One embedding must summarise one chunk. Twenty pages of mixed topics average into a vector that matches everything weakly, and each retrieved hit drags mostly-irrelevant tokens into the prompt. Chunking is a precision-versus-context trade, not a formality.

4. Your leave policy changed yesterday. Which approach reflects the change in today's answers, cheapest?

RAG keeps facts outside the model, so freshness is just re-indexing one file. Fine-tuning bakes facts in blurrily, cannot cite, and needs a retrain per update — it is for behaviour and style, not for knowledge. Fine-tune for behaviour, retrieve for knowledge.

5. A user asks about something genuinely absent from the corpus. What should a well-built RAG system do?

Grounding rules include the refusal path: "if it is not in the context, say so." A grounded refusal is a feature, not a failure — the silent fallback to memory is exactly the confident hallucination RAG exists to prevent.

Key takeaways

  • RAG is not a model feature — it is plumbing: search your documents for the passages relevant to the question and paste them into an ordinary prompt so the model answers from evidence, with citations.
  • The pipeline has two timelines: ingestion offline (chunk, embed, store in a vector database) and query time online (embed the question, top-k retrieve by cosine similarity, stuff the prompt, generate).
  • Embeddings turn meaning into geometry — similar texts land nearby even with zero shared words — and both sides must use the same embedding model or similarity scores are noise.
  • Chunking is the highest-leverage knob: a few hundred tokens, some overlap, split along headings. When answers go weird, inspect the retrieved chunks before blaming the model.
  • Grounding reduces hallucination but does not abolish it — wrong retrieval yields confident wrong answers with citations, so evaluate retrieval and faithfulness separately.
  • Versus alternatives: long context re-bills the whole corpus every question and dilutes attention; fine-tuning teaches behaviour, not fresh facts. Fine-tune for behaviour, retrieve for knowledge — which is why RAG is the most common enterprise LLM pattern alive.
