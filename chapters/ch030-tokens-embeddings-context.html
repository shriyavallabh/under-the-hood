Tokens, embeddings, and context windows

Three quantities govern every model you operate: what a token costs, where an embedding sits, and how much fits on the desk. Learn them once and half of your production mysteries dissolve on sight.

~30 minChapter 30 of 117
The story

There is an old telegram office where you pay per rubber stamp, not per word. Decades of traffic taught the clerks which phrases keep recurring — ARRIVING TUESDAY, WEDDING CONFIRMED, SEND MONEY — so each of those was carved as a single stamp. One stamp, one charge, however long the phrase. But hand the clerk an unusual surname or a chemical formula and he shrugs and spells it out letter by letter: eleven stamps, eleven charges. Behind him stands a vast pigeonhole wall where old telegrams are filed by what they are about, not alphabetically — wedding messages cluster in one warm corner, condolences a shelf below, shipping notices far across the wall. Even a brand-new clerk can find "messages like this one" just by reaching into the same neighbourhood. And the clerk works at a small desk. He can spread out only so many forms at once; when a long exchange overflows it, the oldest pages get swept into a drawer — out of sight, and as far as his work is concerned, out of existence.

That office is a large language model. The stamps are tokens. The pigeonhole wall is embeddings. The desk is the context window. This chapter is a tour of all three.

Where we are coming from

Two chapters ago, why text broke ML ended on a cliffhanger: models can only eat numbers, so text had to become numbers somehow, and doing it badly loses the meaning. Then the transformer chapter showed you the machine that reads a sequence of those numbers and lets every position pay attention to every other. What we skipped, deliberately, was the plumbing on either side of that machine: how your sentence gets chopped into pieces on the way in, what each piece turns into, and how many pieces the machine can hold at once. That plumbing is this chapter — and it is where the practical consequences live. Bills, rate limits, "context length exceeded" errors, agents that mysteriously forget: all of it traces back to these three ideas.

Tokens: the stamps in the drawer

A token is the smallest unit of text a language model reads, writes, and bills for — usually a chunk of a word, sometimes a whole word, sometimes a single character. The model never sees letters or words as you do; it sees a sequence of token IDs, plain integers, one per stamp.

The tokenizer is the component that does the chopping — a completely deterministic, non-AI piece of code that converts text to token IDs and back. It owns a fixed vocabulary of chunks, typically somewhere around 100,000 of them, and here is the part worth remembering: that vocabulary was learned from frequency, not grammar. The standard recipe is called Byte-Pair Encoding (BPE) — start with bare characters, scan a mountain of training text, and repeatedly merge the pair of chunks that occurs most often, until the vocabulary is full. Common sequences earn their own stamp. Rare ones never do.

The consequences are exactly the telegram clerk's rulebook:

  • "the" is one token. So are most common English words — often with the preceding space glued on, as " the".
  • "internationalization" is rare enough that it gets assembled from a few sub-word chunks.
  • A rare proper noun, a typo, or a serial number shatters into many tiny pieces — each one billed.
  • A useful rule of thumb for English: one token is about four characters, or about three-quarters of a word. A thousand tokens is roughly 750 words.

Why the count matters: money and walls

Tokens would be a charming curiosity if they were free. They are not — they are the billing unit and the rationing unit of the entire LLM economy.

Money. Every model API prices in tokens, quoted per million: one price for input tokens (everything you send) and a separate, usually several-times-higher price for output tokens (everything the model writes back). Rate limits are set in tokens per minute. And remember, from operating agents, that each turn of a conversation re-sends the whole transcript so far — so a long-running session's token meter climbs faster and faster as it goes. Chapter 41 does the full economics, including prompt caching, which exists precisely because re-sending the same prefix thousands of times was too expensive to ignore.

Walls. Every model has a maximum number of tokens it can consider at once (the context window — patience, one section to go) and a maximum number it will write in a single reply, often a settable max_tokens parameter. When output stops mid-sentence, you usually hit the second wall, not a bug.

One consequence surprises almost everyone the first time: the same content costs wildly different amounts depending on how it is written. Tokenizer vocabularies are trained mostly on English and code, so common English is dense — and everything else pays a surcharge:

Careful

"Token" is not "word", and estimating cost by word count undercounts badly for JSON payloads, logs, code, and non-Latin scripts. When the number matters, count properly: every provider ships a token-counting endpoint or library (OpenAI's is tiktoken; Anthropic has a count-tokens API), and every tokenizer playground on the web will show you the exact chunks.

Embeddings: the pigeonhole wall

Tokens answer "what does text cost?" Embeddings answer a deeper question: "what does text mean — and what else means nearly the same thing?"

An embedding is a list of numbers — a vector, in maths speak — that places a piece of text at a point in a space of meaning, arranged so that texts with similar meaning land near each other. Two numbers (latitude, longitude) are enough to place you anywhere on Earth. Meaning is bigger than Earth, so embedding models use hundreds or thousands of numbers — 1,536 is a common size — but the idea is exactly a map: every text gets coordinates, and nearby means similar.

Nobody sat down and decided that axis 407 means "royalty". The coordinates are learned, using the training machinery you met in what is a model: texts that show up in similar contexts get nudged closer together, millions of times, until the geometry itself encodes meaning. The famous party trick — the vector from "king" to "queen" points the same way as the vector from "man" to "woman" — is not programmed in. It falls out of the geometry.

Once meaning is a coordinate, similarity becomes measurable. The standard measure is cosine similarity — in plain words, the angle between two arrows: pointing the same way scores near 1, unrelated directions score near 0. One distinction keeps people out of trouble. Embeddings show up in two different jobs. Inside an LLM, step one of the transformer you met in the previous chapter is converting each token into exactly this kind of vector before attention does its work — internal, invisible, not something you touch. Outside, standalone embedding models (with names like text-embedding-3-small) do nothing but convert whole sentences or documents into one vector each, so your systems can file and search them — the pigeonhole wall as a rentable service, priced per token like everything else.

The context window: the size of the desk

The context window is the maximum number of tokens a model can consider in one request — its working memory, the size of the clerk's desk. Everything must fit on it at once: the system prompt, the tool definitions, the whole conversation so far, any documents you paste in, and the reply the model is writing. One desk, shared by all of it.

Sizes have exploded — from a few thousand tokens in the early days to 128,000 or 200,000 on today's frontier models, with some stretching past a million. Two hundred thousand tokens is roughly a 500-page book spread out on the desk at once, which sounds infinite until you meet a real agent session: a system prompt, forty tool definitions, three hours of back-and-forth, and a handful of pasted log files will eat it comfortably.

Because here is the rule that explains more production behaviour than any other in this chapter: what is not on the desk does not exist. A model has no hard drive and no memory between requests. If a fact fell off the desk, the model is not being forgetful or lazy — the fact is simply, physically absent from its input. Watch a session fill up:

Even within the window, position matters. Three dials, one machine

Here is how the three ideas snap together. Tokens are the units — the currency in which everything else is denominated. The context window is a budget written in tokens: a hard cap on how much the model can see at once. And embeddings are the escape hatch from that cap: instead of piling every document you own onto the desk, you file them all on the pigeonhole wall — embed once, store the coordinates — and fetch only the few most relevant pages for the question at hand. That fetch-just-what-you-need pattern is RAG, two acts from now, and the cost arithmetic that makes it necessary is chapter 41. Both chapters lean on today's material with their full weight.

In the wild

You will meet all three constantly. Pricing pages quote "$ per 1M input tokens / $ per 1M output tokens". Configs and merge requests carry max_tokens, context_window, and chunk-size settings; failures show up as context_length_exceeded in error logs. Token counters live in every serious tool — a library like tiktoken, a provider count-tokens endpoint, the running total in your coding agent's status line. Embeddings appear as model names (text-embedding-3-small and kin) in RAG pipelines, and as database columns — a Postgres table with a vector(1536) column, courtesy of the pgvector extension, is a pigeonhole wall in production. And when an agent harness prints "compacting conversation history", you now know exactly which desk is being swept, and what is quietly falling into the drawer.

Check yourself

1. "the" is one token, but "Tiruchirappalli" comes out as a dozen. Why?

BPE merges whatever chunk pairs occur most often in the training text. Frequency, not grammar or word length, decides what gets a single stamp — so common words are cheap and rare ones are billed piece by piece.

2. A team translates its prompts from English to Hindi and the API bill jumps, even though the text says the same thing. The most likely cause?

Billing is per token, and tokenizers compress best what they saw most during vocabulary training — overwhelmingly English and code. The same meaning in a less-represented script simply costs more tokens.

3. Two support tickets have embeddings that sit very close together. What do you actually know?

Embeddings are coordinates of meaning: nearby means semantically similar. "Refund not received" and "my money hasn't come back" land close together with zero shared keywords — which is precisely why semantic search beats keyword search.

4. An agent halfway through a long session suddenly "forgets" the contents of a file it read an hour ago. The most likely explanation?

The model has no memory between requests — only what is re-sent on the desk. When a transcript outgrows the window, harnesses truncate or compact, and whatever was dropped no longer exists for the model. Check the desk before blaming the brain.

5. What counts against the context window in a single request?

It is one shared desk. The system prompt and tool catalogue claim space before the user types a word, and the model's own output needs room at the end — which is why a "nearly full" context can fail even though your message was short.

Key takeaways

  • A token is the model's unit of reading, writing, and billing — roughly three-quarters of an English word, decided by frequency (BPE), so common text is cheap and rare, numeric, or non-English text costs more.
  • Token counts drive both money (priced per million, input and output separately, output pricier) and walls (context limits, max_tokens caps, rate limits) — never estimate by word count when the number matters.
  • An embedding is a list of numbers placing text on a map of meaning; nearby means similar, cosine similarity measures the angle, and that one trick powers search by meaning — the seed of RAG.
  • The context window is the model's desk: system prompt, tools, history, documents, and the reply all share it, and whatever is not on the desk does not exist for the model.
  • When an agent "forgets", suspect the desk before the brain: eviction and compaction explain most production amnesia, and compaction trades detail for space by design.
  • None of this is magic — in this one page you tokenized text, embedded meaning, and ran a nearest-neighbour search with nothing but plain Python.
