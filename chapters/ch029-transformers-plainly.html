Transformers, in plain terms

The architecture behind every LLM you operate, explained with a meeting room, a stack of knobs, and one very repetitive guessing game — plus the honest truth about "it just predicts the next word".

~35 minChapter 29 of 117
The story

Seven people sit around a meeting-room table. Each holds a card with one word on it: The — server — crashed — because — it — was — overloaded. The facilitator explains the rules: "Nobody leaves until every one of you can say, precisely, what your own word means in this sentence. You may look at everyone else's card. You decide for yourself whose cards matter to yours."

The person holding it has the hardest job. Alone, the card is meaningless. So she scans the table: server? Very relevant. crashed? Somewhat. because? Barely. She quietly weighs each colleague's card, blends what she learns, and writes on her own card: "it — almost certainly the server." Around the table, everyone is doing the same thing at the same time — even The is glancing at server to know what it introduces. The facilitator calls time, then grins: "Lovely. Now do it again, using your improved notes." They run the round again. And again. Twelve rounds later, every card carries a rich, situated meaning, and the room as a whole understands the sentence.

That meeting is a transformer. The glancing-and-weighing is attention. Each repeated round is a layer. And the reason the room exists at all is a guessing game we will get to shortly.

Where the last chapter left us

In why text broke classical ML, you watched the old toolkit fail at language: bag-of-words models threw away word order, and models that read strictly left-to-right kept forgetting the start of the sentence by the time they reached the end. The core unsolved problem was context — the meaning of a word depends on other words that may sit far away, in either direction. And from what a model actually is, remember the working definition: a model is just a function full of numbers that were tuned on data rather than typed in by a programmer.

This chapter is where those threads meet. In 2017, a research paper with the cheeky title "Attention Is All You Need" proposed an architecture that solved the context problem head-on. Every LLM you have used since — GPT, Claude, the open-weight models your colleagues download — is its descendant.

Attention: every word looks at every word

Attention is a mechanism that lets each word in a piece of text look at every other word, score how relevant each one is to itself, and blend in information from the relevant ones — exactly like the people around that meeting table. No word is processed alone, and no word has to wait its turn in a left-to-right queue: the whole table works simultaneously.

That simultaneity matters twice. It fixes the forgetting problem — word 900 can look directly at word 3, no whispering chain in between. And it is why transformers took over so fast: all those glances happen as a few giant batches of arithmetic, which is exactly what GPUs — graphics cards repurposed as maths engines — do brilliantly. The old read-one-word-at-a-time models could never use the hardware this well.

Note

If you ever read a technical write-up, you will meet the words queries, keys, and values — the machinery behind steps 2 to 4. A query is the question a word asks; a key is what each word offers for matching; a value is the information handed over once a match scores well. You do not need the matrix algebra to reason correctly about LLMs — the meeting room is a faithful mental model.

Layers: running the meeting again and again

A layer is one complete round of that meeting — every word attends to every word, blends, and updates — followed by a small private "digest what you just heard" step for each word. A transformer is simply many of these layers stacked, so the output of round one becomes the input of round two.

Stacking is where depth comes from. Early layers sort out local, mechanical things: adjacency, basic grammar, which noun a pronoun leans towards. Middle layers assemble relationships: who did what to whom. Deep layers deal in abstractions: sentiment, the fact being referenced, sarcasm, genre. None of this division of labour was designed — it emerges from training, and researchers map it after the fact the way biologists map a brain. The models you use daily stack dozens to around a hundred layers.

Parameters: billions of learned knobs

Everything in that meeting that involved judgement — how "it" phrases its question, how strongly "server" answers, how the blend is weighted, what each word's private digest step does — is controlled by numbers. These numbers are the model's parameters: knobs whose settings were learned during training rather than chosen by a human. Recall the definition from what is a model — a function plus learned numbers. A transformer is exactly that, just with an extravagant number of numbers.

When your colleagues say "the 7B" or "a 70-billion-parameter model", this is what they are counting: total knobs. More knobs generally means more capacity to store patterns and facts — and more memory and compute to run. Two things trip newcomers up. Parameters are frozen after training: while you chat, no knob moves, and the model learns nothing permanent from your conversation. And no single knob means anything readable — knowledge lives smeared across millions of them at once.

Pretraining: one game, played at planetary scale

So far we have an architecture — a meeting room capable of understanding text, with billions of unset knobs. How do the knobs get set? With a game so simple it sounds like a joke.

Pretraining is the phase where the model is shown a fragment of real text with the ending hidden, and asked to predict the next token — a word or word-piece, the model's unit of text (the next chapter is devoted to them). The model guesses, the guess is compared with what actually came next, and every knob is nudged a hair's breadth towards making the right answer more likely. Repeat trillions of times, across a huge slice of the public internet, books, and code, on warehouses of GPUs, for months.

Here is the deep trick: the game is trivial to state but brutally hard to play well. To predict the next token of "The capital of France is", you must know a fact. To continue "She placed the flask on the burner and the liquid began to", you need a whiff of physics. To finish a half-written Python function, you must model what the programmer intends. Nobody taught the model grammar, geography, or code — those abilities are what a system is forced to grow when the only way to keep winning the game is to genuinely model the world that produced the text.

A transformer plays the identical game — probability list over next tokens, sample, append, repeat — but its attention layers let every guess consult the entire preceding text. That single upgrade is the difference between this babble and Claude.

Two details from the toy carry over exactly. A model's output is never "the next word" — it is a probability score for every token it knows, and the running system samples from that list (the temperature knob in every API you call controls how adventurously). And generation is a loop: predict one token, append it, feed the whole thing back, predict again. Every fluent paragraph an LLM has ever produced was assembled one token at a time by that loop.

From autocomplete to assistant: instruction tuning and RLHF

Pretraining alone produces a base model — a pure continuation engine that finishes text the way the internet statistically would. Ask a base model "What is the capital of France?" and it may well reply "What is the capital of Germany? What is the capital of Spain?" — because on the internet, that question most often appears in lists of quiz questions. It is not being obtuse; it is doing exactly what it was trained to do. Nothing in pretraining ever said "you are an assistant and this is a question addressed to you".

Two further phases close that gap, and both are refreshingly plain once de-jargoned.

Instruction tuning is a second, much smaller round of the same next-token training — but on a curated dataset of instruction-and-good-response pairs written or vetted by humans: here is a question and a helpful answer; here is a request to summarise and a good summary. The knobs shift towards a new prior: text shaped like a request should be continued with a fulfilment, not with more requests. Finishing school for an extremely well-read student who has never held a job.

RLHF — reinforcement learning from human feedback — polishes further. The model generates several candidate answers to the same prompt; human reviewers rank them; a separate small model learns to predict those rankings; and the main model is then nudged towards answers that stand-in for human taste scores highly. In plain words: the model practises, a learned copy of human preference marks its homework, and the knobs drift towards answers people actually prefer — more helpful, less rambling, more willing to say "I do not know", more likely to refuse harmful requests. When a model politely declines something dangerous, that behaviour came from this phase, not from pretraining.

"It just predicts the next word" — true, and misleading

You will hear this line for the rest of your career, as both a dismissal ("glorified autocomplete") and an explanation. You are now equipped to see how it is both correct and deeply incomplete.

How it is true. Mechanically, yes — everything you watched above is the whole machine. One token at a time, a probability list, a sample, a loop. There is no separate "reasoning module", no database lookup, no inner fact-checker before the words come out. When an LLM confidently invents a court case or an API method that does not exist, this is why: the machinery must emit a next token, and a fluent wrong token can score higher than an awkward true one. Hallucination is not a bug bolted onto the architecture; it is the architecture, operating without a truth check.

How it is misleading. The word "just" is doing fraudulent work. "A grandmaster just moves one chess piece at a time" is also mechanically true — and says nothing about the depth of what selects each move. To predict the next token well across law, code, poetry, and your production incident report, the model was forced to build internal machinery that tracks grammar, referents, facts, and the author's intent — you watched a miniature of it resolve "it" to "server" in the stepper. The prediction is simple; what is computed in order to predict is not. The model you operate is also not the raw predictor: instruction tuning and RLHF have reshaped it. And because each predicted token is fed back in, the loop can lay down a plan in its own output and then follow it — which is what you exploit every time you ask an agent to "think step by step".

So when a colleague says "it just predicts the next word", the honest reply is: "True — and to predict it well, it had to learn a compressed model of most things humans write about. The 'just' is where the interesting part hides." It fails in ways no human would (inventing citations with total confidence) and succeeds in ways no earlier software could (reading your half-broken YAML and inferring what you meant). Hold both.

In the wild

This vocabulary is everywhere once you can hear it. A Hugging Face model card that says "decoder-only transformer, 32 layers, 8B parameters" is telling you: this meeting room stacks 32 rounds and has 8 billion knobs. Model names carry the training story — Llama-3.1-8B is a base model, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct has been through instruction tuning and preference training; agent harnesses always want the instruct variant. The temperature parameter in every LLM API call you have ever configured controls how adventurously the loop samples from the probability list. And when a data scientist says "we can't fine-tune the frontier model, we only have API access", they mean: the knobs are frozen and behind someone else's wall — which is why your team steers behaviour with prompts and tools instead.

Check yourself

1. In an attention pass, what does each word actually do?

Every word looks at every word, in parallel — the meeting room. That is what fixed the forgetting problem of left-to-right models and what made transformers a perfect fit for GPUs.

2. A colleague mentions "the 70B model". What are they counting?

Parameter count = number of learned knobs. Layers are counted in the dozens, training tokens in the trillions — 70 billion is the knobs. And they stay frozen while you chat.

3. What single task does pretraining optimise, across its entire mountain of text?

Only next-token prediction. Helpfulness and safety come later, from instruction tuning and RLHF — which is why a raw base model answers a question with more questions.

4. Why does a base model reply to "What is the capital of France?" with more quiz questions instead of "Paris"?

The fact is in there (it helps win the guessing game), but a base model is a continuation engine. Instruction tuning is what installs "a request should be continued with a fulfilment".

5. "It just predicts the next word" — what is the strongest honest correction to the "just"?

The mechanism really is one token at a time with no database and no inner fact-checker — that part is true, and it is why hallucination exists. The misleading part is "just": playing the game well required learning a compressed model of what humans write about.

Key takeaways

  • A transformer is a meeting room: attention lets every word score and blend information from every other word, all in parallel — which fixed context and suited GPUs perfectly.
  • Layers are repeated rounds of that meeting; early rounds capture grammar, deeper rounds capture relationships, facts, and tone. Modern models stack dozens to around a hundred.
  • Parameters are the billions of learned knobs controlling every score and blend — set during training, frozen while you chat, meaningless individually.
  • Pretraining is next-token prediction at planetary scale: a trivial game that can only be won by internally modelling the world that produced the text.
  • Pretraining yields a continuation engine, not an assistant; instruction tuning teaches it to fulfil requests, and RLHF steers it towards answers humans prefer.
  • "It just predicts the next word" is mechanically true — hallucination included — and strategically misleading: the depth lives in what must be computed to predict well.
