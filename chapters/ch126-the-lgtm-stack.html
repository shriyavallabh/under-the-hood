The LGTM stack: where telemetry lives

OpenTelemetry emits the signals; now they need a home and a window. Meet the four stores behind one dashboard - and learn to read a real conversation trace, span by span.

~30 minChapter 126 of 133
The story

Think of a busy hospital. Different records go to different departments: the pharmacy keeps the medication log, radiology keeps the scans, the billing office keeps the invoices. Each is a specialist archive, shaped for the thing it stores. But when a doctor sits down to understand one patient, she does not walk to three buildings - she opens one screen that pulls from all of them, and the patient's whole story appears in one place, cross-linked, so a line in the medication log jumps her straight to the scan that prompted it.

Your telemetry works the same way. Last chapter, OpenTelemetry became the tap that emits logs, metrics, and traces. But a tap with nothing under it just floods the floor. Each signal needs a store shaped for it, and you need one screen - one dashboard - that reads from all of them and lets you jump between them. The most common open-source answer to "where does it all live, and how do I look?" is a bundle nicknamed by its four initials: LGTM.

Four stores, one window

LGTM is a stack of open-source tools from Grafana Labs, and the name is just the first letters of its parts. Each specialises in one signal, and the last one is the window you look through:

The shape to remember: Loki holds logs, Tempo holds traces, Mimir holds metrics, and Grafana is the single screen that reads all three. Your OpenTelemetry pipeline from last chapter feeds them; Grafana lets you look.

The superpower: correlation

Storing three signals separately is useful. Linking them is the leap. Because every span and every log line can carry the same trace id, Grafana can offer a "trace to logs" jump: you find a slow span in Tempo, click once, and land on exactly the Loki logs that span produced. No more copying a timestamp and grepping a wall of text - the tools already know which log lines belong to which span. That single click is why teams put the effort into stitching a trace id onto their logs in the first place.

Reading a conversation trace

Here is what it actually looks like to debug an agent turn on this stack. No screenshots - just the sequence of clicks, drawn as boxes. Follow the highlight: you start at a list of recent conversations and drill down until you are staring at the exact request and response the model saw.

1. A list of recent conversation traces

conversation 8f3a1.84 s · 3 spans
conversation 7c110.62 s · 2 spans
conversation 4d905.10 s · 9 spans

Grafana queries Tempo and lists recent traces, each one a whole conversation turn. You spot the slow one and click in. (The 5.1-second outlier would be the one to open on a bad day.)

2. The turn opens as a waterfall of spans

user_turn=================== 1840 ms
llm.plan===== 520 ms
tool.search========= 900 ms
llm.answer==== 400 ms

Each span is a bar; its width is its duration. The eye goes straight to the widest child - the search tool. This is the tree you built by hand in the opening chapter, now drawn for you.

3. Click the span to read its attributes

tool.searchattributes
→
status: ok
query: "q3 revenue"
results: 12

Every attribute you recorded is here: for a model span you would see the model name, input and output token counts, and latency; for this tool span, the query and result count. Facts, not guesses.

4. One click jumps to the linked logs

tool.search
→
Loki logssame trace id

The "trace to logs" link carries the trace id across to Loki and shows only the log lines this span emitted - including the exact endpoint it called and any warning it logged. Trace said WHERE; the log now says WHAT, verbatim.

Grafana adds a few more views on top: a node/graph picture of the agent's steps, a live-tail of logs streaming from Loki, and metric panels (request rate, memory, total requests) drawn from Mimir. And crucially, a dashboard is just a saved set of queries stored as JSON - which means you can clone someone else's and adapt it, or write your own by pointing panels at your service.

Who hosts all this?

You almost never run LGTM yourself for one agent. In a large organisation, a central platform team runs one shared LGTM instance for everyone; your app simply points its OpenTelemetry exporter at that instance's address, and you are handed a starter dashboard to clone. The alternatives are a managed service (Grafana Cloud) or, for a small project, self-hosting the stack. Either way, the split is the same as last chapter: your code emits standard OTLP; where it lands and how you view it is someone else's infrastructure, reachable by a config line.

In the wild

You will hear "add a Tempo data source," "clone the agent dashboard," or "the p95 panel is red" in any team running this stack. A TraceQL query like { .service.name = "agent" && duration > 1s } in a review means "show me every agent turn slower than a second." When a platform team at a large bank says "send your telemetry to the central Grafana," they are pointing you at exactly this: their hosted LGTM, one dashboard to clone, and a "trace to logs" button that turns a 4pm Thursday argument into a thirty-second investigation.

Check yourself

1. In the LGTM stack, which component actually stores your traces?

Tempo is the traces store, Loki holds logs, Mimir holds metrics. Grafana stores nothing - it is the window that queries all three.

2. What does Grafana itself do?

Grafana is the single screen. It holds no data of its own; each panel is a query against a data source (Loki, Tempo, or Mimir).

3. The "trace to logs" jump works because of what shared piece of data?

Correlation is the whole superpower, and it rides on the trace id: because a span and its logs carry the same id, one click can carry you from the slow span straight to the exact log lines it produced.

4. You are one agent team at a large organisation. How do you usually get an LGTM dashboard?

Central hosting is the norm: your app emits standard OTLP to the shared instance, and you clone a dashboard. Same as the OTel split - you emit, the platform stores and shows.

Key takeaways

  • LGTM is a Grafana Labs stack named by its parts: Loki (logs), Grafana (visualisation), Tempo (traces), Mimir (metrics). Your OpenTelemetry pipeline feeds them.
  • Grafana stores nothing; it queries the other three as data sources and draws the panels. Loki, Tempo, and Mimir are the specialist stores.
  • The superpower is correlation: because spans and logs share a trace id, one click jumps you from a slow span in Tempo to the exact logs in Loki.
  • Reading an agent trace is a drill-down: trace list to span waterfall to span attributes to linked logs - the same tree from the opening chapter, drawn for you.
  • A dashboard is just a saved set of queries (JSON) you can clone and adapt, or write yourself with TraceQL, LogQL, and PromQL.
  • A central platform team usually hosts one shared LGTM; you emit standard OTLP to it and clone a starter dashboard rather than running the stack yourself.
