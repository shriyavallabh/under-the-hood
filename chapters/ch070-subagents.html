Subagents: divide and conquer

Spawn a worker with a spotless, empty context, let it wade through the mess on your behalf, and take back only the one paragraph that matters. Context isolation is the deep agent's sharpest architectural trick — and this chapter shows you exactly how the task tool pulls it off.

~25 minChapter 71 of 117
The story

The senior advocate's desk is famously bare: one legal pad, one pen, tomorrow's hearing brief. Down the corridor, the firm's archive room holds four thousand pages on the case — contracts, amendments, three years of increasingly cross letters. She does not go there. Instead she calls in a junior associate: "Find me every precedent where a vendor missed a delivery deadline and the penalty clause still held up on appeal. One page, on my desk by six."

The junior disappears into the archive. He pulls forty boxes, reads three hundred pages, chases five leads, abandons four of them. His desk becomes a landslide. At six o'clock he knocks, hands over a single page — three precedents, each with a citation — and goes home. His landslide stays in the archive room.

Tomorrow, in court, the advocate carries the legal pad and the one page. She never saw the boxes. She never needed to. That move — send someone else's desk to absorb the messy reading, and take back only the page that matters — is exactly what a deep agent does when it spawns a subagent.

The mess that files could not fix

Quick recap, because this chapter stands on the last one. In the virtual file system chapter you learned context offload: when a tool returns something huge, the deep agent writes it to a file and keeps only a one-line stub in its history, because the history is re-sent — and re-billed — on every turn of the loop you built in chapter 45. Files solve the problem of big results.

But files do not solve the problem of big journeys. Think about what research actually looks like in an agent transcript: a search that returns nothing useful, a file opened and found irrelevant, a promising lead that dies three tool calls later, a re-search with better keywords. Every one of those steps — the tool call, the disappointing observation, the model's "hmm, let me try again" — lands in the history and stays there. You can file the cargo, but the travel diary of finding it still fills the lead agent's desk. Twenty turns of rummaging is twenty turns of clutter, carried and paid for on every turn that follows, quietly distracting the model from the actual task.

The fix is the advocate's fix: do not make the journey yourself. Delegate it to a worker whose desk you never have to look at. A subagent is a complete, separate agent — its own loop, its own system prompt, its own tools, its own history — that the main agent can spin up for one bounded task and dismiss when the task is done.

The task tool: one call, one worker, one report

Mechanically, delegation arrives as one more tool on the menu. In the create_deep_agent anatomy chapter you saw that the subagent layer installs a tool named task alongside the planning and file tools. When the main agent — call it the lead from here on — decides some work is delegable, it calls task() the same way it would call a calculator: it writes a tool call, and the harness does the rest.

What the harness does is the interesting part. Step through it:

Context quarantine: the actual point

It is tempting to describe subagents as "parallelism" or "specialisation", and they can be both. But the reason they exist in every deep agent architecture is simpler and stricter: context quarantine — keeping one agent's mess from ever entering another agent's history. The quarantine runs in both directions, and each direction buys you something concrete.

Outbound: the worker starts clean. A fresh context is not just cheap — it is focused. The worker is not distracted by the lead's plan, the user's small talk, or the residue of earlier subtasks. Models follow instructions noticeably better in short, single-purpose contexts, so a worker with one job in one message often out-performs a lead juggling nine concerns.

Inbound: the lead stays clean. Whatever happens on the worker's desk — the forty search results, the poisoned lead, the tool error it recovered from — never reaches the lead's history. If a worker goes down a rabbit hole and fills its context with junk, the damage is contained: the lead gets back one disappointing report, not the rabbit hole itself. In the failure-modes language of chapter 48, a subagent turns context poisoning from an infection into a contained incident.

There is one rule you must respect for the trick to work: delegation is one-shot. The lead writes the description, the worker runs to completion, one report comes back. The worker cannot lean over mid-task and ask the lead a clarifying question, and it cannot see anything the lead did not put in the description. If the job needs context, the description must carry it. Under-described tasks are the number one way subagents go wrong — the worker returns a confident answer to the wrong question, like a junior who spent all day in the archive researching the wrong clause.

Note

Quarantine has a doorway: in deepagents the workers share the lead's virtual file system. A worker can write its full thirty-page findings to a file and return a two-line report saying "summary below; full detail in /research/vendor-disputes.md". The lead's history stays tiny, and the detail is on the shelf if anyone needs it. Messy histories are quarantined; useful artefacts are shared. This combination — subagent plus file — is the workhorse pattern of every long-running agent you will read.

Custom subagents: name, prompt, tools

Out of the box, deepagents gives every lead one employee: the general-purpose subagent. It carries the same tools as the lead and no special instructions — you hire it purely for the clean context, the way our advocate might hand a colleague of equal rank the boring half of the reading. For anything more deliberate, you define custom subagents: named specialists with their own prompt, their own tool belt, and optionally their own model. Each one is just a dictionary handed to create_deep_agent:

hiring a specialist — the subagents parameter
from deepagents import create_deep_agent

precedent_hunter = {
    "name": "precedent-hunter",
    "description": "Searches case-law archives and returns a short, "
                   "cited summary. Give it one legal question at a time.",
    "system_prompt": "You are a meticulous legal researcher. Read "
                     "everything relevant, then reply with at most "
                     "five findings, each with a citation.",
    "tools": [search_case_law],
    "model": "openai:gpt-5.5-mini",  # optional: cheaper model for grunt work
}

agent = create_deep_agent(
    model="openai:gpt-5.5",
    tools=[search_case_law, draft_brief],
    system_prompt="You are the lead advocate. Delegate heavy "
                  "reading to your subagents; you write the brief.",
    subagents=[precedent_hunter],
)

Read that dictionary the way a reviewer would — each key answers a different question:

the anatomy of a subagent definition
"name": "precedent-hunter",
The worker's unique identifier. This exact string is what the lead passes as subagent_type when it calls the task tool. Name one of your subagents "general-purpose" and you replace the built-in default.
"description": "Searches case-law archives and returns a short, "
The job advert — and here is the subtlety: this text is for the lead, not the worker. It is all the lead sees when choosing whom to hire, so it should say what the worker is good at and how to brief it ("give it one question at a time"). Vague descriptions produce a lead that never delegates, or delegates the wrong jobs.
"system_prompt": "You are a meticulous legal researcher. Read "
The worker's standing instructions — its whole identity. Crucially, it does not inherit the lead's system prompt: quarantine starts at birth. Tell the worker how to work and, above all, what shape its final report must take, because that report is the only thing anyone will ever see.
"tools": [search_case_law],
Optional. Omit it and the worker inherits the lead's tools; provide it and the worker gets exactly this list and nothing else. A narrow belt is both focus and safety — a research worker with no write-tools cannot break anything, no matter how confused it gets.
"model": "openai:gpt-5.5-mini",
Optional. Workers can run on a different — usually cheaper — model than the lead. Bulk reading rarely needs the expensive brain; judgement does. This one line is the biggest cost lever in the whole pattern.
subagents=[precedent_hunter],
The roster. Every entry becomes a hireable subagent_type, and the task tool's own manual — generated from these descriptions — is added to the lead's prompt so it knows who is on staff.

If this smells like the multi-agent patterns of Act VII, your nose is right — with one difference of temperament. A subgraph is wired: you draw the org chart in advance as nodes and edges. A subagent is hired: the lead decides at run time whether, when, and whom to delegate to, based on nothing but the descriptions on the roster. Wiring gives you control; hiring gives you flexibility. Deep agents choose hiring, and accept that the lead's judgement — like any manager's — will occasionally be wrong.

When the intern costs more than the work

Now the honest section. Delegation is not free, and reaching for subagents by reflex is one of the most common ways deep agents get slower and more expensive without getting better. Every task() call pays three tolls. First, a setup toll: the worker's system prompt and tool manuals are sent afresh, and everything relevant must be restated in the description. Second, a latency toll: an entire nested agent loop runs to completion inside one tool call while the lead — and your user — waits. Third, a fidelity toll: compression loses detail, and the lead cannot ask follow-ups; if the report missed the one nuance that mattered, the lead either proceeds wrong or pays for a second delegation.

So when do subagents add cost without benefit? Four patterns cover most of the wasted money:

  • The one-button errand. The job is a single tool call — fetch one record, run one query. Hiring a worker means paying a full system prompt and loop setup to press one button. Just press the button.
  • The answer is the bulk. "Rewrite this entire document" produces output as big as the input. The full result must come back to the lead anyway, so nothing gets quarantined — you paid the tolls and kept the landslide.
  • The conversation-shaped task. Work that needs mid-course steering — "draft, show me, adjust" — fights the one-shot contract. Each round trip is a fresh worker with fresh amnesia, and the description grows into a novel trying to compensate.
  • The context-heavy task. If the worker genuinely needs most of what the lead knows, you must paste it all into the description — at which point the "clean context" is neither clean nor cheaper. Work that is entangled with the lead's history should stay with the lead.

The rule of thumb that survives contact with production: delegate when the reading is much bigger than the answer. Ten thousand tokens of searching that distils to a hundred-token summary is the perfect subagent job. A hundred tokens of work that produces ten thousand tokens of answer is the perfect job to keep inline. And when the totals still surprise you — they will — the next chapter exists precisely to explain why multi-agent bills run roughly double what intuition predicts.

In the wild

You have almost certainly watched this pattern without naming it. When Claude Code prints a Task(...) line and goes quiet for a minute, that is a subagent — a fresh-context worker exploring the repository so the main session's context stays lean, returning a distilled report. Its custom agents are defined in small files with exactly the fields you met today: a name, a description for the caller, a prompt for the worker, and an allowed-tools list. In deepagents source you will meet the same machinery under the subagent middleware and its task tool. And in code review you will read the pattern in prose: "moved the doc-crawl into a subagent to keep the main context clean", or "this doesn't need a subagent, it's one API call" — sentences you can now argue about with numbers.

Check yourself

1. A lead agent calls the task tool and the worker runs for forty turns. What ends up in the lead's history?

The entire worker loop runs inside a single tool call, and only the final answer crosses back. The forty turns of rummaging are discarded with the worker — that disposal is the whole point of the pattern.

2. What does the worker see of the lead's world when it wakes up?

Quarantine runs both ways: the worker is born with a one-message history containing just the description. Anything the job needs must be written into that description, because the worker cannot ask follow-up questions.

3. In a custom subagent definition, who is the description field written for?

The description is all the lead reads when choosing a worker; the worker's own instructions live in system_prompt. Mixing the two up produces workers that never get hired, or get hired for the wrong jobs.

4. Which task is the WORST candidate for a subagent?

When the answer is as big as the work, nothing gets quarantined — the full result lands in the lead's history anyway, and you have paid the worker's setup, latency, and re-billed tokens for no context saving. Delegate when reading dwarfs the answer.

5. Delegating usually shrinks the lead's context but raises the total token bill. Why?

Every worker is a full agent with its own prompt, tool manuals, and turns; then its distilled findings re-enter the lead's history and are carried from there. Less context per agent, more tokens overall — the arithmetic the next chapter unpacks.

Key takeaways

  • Files offload big results; subagents offload big journeys. The searching, the dead ends, and the bulky observations happen on a worker's desk that the lead never sees.
  • The task tool is the whole interface: the lead sends a description and a subagent_type, a fresh worker runs a complete agent loop inside that one tool call, and only its final report returns.
  • Context quarantine cuts both ways — the worker starts focused with an empty history, and the worker's mess (even a poisoned context) can never infect the lead.
  • Delegation is one-shot: no mid-task questions, no shared history. The description must carry the entire briefing, and the worker's system_prompt must specify the report format, because the report is all that survives.
  • Custom subagents are dictionaries — name, description (for the lead), system_prompt (for the worker), optional tools and model — while the built-in general-purpose worker offers quarantine with no speciality.
  • Delegate when the reading is much bigger than the answer; skip subagents for one-call errands, bulk-output tasks, and work entangled with the lead's context — otherwise you pay setup, latency, and double-billed tokens for nothing.
