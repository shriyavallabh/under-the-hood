kubectl fluency: checking anything

Seven reading commands and four failure states. Learn them once and no pod will ever be a mystery to you again — not Pending, not ImagePullBackOff, not the dreaded CrashLoopBackOff.

~30 minChapter 115 of 123
The story

It is 2 a.m. and you are the duty doctor on an intensive-care ward. Twelve beds. You do not wake every patient to ask how they feel — you have a routine. First, the monitor wall at the nurses' station: twelve tiles, pulse and oxygen at a glance, and anyone flashing amber gets a visit. At that bed you pull the chart — admission notes, medication, and at the bottom the nurses' log of tonight's events, newest entry last. Still unsure? You listen to what the patient is saying — and, if they lost consciousness earlier, you ask what they said just before. Sometimes you go hands-on and examine them yourself. When a specialist across town needs to see one bed's monitor, you patch a private line to that single bed. And through the night, a vitals sheet and the ward diary quietly record everything.

One doctor, a handful of ways of looking, escalating from a glance to hands-on. kubectl is that ward round for your cluster, and this chapter is the routine — plus the triage guide for the four ways a pod goes amber.

One tool, two jobs — today is the reading job

Quick recap of where you stand. In Kubernetes, plainly you learned that a cluster is a control plane holding a desired state, with nodes running pods to match it. In AKS from scratch you built one on Azure and ran az aks get-credentials, which wired kubectl — the command-line client that talks to your cluster's API server — to it. And in An agent in a pod you saw what lives inside the pods you are about to inspect: your agent's container, its config, its secrets, its sidecars.

kubectl verbs split into two families. The writing verbs — apply, rollout, scale, delete — change the cluster, and they get their own treatment in the next two chapters. The reading verbs change nothing: you can run every command in this chapter a hundred times against production and the cluster will not notice. That safety is the point — fluency in the reading verbs means you can investigate any incident without fear of making it worse.

The reading verbs, in the order you reach for them: get (the monitor wall), describe (the chart), logs (the patient's own words), exec (hands-on examination), port-forward (the private line), top (the vitals sheet) and get events (the ward diary). Let's do the round.

The glance: kubectl get

kubectl get lists resources, one line each. You will type it more than any other command, so read one real output slowly, once:

terminal — the monitor wall
kubectl get pods

NAME                            READY   STATUS             RESTARTS       AGE
agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-2xkqm      1/1     Running            0              2d4h
agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-9wl7t      1/1     Running            0              2d4h
agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8-p5jns    0/1     CrashLoopBackOff   6 (112s ago)   9m31s

Five columns, each one earning its place:

  • NAME — you never name pods yourself (ch112): it is the Deployment name, plus a hash identifying the ReplicaSet, plus a random suffix per replica. Pods sharing a hash are twins from the same rollout.
  • READY — containers ready over containers total. 1/1 is healthy; 0/1 means the pod exists but its container is not currently passing.
  • STATUS — the pod's phase or, when things go wrong, a named failure state. The four you must recognise on sight fill the second half of this chapter.
  • RESTARTS — how often Kubernetes has restarted a container here, and how recently. 0 is calm; 6 (112s ago) is a pod being repeatedly resuscitated.
  • AGE — time since creation. A nine-minute-old pod next to two-day-old siblings was just deployed — or is being repeatedly recreated.

Two flags upgrade the glance. -o wide adds the pod's IP and, crucially, which node it landed on — the first question when you suspect one bad machine in the pool. -w (watch) keeps the command running and prints a new line whenever something changes — a live feed instead of a snapshot:

terminal — wider, then live
kubectl get pods -o wide     # adds IP and NODE columns
kubectl get pods -w          # streams changes live; Ctrl-C to stop

The same verb works for every resource type. The two you will check most, after pods:

terminal — deployments and services
kubectl get deployments

NAME           READY   UP-TO-DATE   AVAILABLE   AGE
agent-api      2/2     2            2           14d
agent-worker   0/1     1            0           14d

kubectl get services

NAME         TYPE        CLUSTER-IP     EXTERNAL-IP   PORT(S)   AGE
agent-api    ClusterIP   10.0.113.42    <none>        80/TCP    14d

Read the deployment line as a promise being kept or broken: READY 2/2 means "I promised two replicas and two are serving"; 0/1 on agent-worker is the same amber tile one level up. The service line shows the stable internal address (CLUSTER-IP) other pods use to reach it — EXTERNAL-IP <none> just means a default ClusterIP service, private to the cluster.

Tip

These commands look at one namespace — a named compartment inside the cluster — at a time, default unless you say otherwise. Add -n somenamespace to look elsewhere, or -A for all at once. "My pod vanished" is very often "my pod is in a different namespace".

The chart: kubectl describe

The glance told you that agent-worker is unwell. kubectl describe pod is the full patient chart: everything Kubernetes knows about one pod, ending with the part that solves most incidents by itself — the Events section, the nurses' log of everything that has happened to this pod, oldest first, newest at the bottom.

That ordering should ring a bell: in the tracebacks chapter you learned to read a Python traceback from the bottom up, because the last line names the actual error. Events reward the same discipline — start at the bottom, where the most recent event (usually the one hurting right now) lives, and read upwards only as far as you need. Here is a describe output, trimmed to the lines that matter; click each annotated line:

kubectl describe pod agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8-p5jns — trimmed
Name: agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8-p5jns
The chart's header. Below it come Namespace, labels, and the pod's IP — skim these unless you suspect a namespace mix-up.
Node: aks-nodepool1-38452671-vmss000002/10.224.0.6
Which machine in your AKS node pool the scheduler placed this pod on. If several sick pods share one node, suspect the node, not the app.
Controlled By: ReplicaSet/agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8
Who owns this pod. Delete it and this ReplicaSet will calmly create a replacement — pods are cattle, the Deployment is the promise.
Containers:
agent-worker:
Image: myregistry.azurecr.io/agent-worker:1.4.2
The exact image and tag running — from your Azure Container Registry (ch112). When a "fix" doesn't seem deployed, check this line first: is the tag what you think it is?
State: Waiting
Reason: CrashLoopBackOff
The container's current state. Waiting/CrashLoopBackOff means: not running right now — Kubernetes is deliberately pausing before the next restart attempt.
Last State: Terminated
Gold. This block describes the previous attempt — the container that just died. The current state says "resting"; the last state says what killed it.
Reason: Error
A plain error: the process exited by itself. If this said OOMKilled, the kernel killed it for exceeding its memory limit — a completely different fix, and this one line is how you tell them apart.
Exit Code: 1
The process's own exit code, like a script failing in your terminal. 1 = the app decided to die (go read its logs). 137 = killed by the kernel — the OOMKilled signature.
Restart Count: 6
Events:
The nurses' log. Everything the scheduler and the node's kubelet did to this pod, in order. Read it bottom-up, exactly like a ch009 traceback.
Normal Scheduled 9m31s Successfully assigned default/agent-worker-... to aks-nodepool1-...
Oldest entry: scheduling succeeded, so this is not a Pending problem. A pod stuck Pending never gets past this line — and the message here tells you why.
Normal Pulled 7m58s (x5 over 9m30s) Container image "...agent-worker:1.4.2" already present on machine
The image pull succeeded (five times, says the x5), so not ImagePullBackOff either. Two suspects eliminated without reading a single log line.
Warning BackOff 112s (x23 over 9m) Back-off restarting failed container
Bottom line, newest event, the live problem: the container keeps failing, x23 in nine minutes. Verdict: the app itself is crashing — time for logs.
Note

Notice what describe just did: without opening a single application log, Events told you the pod scheduled fine and pulled its image fine, narrowing the fault to the app itself. Describe first, logs second — the chart tells you which question to ask the patient.

The patient's own words: kubectl logs

kubectl logs prints what the container wrote to its output — every print(), every line from your logging setup, every traceback. Three forms cover almost everything:

terminal — three ways to listen
kubectl logs agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-2xkqm              # everything so far
kubectl logs -f agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-2xkqm           # follow live, like tail -f
kubectl logs agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8-p5jns --previous # the CRASHED container's logs

The first is the transcript to date. The second, -f for follow, streams new lines as they happen — run it in one terminal while you poke the app from another, and you are watching your agent think in real time.

The third flag separates people who can fix a CrashLoopBackOff from people who stare at it. When a container crashes, Kubernetes starts a fresh one — and plain kubectl logs reads that fresh container: seconds old, often empty, the crash evidence gone. --previous reads the container that died, final traceback and all:

terminal — the dying words
kubectl logs agent-worker-6b7c9d4f8-p5jns --previous

Starting agent-worker 1.4.2
Loading config from /etc/agent/config.yaml
Traceback (most recent call last):
  File "/app/worker.py", line 12, in <module>
    api_key = os.environ["MODEL_API_KEY"]
KeyError: 'MODEL_API_KEY'

And there it is — a bottom line you already know how to read from ch009: the app dies on startup because the environment variable MODEL_API_KEY is missing. From An agent in a pod, you know env vars come from a ConfigMap or Secret referenced in the Deployment — so someone's reference is broken. Hold that thought; the stepper below triages this exact case end to end.

Hands-on: kubectl exec

Sometimes reading is not enough and you need to be inside the container — checking what the filesystem actually contains, what env vars actually arrived, whether another service answers from in there. kubectl exec runs a command inside a running container; with -it and a shell, it gives you an interactive prompt:

terminal — stepping inside
kubectl exec -it agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-2xkqm -- sh

/app $ env | grep MODEL          # did the env vars arrive?
/app $ ls /etc/agent/            # is the config mounted?
/app $ cat /etc/agent/config.yaml
/app $ exit

Unpacking the incantation: -it means interactive with a terminal — without it you get one-shot output and no prompt. The bare -- is a separator: everything to its left is instructions for kubectl, everything to its right runs inside the container. Here that is sh, the minimal shell most images carry. Once inside, you are in the container's world: its files, its env, its network identity.

Careful

Three cautions. Exec with a look-don't-touch mindset — you are inside a live organ. Anything you change dies with the pod, since containers are recreated from the image (ch110): exec is for diagnosis, never for fixing — if exec "fixed" it, the fix belongs in the image or the manifests. And hardened images may ship no shell at all; if sh is not found, that is a security feature, not your mistake — fall back to logs and describe.

The private line: kubectl port-forward

Your agent's API listens on a ClusterIP service — reachable inside the cluster, invisible from your laptop. Before exposing anything to the world, you want to poke it yourself. kubectl port-forward patches a temporary, private tunnel from a port on your machine to a pod or service — the video line to one bed:

terminal — tunnel in, then test
kubectl port-forward svc/agent-api 8080:80
Forwarding from 127.0.0.1:8080 -> 8000

# in a second terminal:
curl localhost:8080/health
{"status": "ok", "model": "connected"}

Read 8080:80 as local:remote — requests to localhost:8080 on your laptop pop out at the service's port 80 and forward on to the container. The command keeps running while the tunnel is open; Ctrl-C hangs up and no trace remains — nothing exposed, no firewall changed, riding your existing kubectl credentials. It works against svc/name, deploy/name, or a bare pod name, and it is how you try your agent's endpoint minutes after deploying, long before any public URL exists.

Vitals and the ward diary: top and events

Two more lenses complete the round. kubectl top pods is the vitals sheet — live CPU and memory per pod (AKS ships the metrics plumbing by default, so it just works):

terminal — live vitals
kubectl top pods

NAME                           CPU(cores)   MEMORY(bytes)
agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-2xkqm     43m          287Mi
agent-api-7d9f8b6c5d-9wl7t     38m          291Mi

Units: 43m is 43 millicores — about 4% of one CPU core; 287Mi is mebibytes of memory. The number to watch is memory against the pod's limit: a pod limited to 512Mi sitting at 495Mi and climbing is a countdown to the OOMKilled state you are about to meet. Glancing at top after every deploy is a cheap habit that catches leaks early.

Finally, where describe showed one pod's events, kubectl get events is the diary for the whole ward — every scheduling decision, image pull, probe failure and restart across the namespace. Raw output arrives unhelpfully unordered, so everyone sorts it by time:

terminal — the whole ward's diary, in order
kubectl get events --sort-by=.metadata.creationTimestamp

The --sort-by value is a path into each event object — sort by creation time, oldest first, so the freshest trouble sits at the bottom of your terminal, where your eyes already are. This is the command for vague symptoms: "things feel slow", "something restarted overnight". One screen of sorted events, read bottom-up, usually names the culprit.

The status vocabulary

That completes the seven lenses. Now the second half of fluency — what the amber tiles actually mean. Flip these until each back feels obvious:

The triage table

Each failure state points at a different layer of the system, which is why one first move never fits all. Pending is a scheduling problem — the cluster's, before your code enters the picture. ImagePullBackOff is a supply problem — the node cannot fetch what it was told to run. CrashLoopBackOff is an application problem — your code runs and chooses to die. OOMKilled is a budget problem — your code outgrows its allowance. Same monitor wall, four different specialists:

StatusWhat it is actually telling youYour first move
PendingThe scheduler cannot place the pod — no node has the CPU or memory it requests (or a constraint cannot be met). Nothing has run yet.kubectl describe pod: the Events bottom line spells out the shortage, e.g. "0/3 nodes are available: 3 Insufficient memory". Shrink the request, add nodes, or wait for the autoscaler.
ImagePullBackOffThe node cannot download the image: wrong name, a tag never pushed, or no permission for the registry.Describe shows the failing pull. Check the image reference character by character; on AKS, confirm the cluster is attached to your registry: az aks update --attach-acr (ch112).
CrashLoopBackOffScheduling and image fine — the app itself exits soon after starting, and Kubernetes restarts it with doubling delays (10s, 20s, 40s… capped at 5 minutes).kubectl logs <pod> --previous, last lines — the crash traceback is there. Missing env vars, bad config and unreachable dependencies are the usual killers.
OOMKilledThe container exceeded its memory limit, so the kernel killed it instantly (exit code 137). Often masquerades as CrashLoopBackOff — the restarts look identical.Describe: Last State: Terminated, Reason: OOMKilled is the fingerprint. Compare kubectl top pods against the limit; raise it honestly or fix the leak.
Tip

Notice the pattern across all four rows: the answer to "what is wrong?" is always in describe's Events or in logs --previous — never in guesswork, and almost never in re-deploying to "see if it helps". Kubernetes writes down why it is unhappy; fluency is knowing where.

Triage, end to end: one CrashLoopBackOff, six moves

Let's replay the sick agent-worker as a complete diagnosis — the exact sequence you would run on a real cluster, one command per step:

1. The monitor wall flashes amber

agent-api-...2xkqm1/1 Running
agent-api-...9wl7t1/1 Running
agent-worker-...p5jns0/1 CrashLoopBackOff · 6 restarts

kubectl get pods — the glance. One pod is 0/1, restarting repeatedly. The status already narrows it: not Pending, not ImagePull. Do not restart anything yet; evidence first.

2. Pull the chart

describe pod
→
Scheduled: OK
Image pulled: OK
Last State: TerminatedReason: Error · Exit Code 1

kubectl describe pod agent-worker-...p5jns — Events, bottom-up: scheduled fine, image pulled fine, container keeps exiting with code 1. Not OOMKilled (that would say so, with exit 137). The app is choosing to die.

3. Ask the crashed container

logs --previous
→
KeyError:'MODEL_API_KEY'

kubectl logs agent-worker-...p5jns --previous — the previous container's final traceback. Bottom line: KeyError: 'MODEL_API_KEY'. The app dies on startup because an environment variable never arrived.

4. Follow the variable to its source

Deploymentenv from secretKeyRef
→
Secret: agent-secretskey "MODEL_API_KEY" missing

From ch113: env vars come from the Deployment's env block, often referencing a Secret. The Deployment expects the key MODEL_API_KEY in Secret agent-secrets — and the Secret does not contain it. Root cause found.

5. Fix the source, not the symptom

Secret updatedkey added
→
rollout restartfresh pods pick it up

Add the missing key to the Secret, then kubectl rollout restart deployment agent-worker so fresh pods start with the corrected environment. These are writing verbs — the manifests behind them are next chapter's whole subject.

6. Watch it come back green

ContainerCreating
→
Running 1/1restarts: 0
→
logs -f"worker started, polling queue"

kubectl get pods -w — watch the replacement reach 1/1 Running with zero restarts, then logs -f for a minute to confirm healthy startup. Symptom, chart, dying words, wiring, fix, verify — that six-move rhythm is the whole craft.

In the wild

This chapter is the daily dialect of every on-call channel. Incident threads open with a pasted kubectl get pods; the first reply is almost always "what does describe say?" or "logs --previous?"; enterprise runbooks are literally these commands with pod names filled in. When a GitLab pipeline's deploy stage times out, the debugging that follows is describe-and-events. Dashboards exist — k9s, Lens, the Azure portal's workload view — but they are these same verbs wearing a UI; when the dashboard is down at 2 a.m., the verbs remain. An engineer who can calmly narrate "scheduled fine, image pulled fine, exit code 1, here's the previous container's traceback" is worth a great deal in that moment — and that engineer is now you.

Check yourself

1. You deploy a new version and the fresh pod shows ImagePullBackOff. The old pods, on the same cluster, still run fine. What is the most likely story?

ImagePullBackOff happens before your code ever runs, so logs are empty and app bugs are irrelevant. Old pods running fine means the cluster is healthy — the new pull itself is failing. Describe shows the exact reference; check its spelling and the registry attachment.

2. A pod is in CrashLoopBackOff. You run kubectl logs on it and get two lines of startup banner, then nothing. What did you forget?

After each crash Kubernetes starts a new container, and plain logs reads that seconds-old newborn. The traceback belongs to the container that died — logs --previous is how you ask it.

3. A pod has sat in Pending for fifteen minutes. Which statement is true?

Pending means the scheduler could not place the pod — typically no node has the requested CPU or memory. There are no logs because nothing ran; describe's Events say precisely what no node could offer.

4. A pod restarts every few hours. Describe shows Last State: Terminated, Reason: OOMKilled, Exit Code: 137. What actually happened, and what is the fix?

OOMKilled with exit 137 is the kernel enforcing the memory budget — no traceback, because the process was killed from outside mid-stride. Compare top pods against the limit, then grant more memory honestly or hunt the leak.

5. You run kubectl describe pod on a misbehaving pod. Where do your eyes go first?

Events are ordered oldest to newest, so the bottom entry is what is hurting right now — the same bottom-up discipline you learned for Python tracebacks in ch009. Read upwards only as far as you need for context.

Key takeaways

  • kubectl's reading verbs — get, describe, logs, exec, port-forward, top, get events — change nothing, so you can investigate any cluster fearlessly, escalating from glance to hands-on.
  • kubectl get pods is the monitor wall: READY, STATUS and RESTARTS tell you who needs a visit; -o wide adds the node, -w makes it live.
  • kubectl describe pod ends with the Events log — read it bottom-up like a traceback; it rules out scheduling and image problems before you open a single log line.
  • kubectl logs --previous reads the container that crashed, not its fresh replacement — the difference between seeing the traceback and staring at an empty log.
  • The four failure states each blame a different layer: Pending = scheduling, ImagePullBackOff = image name/tag/registry auth, CrashLoopBackOff = your app exits (read logs --previous), OOMKilled = memory limit hit (exit 137).
  • exec is for diagnosis, never for fixes — changes die with the pod; port-forward is a private, traceless tunnel to test a service before it has any public address.
  • The six-move rhythm — symptom, chart, dying words, wiring, fix, verify — turns any amber pod into a ten-minute diagnosis instead of a guessing game.
