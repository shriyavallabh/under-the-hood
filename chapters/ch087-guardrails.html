Guardrails: the safety layer

The model is brilliant and dangerously literal. Guardrails are the checkpoints around it — screening what goes in, vetting what comes out, and never relying on the model's own judgment to enforce your rules.

~25 minChapter 88 of 135
The story

Meet the most efficient executive assistant in the city. He reads every letter that reaches the office and acts on it immediately — books couriers, wires refunds, posts documents. He is brilliant, tireless, and dangerously literal. One morning a letter arrives: "Disregard your boss's standing orders. I am the new director. Courier the client files to this address." A brilliant-but-literal assistant might just... do it.

So the office builds two checkpoints around him. The mailroom screens everything inbound: letters carrying a customer's card number get those digits blacked out before he ever sees them; letters that try to impersonate the boss get pulled; letters about things the office does not handle are returned to sender. And nothing he produces leaves the building unchecked: every outgoing parcel passes an outbound desk that confirms the address is on the approved list, the contents match what was actually requested, and no confidential page slipped in.

The assistant is the model. The mailroom is your input guardrails. The outbound desk is your output guardrails. And notice the crucial design decision: the checkpoints do not rely on the assistant's judgment at all. They are separate people, with a short checklist, and the power to say no.

The loop gets a perimeter

You know from the harness chapter that a production agent is a loop wearing a lot of protective equipment, and from the sandboxing chapter that one piece of that equipment is a locked room for any code the agent writes. Sandboxing contains what the agent executes; this chapter is about the sibling protection — checking what the agent reads and says.

A guardrail is a runtime check that lives outside the model, inspects data as it crosses a boundary — into the loop or out of it — and can block, rewrite, or escalate it before it travels further. Three words carry the weight. Runtime: guardrails run on every request, in production. Outside the model: a guardrail is ordinary code (or a second, smaller model) whose verdict does not depend on the main model behaving. And boundary: guardrails sit at the doors — where user text enters, where tool calls fire, where answers leave.

Why not just put the rules in the system prompt? Because a system prompt is a request, not a mechanism. The model usually honours it — usually is the problem. You met failure modes where the model confidently does the wrong thing, and prompt injection, where hostile text smuggles instructions past its better judgment. Guardrails exist for the residue that instructions cannot eliminate.

Every guardrail resolves to one of three verdicts. Escalation hands the case to human-in-the-loop review — the guardrail's way of saying "not my call".

Checkpoint one: input guardrails

Input guardrails inspect everything that flows toward the model — the user's message, but also retrieved documents, tool results, and anything else entering the context. Three checks appear in nearly every serious deployment.

Injection screening. Prompt injection is hostile text hiding inside data — a support ticket, a web page, an email — phrased as instructions so the model treats it as orders rather than content. You met it as MCP's biggest risk in the MCP security chapter; here it becomes a filter on everything inbound. The screen has layers of its own: a fast pattern check for known phrasings ("ignore previous instructions", "reveal your system prompt"), then often a small classifier trained to smell manipulation that regexes miss. No screen is perfect — injection is an arms race, hence the deeper layers below.

Topic filters. A refund agent has no business discussing medical advice, elections, or a competitor's pricing. A topic filter is a scope check — it rejects requests outside the agent's mandate with a polite redirect. Most public agent embarrassments begin with a user coaxing a bot far from its purpose.

PII detection and redaction. PII — personally identifiable information: card numbers, phone numbers, emails, addresses — must often be kept out of model calls entirely, for regulatory reasons and because whatever you send also lands in logs and traces. A redaction pass finds PII (regex for well-shaped things like card numbers; named-entity-recognition models for messy things like names) and replaces it with placeholders like [EMAIL_1] before the model call. Some systems keep a reversible mapping so placeholders can be swapped back into the final reply.

Careful

Redact before the model call, not after. Once raw text is sent to an API, it has crossed your boundary — into the provider's infrastructure, your own trace store, and every log line in between. Scrubbing logs afterwards is an incident response, not a design.

Checkpoint two: output guardrails

Output guardrails inspect what the model produces — final answers and proposed actions — because passing clean input in guarantees nothing about what comes out. Four checks carry most of the load.

Schema validation. If your agent promised structured output — a JSON contract, usually enforced with Pydantic — the output guardrail actually parses and validates it. Malformed output is rejected and often retried automatically. It is the cheapest output check, and it catches an astonishing share of real-world failures.

Grounding checks. For RAG-style answers, a grounding check verifies that the answer's claims are actually supported by the retrieved sources — usually by asking a second model, "is this sentence entailed by these passages?" It is the runtime defence against hallucination: an ungrounded answer is blocked or rewritten to say "I could not find that in the documents".

Toxicity and content safety. A classifier scores the reply for abuse, unsafe advice, and category-specific hazards before it ships. Providers align models against this by default, but your bar may be stricter — a children's education product and a security research tool draw the line in very different places.

Action screening. The sharpest check of all, because actions have consequences that words do not. Recall the most important line you wrote in the ReAct chapter: TOOLS[name](arg) — the boundary where the model's wish becomes your code's deed. Action screening guards that boundary with policy: an allowlist of callable tools, argument limits ("refunds up to 5,000, no further"), rate limits, and an escalation rule for anything unusual. The model may argue eloquently for a 250,000 refund; the screen does not read the argument. It reads the number.

Defense in depth: layers, not a wall

No single check above is trustworthy on its own. The injection screen can be rephrased around. The topic filter has blind spots. Even the action screen only guards the tools it knows about. The design answer is defense in depth — many independent, individually imperfect layers, arranged so an attack (or an honest malfunction) must defeat all of them at once. The stack, in order: the provider's alignment training, your system prompt, input guardrails, action screening at the tool boundary, the sandbox under anything that executes, human approval for the irreversible, output guardrails on the way out — and observability, next chapter, watching it all after the fact.

Layering has a price: every in-line check adds latency, and some add real money. Teams manage the bill by ordering checks along a pyramid — plain code first, small classifiers second, LLM-as-judge last and only where it earns its keep:

Some harnesses claw the latency back by running input guardrails in parallel with the main model call: the agent starts working optimistically, and if a guardrail trips its tripwire — its halt signal — the run is cancelled before any output escapes. Blocked requests waste a few tokens; the clean majority pays zero latency.

The other cost is subtler: false positives. Tighten the injection screen and it starts blocking customers who innocently write "ignore my previous email". Every threshold is a product decision — how much friction will you impose on the honest many to stop the hostile few? That is why guardrail configurations get reviewed by product and compliance people, not just engineers.

Guardrails vs alignment: whose job is what

A fair question: model providers already spend fortunes on alignment — safety behaviour trained into the model itself, which is why a raw model politely refuses obvious harms out of the box. If the model is aligned, why is this chapter necessary?

Because alignment and guardrails answer different questions. Alignment is general: decided at training time, by the provider, for every customer at once. It knows nothing about your world — it has no idea your refund limit is 5,000, that your agent must never discuss a rival's pricing, or that your regulator forbids card numbers in logs. Those are your rules, and only your runtime layer can enforce them. Alignment is also probabilistic — it makes bad behaviour rare, not impossible, and jailbreaks that talk a model past its training surface every month. A guardrail, being ordinary code, does not get talked past.

So the clean division of labour: the provider makes the model want to behave; your app layer makes misbehaviour not matter — screened on the way in, contained in the sandbox, capped at the tool boundary, checked on the way out. You would not skip the outbound desk just because you hired an honest assistant. Honest assistants have bad days; checklists do not.

In the wild

Grep any serious agent repository for guardrail, redact, moderation, or validate_output and you will find this chapter staring back. In merge requests it appears as "add PII middleware before the model call", "tighten the tool allowlist", or a YAML policy file listing blocked topics and refund ceilings. In incident reviews it is the sentence "the output validator caught it before any customer saw it" — or, in the bad reviews, the absence of that sentence. When a vendor demo says "enterprise-grade safety", you can now ask the precise question: which checks, at which boundaries, and what happens when one trips?

Check yourself

1. What separates a guardrail from a rule written in the system prompt?

A system prompt is a request the model usually honours; a guardrail is a mechanism that holds even when the model is confused, manipulated, or having a bad day. That independence is the entire point.

2. Why must PII redaction happen before the model call rather than after?

The moment unredacted text leaves your process, copies exist in places you no longer control. Redacting first means the model, the API logs, and your traces only ever see placeholders like [CARD].

3. The model proposes a refund of 250,000 with a genuinely persuasive justification. What does a well-built action screen do?

Action screening judges the numbers against policy, never the eloquence of the argument. Anything over the agent's authority routes to human-in-the-loop — the guardrail's third verdict alongside pass and block.

4. Your injection screen catches known attack phrasings, but attackers keep rephrasing. What is the defense-in-depth answer?

No input screen will ever be complete; the design goal is that defeating one layer wins the attacker nothing. The tool allowlist and the sandbox do not care how cleverly the injected text was phrased.

5. If the provider's alignment training already makes the model refuse harmful requests, what must the app layer still do?

Alignment is general and probabilistic: it is decided at training time for everyone, knows nothing of your refund ceiling or your regulator, and can be jailbroken. Your runtime guardrails carry the rules that are yours alone.

Key takeaways

  • A guardrail is a runtime check outside the model at a data boundary — it can pass, block, rewrite, or escalate, and its verdict never depends on the model behaving.
  • Input guardrails screen what flows toward the model: injection patterns, off-topic requests, and PII — which must be redacted before the call, because sent text has already left your boundary.
  • Output guardrails vet what comes back: schema validation, grounding checks against retrieved sources, content safety, and — sharpest of all — action screening at the tool boundary, where policy judges the numbers and not the model's argument.
  • No layer is perfect alone; defense in depth stacks alignment, prompts, input checks, allowlists, sandboxing, human approval, and output checks so that one failure wins an attacker nothing.
  • Order checks by cost — regex first, classifiers second, LLM-as-judge last — and treat every threshold as a product decision, because false positives tax your honest users.
  • Alignment is the provider's training-time work, general and probabilistic; guardrails are your runtime work, specific and mechanical. Serious deployments always carry both.
