Speaking data scientist

Train/test splits, overfitting, precision vs recall, drift — about fifteen words carry every model review on Earth. By the end of this chapter you will not just understand them; you will know which question to ask after each one.

The story

Your niece is preparing for a bank recruitment exam. She has found a book of five hundred past questions and memorised every single one — question, answer, even the page numbers. On practice tests drawn from that book she scores a perfect 100%. Her friend Meera works through the concepts instead, does fewer past papers, and scores a humbler 85%.

Exam day arrives, full of questions neither has ever seen. Meera scores 84% — almost exactly her practice score. Your niece scores 58%, because her perfect memory of old questions says nothing about new ones. Their tutor saw it coming: that is why she always kept one sealed mock paper nobody was allowed to practise on. Whatever you scored on that paper was your real level.

Everything in this chapter — held-out test sets, overfitting, cross-validation, even drift — is this one tutoring insight, wearing different hats: the only score that counts is the score on questions you have never seen.

Why data scientists hide data from their own model

In the previous chapter you opened a PKL file and found a trained model preserved inside like a pickle in a jar. Before it went into the jar, that model went through school — a school with a strict exam policy, because a model is a spectacular memoriser, exactly like your niece.

So data scientists split their labelled data — say, 10,000 past transactions, each labelled fraud or legit — into three piles before training begins. The training set is what the model actually learns from — its textbook and homework, usually the biggest share. The validation set is a held-out slice used during development to compare candidate models and tune settings — the mock exams. And the test set is the sealed paper: locked away and touched exactly once, at the end, to produce the honest number you report.

Note

For anything time-shaped — fraud, prices, demand — good teams split by time: train on January–September, test on October onwards. A random split would let the model train on Tuesday's data and be "tested" on the Monday before it, quietly leaking the future into the past.

Overfitting and underfitting: the memoriser and the slacker

Your niece has an official name in this world. Overfitting is when a model learns the training data too specifically — memorising quirks and noise instead of the underlying pattern — so it aces training and stumbles on anything new. Its opposite, underfitting, is the slacker: a model too simple (or trained too little) to capture the pattern at all, so it does badly everywhere. Between them sits the goal, generalisation — performance that carries over to unseen data. Generalisation is the entire game; everything else in this chapter is bookkeeping in its service.

Here is the beautiful part: you do not need to understand a model's internals to diagnose overfitting. You only need two numbers, and the gap between them.

So when a colleague says "training accuracy is 99 but test is 62", you can reply "so it's overfitting" and be exactly right. Bigger, fancier models have more room to memorise, which is why data scientists fight back with more data, simpler models, and regularisation — deliberate constraints that make memorising harder than actually learning.

Cross-validation: rotating the mock papers

One held-out slice gives one estimate — and a single slice can be lucky or unlucky, especially when data is scarce. Cross-validation fixes this by rotation: cut the data into, say, five equal folds and train five times, each time holding out a different fold as the exam. Every row gets one turn at being "unseen"; averaging the five scores gives a far steadier estimate, plus a spread that shows how much the score wobbles.

So when a colleague reports "5-fold CV, 87 plus or minus 2", translate: we ran the exam five times with rotated papers; the model scores about 87, consistently. The "plus or minus" matters — a model scoring 87, 88, 86, 87, 87 is a very different beast from one lurching between 99 and 68 on its way to the same average.

Precision vs recall: a day at the fraud desk

Accuracy has a dirty secret, and fraud is the cleanest place to see it. Suppose 1,000 payments flow through today and 10 are fraudulent — a 1% base rate, the background frequency of the thing you are hunting. The laziest possible "model" — predict legit for everything — is 99% accurate and catches zero fraud. On imbalanced problems, accuracy flatters the do-nothing strategy, which is why fraud teams barely mention it.

Instead they count four outcomes. Say the model flags 20 payments, and 8 really are fraud:

Actually fraud (10)Actually legit (990)
Flagged8 — true positives (caught)12 — false positives (false alarms)
Not flagged2 — false negatives (slipped through)978 — true negatives (correctly ignored)

That 2-by-2 grid is the confusion matrix — hits, false alarms, misses, correct passes. From it come the two words you will hear in every review from now on:

Precision asks: of everything we flagged, how much was real? Here, 8 of 20 — 40%. It measures how clean the alarms are; low precision means the review team drowns in false alarms and starts ignoring the model. Recall asks: of everything real, how much did we flag? Here, 8 of 10 — 80%. It measures how much the net actually catches; low recall means fraud walks out undetected.

And here is the tension that makes this vocabulary matter: you can almost always buy more of one by giving up the other. The model does not really say "fraud" or "legit" — it produces a risk score, and a threshold decides where suspicion begins. Lower it and you flag more: recall rises, precision falls. Raise it: cleaner alarms, more fraud slips through. Choosing the threshold is a business decision dressed as a technical one — does a missed fraud or an annoyed customer cost you more? Because precision and recall pull against each other, teams often want one combined number. F1 is that number: the harmonic mean of precision and recall. "Harmonic" is the key word — unlike a plain average, it collapses towards the weaker of the two, so precision 100% with recall 2% earns an F1 near 0.04, not a flattering 51%. F1 is only high when both are. A decent single dial — but never let it hide the two numbers underneath, because the costs of false alarms and misses are rarely symmetric, and F1 pretends they are.

AUC: judging the ranker, not the threshold

Every metric so far depended on where you set the threshold. AUC (area under the ROC curve) deliberately does not. Its interpretation is wonderfully plain: pick one random fraud and one random legit transaction — AUC is the probability the model scores the fraud higher. A coin-toss model gets 0.5; a perfect ranker gets 1.0.

So AUC measures the quality of the ranking, across all possible thresholds at once. That makes it ideal for comparing candidate models before a threshold is chosen — and slightly dangerous on a slide, because a gorgeous AUC says nothing about the operating point you actually ship. When AUC appears in a review, the follow-up writes itself: "lovely — and at the threshold we deploy, what are precision and recall?"

Calibration: when 90% should mean 90%

One more property, subtler and often forgotten: are the model's probabilities honest? A model is well calibrated when, across all the cases where it says "90% likely fraud", roughly 90% really are fraud. Think of a weather forecaster who says "80% chance of rain" on 100 different days: trustworthy if it rained on about 80 of them, whatever any single day did.

Calibration is separate from ranking. A model can have superb AUC while its numbers are fantasy — saying "99%" when it should say "60%". Harmless if scores are only ever compared against a threshold; disastrous the moment anyone treats them as real probabilities, multiplying by transaction value to estimate expected loss or feeding a downstream system that trusts the number. If people will read the probability rather than merely sort by it, ask whether it has been calibrated.

Drift: the world moves, the jar doesn't

Now for the vocabulary that earns its keep after launch. The model you unpickled from its PKL jar is frozen — a snapshot of the months it trained on. The world it operates in is not. As the gap between the two grows, performance rots quietly; data scientists call the rot drift.

It comes in two flavours worth distinguishing in a meeting. Data drift means the inputs have changed shape since training — a new payment method launches, a marketing push brings customers from a new region. The model is being asked questions from a syllabus it never studied. Concept drift is sneakier: the inputs look the same, but the relationship between inputs and the answer has changed. Fraudsters are the classic case — they study what gets blocked and adapt, so January's tell-tale patterns mean nothing by June. Data drift is the exam changing subjects; concept drift is the answer key being rewritten.

Neither announces itself with an error message. The model keeps returning confident scores; a traceback would almost be kinder. The only defence is monitoring: dashboards comparing today's input and score distributions against training-time baselines, and — once true labels arrive weeks later, when chargebacks land — tracking live precision and recall. Mature teams define retraining triggers in advance instead of waiting for a bad quarter to reveal the rot.

How to ask smart questions in a model review

You now own the vocabulary; here is how it converts into a seat at the table. Most model reviews walk through the same five slides, and each has a question that marks you as someone worth inviting back:

Slide: "Accuracy is 99.2%"

Slide"Accuracy: 99.2%"
→
You"What's the base rate — and what would always-predicting-no score?"

With 1% fraud, always-predicting-no scores 99% — so 99.2% may be barely better than a doorstop. A baseline is the simplest strategy a model must beat; every headline metric needs one.

Slide: "Evaluated on 20% held-out data"

Slide"80/20 split"
→
You"Random split, or by time? Was the test set touched during tuning?"

A random split on a time-shaped problem leaks the future into training; a test set consulted repeatedly has become a mock exam. Either silently inflates the reported score.

Slide: "Precision is 90%"

Slide"Precision: 90%"
→
You"At which threshold — and what's recall there?"

One of the pair alone is meaningless: 90% precision at 5% recall is a net that catches almost nothing, immaculately. Ask for both at the shipping threshold — and who bears the cost of each kind of error.

Slide: "AUC is 0.97"

Slide"AUC: 0.97"
→
You"Great ranking. Are the probabilities calibrated, and what operating point ships?"

AUC praises the ranking across all thresholds and says nothing about the one you will deploy — or whether "0.93 risk" means anything as a number. Ranking, threshold, calibration: three separate claims.

Slide: "Ready to deploy"

Slide"Ship it"
→
You"How will we notice drift — and what triggers a retrain?"

Ask what is monitored (input and score distributions, live metrics once labels arrive), who gets paged, and what degradation triggers retraining. Teams with crisp answers here are the ones whose models still work in a year.

Tip

None of these questions requires you to have trained anything. They are all one shape: "this number was measured under certain conditions — do those conditions match production?" That is an operator's question, and you have asked it about deployments for years. You are not visiting this world; you live next door.

In the wild

This vocabulary is everywhere once you can hear it. A merge request adding a model carries an evaluation table — precision, recall, F1, AUC — often pasted straight from scikit-learn's classification_report. Experiment trackers like MLflow plot train-vs-validation curves whose diverging lines are overfitting drawn as a picture. Monitoring dashboards alert on distribution shift (you may see "PSI", population stability index, as the drift alarm). And the same words follow you into LLM land: teams hold out evaluation prompts so prompt-tweaks are not graded on questions they were tuned on, worry about leaked benchmarks — overfitting's twin — and track answer-quality drift after every model upgrade. Fifteen words, an entire industry.

Check yourself

1. Why do data scientists lock away a test set before training even begins?

A model is a spectacular memoriser, so its score on its own training data is nearly meaningless. The sealed test set is the exam nobody practised on — the only score that counts.

2. Training accuracy 99%, test accuracy 62%. What is the diagnosis?

The big train-to-test gap is the overfitting signature. Underfit models are bad on both; drift is a post-launch disease, and this model has not shipped yet.

3. A missed fraud costs your bank far more than a false alarm. Which metric do you push hardest to raise?

Recall measures how much of the real fraud the net catches. When misses are the expensive error, you lower the threshold and deliberately trade precision away for recall.

4. A model has AUC 0.97, yet when it says "90% fraud risk" only half of those cases are fraud. What is missing?

AUC only rewards putting positives above negatives — it says nothing about whether the scores mean what they claim. Calibration is the separate, fixable property.

5. A fraud model performed beautifully at launch. Six months later it misses most new fraud, though the code and the model file are byte-for-byte unchanged. Most likely cause?

The model is a snapshot of the past; fraudsters adapt (concept drift) and the customer base shifts (data drift). Nothing "breaks", no error is raised — exactly why monitoring and retraining triggers exist.

Key takeaways

  • The only score that counts is the score on unseen data: train to learn, validate to tune, test once to report — a test set that got peeked at is just another mock exam.
  • Overfitting is memorisation, diagnosed from two numbers: a big gap between training and test performance. Cross-validation rotates the held-out slice for a steadier measurement.
  • On imbalanced problems, accuracy flatters the do-nothing baseline. Ask for precision (how clean the alarms are) and recall (how much of the real thing is caught) at the threshold that ships.
  • F1 squeezes precision and recall into one honest-ish number; AUC scores the ranking across all thresholds; calibration asks whether "90%" is right 90% of the time. Three different claims — keep them separate.
  • A deployed model is frozen while the world moves: data drift changes the questions, concept drift rewrites the answer key, and only monitoring plus planned retraining catches the silent rot.
  • Every smart review question has one shape: "this number was measured under certain conditions — do they match production?" You have asked that your whole career; now ask it here.
