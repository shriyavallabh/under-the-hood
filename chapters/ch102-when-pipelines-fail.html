When pipelines fail

A red pipeline is not an accusation — it is a message with a return address. This chapter teaches you to open the log, read it from the bottom, name the failure in one of six ways, and know instantly whether to retry, fix, or ask.

~30 minChapter 103 of 135
The story

The departure board flips to CANCELLED in red, and half the gate erupts. People photograph the board. Someone demands a supervisor. But the woman next to you — clearly a veteran of a thousand flights — just picks up her bag and walks to the desk with one question: "Why?" Because the reason decides everything. Fog over the runway? Wait an hour and the same plane flies — no action needed, just retry. Crew shortage? The airline rebooks you — someone else's fix. Your visa expired last Tuesday? No amount of waiting at the gate will ever board you — that one is yours to fix, and retrying is pure theatre.

A failed pipeline is that cancelled flight. The red icon is just the board — loud, alarming, and almost information-free. The reason lives one click deeper, in the job log, and it nearly always falls into one of six familiar categories. Veterans do not panic at red. They ask why, classify, and act. By the end of this chapter, you are the woman with the bag.

Red is information, not a verdict

In the previous chapter you decoded .gitlab-ci.yml: a pipeline is stages, stages are jobs, and a job is nothing more mysterious than a fresh container running the shell commands listed under its script:. That plumbing tells you exactly what "failed" means. A job fails when one of its commands finishes with a non-zero exit code — the little number every program hands back to the shell when it ends, where zero means "all good" and anything else means "something went wrong". GitLab runs your commands, watches the exit codes, and paints the job red the moment one is not zero.

That is the whole trick. Nobody's judgement is involved, no AI decided your code is bad, and the red X on your merge request is not a comment on you as a professional. A command returned 1 instead of 0. Your job now is exactly the airport question: why? — and the answer is always written down, waiting, in the job log: the captured, timestamped output of everything the job printed while it ran.

Getting to it takes two clicks, in the GitLab screens you toured in the GitLab tour: from the merge request, click the failed pipeline in the pipeline widget, then click the job with the red X. You land on a dark page full of scrolling text. Most newcomers freeze here, because the log for even a small job can run to hundreds or thousands of lines. The next section is the skill that unfreezes you.

Read the log bottom-up

You already own this skill. Back in the errors and tracebacks chapter you learned that a Python traceback reads bottom-up: the last line names the actual error, the line above it points at the exact code that raised it, and everything higher is just the trail of calls that led there. Reading top-down drowns you in framework noise; reading bottom-up hands you the culprit in seconds. Job logs obey the same law, for the same reason — output is written in time order, so the failure is always the last interesting thing that happened.

The top of every job log is runner housekeeping: which machine picked up the job, which Docker image it pulled, the git checkout, the cache restore. It looks intimidating and matters almost never. The bottom is where the verdict lives — so scroll straight to the end, find the failure line, and walk upward until you hit the first line that actually explains something. GitLab even helps: the log is folded into collapsible sections (preparing executor, getting source, your script, and so on), and your commands appear prefixed with $, so you can see exactly which command was running when things died.

Install succeeded — so on your upward walk you can rule out a dependency failure. Every command that finished cleanly is a suspect eliminated.
$ ruff check src/ tests/
All checks passed!
Lint passed too. Not a formatting problem. Two suspects down.
$ pytest -q tests/
The command that was running when the job died — it matches a line under script: in .gitlab-ci.yml. This is what you will re-run locally.
.........................................F
FAILED tests/test_retry.py::test_gives_up_after_three - AssertionError: assert 5 == 3
THE CULPRIT — the first genuinely explanatory line on your walk up from the bottom. It names the file, the exact test, and the broken expectation: the code retried five times, the test expected three. Ninety percent of triage is finding this one line.
1 failed, 41 passed in 14.20s
The summary. One test failed, forty-one are fine — this is a scalpel problem, not a bulldozer problem.
ERROR: Job failed: exit code 1
START HERE, at the very bottom. This line is the verdict, and it appears on virtually every failed job — which means it tells you almost nothing. It only says "a command exited non-zero". The truth is always in the lines above it.
Tip

On long logs, do not scroll by hand. Use your browser's find-in-page for FAILED, Error, or Traceback, or click GitLab's "scroll to bottom" button on the job page. The goal is always the same: reach the last interesting line fast, then walk up.

The six usual suspects

Here is the secret that turns pipeline panic into routine: almost every failure you will ever see is one of six types. Data scientists and platform engineers triage red pipelines quickly not because they read faster, but because they pattern-match the bottom of the log against this list. Now the full field guide — symptom, cause, and exactly what to do:

Failure typeTell-tale bottom linesUsual causeYour move
Lint / formatwould reformat, line too long, a wall of style codes like E501Code works fine but violates the team's style rulesRun the linter/formatter locally, commit the diff it produces

A few field notes that save real time. Lint failures are the most common and the least scary — the fix is usually one command (the same ruff or formatter the pipeline ran) followed by a commit; many teams wire it into pre-commit hooks so it never reaches CI at all. Test failures are the pipeline doing its job: a test encoded an expectation and your change violated it — the next chapter, on pytest, teaches you to read and write those expectations yourself. Dependency failures often appear when someone adds an import without adding the package, or when CI's Python version differs from the laptop's — the environment discipline from the environments chapter is the vaccine. Build failures fail fastest and explain themselves best; a SyntaxError with a line number is the friendliest error in this whole zoo. Flaky failures are the only type where the honest answer is "not my commit" — and the only type where retry is a legitimate first move. Permissions failures are special for one more reason: they are frequently not yours to fix, and the fastest resolution is a well-written message to whoever maintains the project's CI settings.

Retry or fix?

Every failed job in GitLab has a tempting Retry button, and using it well is a mark of seniority. The rule is one sentence: retry is only for failures that were never about your commit. If the bottom of the log points at your code, your tests, your requirements file, or your formatting, retrying is the visa-expired traveller re-checking the departures board — the same red will come back in eight minutes, you will have learned nothing, and you will have burned runner minutes the whole team shares.

So ask two questions, in order. First: does the failing line mention something my change plausibly touched? If yes, fix. Second: is the failure external — a timeout, a 503, a runner crash, a registry blip? If yes, retry once. If the same external failure comes back on the retry, it is no longer flaky — the word for a failure that comes and goes without any code change — it is a real outage or a real misconfiguration, and it deserves a report, not a third retry.

Careful

Serial retrying is how teams end up "living with" a flaky test that fails one run in five. Every mashed retry teaches everyone to distrust the pipeline, and a distrusted pipeline stops gating anything. If you retry the same job twice in one day for the same reason, say so out loud in the merge request — naming a flake is the first step to killing it.

Reproduce it locally

Here is the demystifying move: a CI job is just commands in a container, so you can almost always run the failure on your own laptop. Open .gitlab-ci.yml, find the failing job, and read its script: list — those lines are shell commands, nothing more. Check out the same branch (just enough git covers the checkout), then run the same commands in order:

terminal — replaying the failed job by hand
git switch feature/faster-retries
pip install -r requirements.txt
ruff check src/ tests/
pytest -q tests/test_retry.py::test_gives_up_after_three

Note the last line: you do not need to re-run all forty-two tests. The FAILED line in the log gave you the exact test's address, and pytest accepts it verbatim — a tight loop of seconds instead of minutes. If the failure reproduces locally, you are in the best possible position: a private, fast, fully inspectable copy of the bug. If it does not reproduce, that is information too — the difference between your machine and CI (Python version, missing environment variable, an OS difference) is now the prime suspect, and the job log's header tells you the exact Docker image to compare against. For a perfect replica, run the commands inside that same image with docker run -it python:3.11-slim bash — same container, same behaviour.

Asking for help without losing goodwill

Sometimes triage dead-ends: the log points somewhere you cannot see, the failure will not reproduce, or the fix clearly needs permissions you do not have. Asking for help is the correct move — senior engineers do it constantly — but how you ask decides whether you get an answer in three minutes or a sigh. The unspoken etiquette: retry once if it smelled like infra, spend an honest thirty to forty-five minutes on your own triage, and then ask with a failure report that does the reader's first five minutes of work for them:

a failure report that gets answered fast
Job: <link to the failed job>
Branch / commit: feature/faster-retries @ 4f9c2d1e

What failed (last lines of the log):
    FAILED tests/test_retry.py::test_gives_up_after_three - AssertionError: assert 5 == 3
    1 failed, 41 passed in 14.20s

What I tried:
    - Reproduced locally with pytest — same failure
    - Passes on main; fails only with my change to RetryPolicy
    - Not flaky: failed identically on a retry

My best guess: my new backoff loop counts the first attempt as a retry.
Question: should max_retries include the first attempt, or not?

Every element earns its place. The link means nobody has to hunt. The exact bottom lines mean nobody has to scroll. What you tried proves effort and eliminates the first three suggestions anyone would make. The best guess gives an expert something to confirm or correct in one sentence — far faster than composing an explanation from scratch. Compare this with "pipeline is broken, can someone look?" — a message that transfers all of the work and none of the information. One habit, and never break it: paste log lines, but never paste secrets, tokens, or anything from the CI/CD variables screen. Logs mask known secrets automatically; your clipboard does not.

In the wild

Watch any active repository's merge requests for a week and you will see this whole chapter play out: commits titled "fix lint" thirty seconds after a red pipeline (lint failure, correctly triaged), a lonely "retry" on a job that timed out pulling an image (flaky, correctly retried once), and an MR comment that quotes four log lines and pings the platform channel about a 403 (permissions, correctly escalated). You will also spot the anti-patterns — the same job retried five times in an hour, or a red pipeline sitting untouched for two days because its author found the log intimidating. When a data scientist mutters "CI is red again" in standup, you can now ask the only question that matters: what do the bottom lines say?

Check yourself

1. Your merge request's pipeline just went red. What is the correct first move?

The reason decides the action, and the reason lives at the bottom of the job log. Retrying or re-pushing before reading is acting before knowing — the same red will usually come straight back.

2. The last explanatory line reads ERROR: No matching distribution found for pandas==9.9.9. Which failure type is this, and what is your move?

pip is saying no such version of the package exists, so the requirements file itself is wrong. No number of retries changes what is on the package index — this is a fix, not a retry.

3. When is the Retry button the right first response?

Retry is only for failures that were never about your change: infrastructure hiccups. And only once — if the same external failure returns on the retry, it is an outage or a misconfiguration and deserves a report.

4. What does the final line ERROR: Job failed: exit code 1 actually tell you?

That line appears on virtually every failed job, whatever the cause — it is the verdict, not the evidence. The bottom-up walk starts there and continues up to the first line that explains something.

5. Which help request will get answered fastest?

A good failure report does the reader's first five minutes of work for them: no hunting, no scrolling, the obvious suggestions already ruled out, and a guess they can confirm or correct in one line.

Key takeaways

  • A job fails when one command exits non-zero; the red X is a message, not a judgement, and the reason is always written in the job log.
  • Read logs bottom-up — the same skill as tracebacks from chapter 9: start at the verdict, walk up to the first explanatory line, and skip the runner housekeeping at the top.
  • Six types cover almost every failure: lint/format, test, dependency/install, build, flaky/infra, and permissions/secrets. Classify first — the type dictates the move.
  • Retry is only for failures that were never about your commit, and only once; everything else needs a fix, and serial retrying teaches the whole team to distrust the pipeline.
  • A CI job is just commands in a container — copy the script: lines from .gitlab-ci.yml and replay them locally, targeting the exact failing test for a seconds-fast loop.
  • Ask for help after honest triage, with a failure report: job link, exact bottom lines, what you tried, and your best guess — never pasted secrets.
