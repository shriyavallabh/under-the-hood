Act XV · The Enterprise Agent Platform

Merging two platforms into one

Two teams built two agent harnesses that do overlapping things, and leadership wants one. This is not a code problem first - it is a decision problem, and the final chapter of the course.

~30 minChapter 133 of 133
The story

Two neighbouring villages each built a bridge across the same river, a little apart, not quite knowing about each other. Now the region wants one road, which means one bridge. The tempting move - tear both down and build a grand new one from scratch - is also the slowest and the riskiest, and it insults two teams of proud bridge-builders. The wiser move is quieter: keep the bridge that already carries daily traffic, study what the other one did better - a smarter railing, a cleverer support - and graft those improvements onto the one people already cross. Nobody's work is wasted; the road opens sooner; and the builders of the second bridge see their best ideas live on.

This is the last thing this act teaches, and it is barely about code. When an organisation ends up with two agent harnesses and decides it wants one offering, the hard questions are human and strategic: whose foundation do we build on, which features survive, and how do we merge without wasting anyone's effort or bruising their pride? Get that right and the diff writes itself. Get it wrong and the best-engineered platform still fails.

Start from what is already in production

The going-in position that survives contact with reality: build on the harness that is already in production with real users. It is the lower-risk foundation - it works, it is battle-tested, it has people depending on it - so you keep it as the base, port the best features from the other one, and refactor to adopt its better practices. Resist the rewrite-from-scratch urge; a grand new bridge that carries no traffic yet is a bet, not a plan. Consolidation is addition and grafting, not demolition.

Each port is a win-win

The best consolidation work delivers value on the way. Take a concrete case you have already met: the in-production base stores conversations but has no replay; the other harness has a working time machine. Port replay into the base and you have done two things at once - shipped a feature real users were asking for, and moved the two platforms one step closer to being one. That is the pattern to look for: a feature that is missing here, present there, and demanded now. Merge those, and consolidation stops being a tax and starts being a series of wins.

Write down why - the decision record

Consolidation decisions get questioned for months. A lead returns from leave; a new engineer asks "why did we pick that one?"; someone wonders if the choice was politics. The antidote is an ADR - an Architecture Decision Record - a short document capturing what was compared and why a choice was made. It is not bureaucracy; it is the proof that a decision was considered, not arbitrary, and it saves you re-arguing the same point every time a new person joins. Keep them tiny:

adr-007-harness-base.md - a decision record
# ADR 007: Which harness is the base
A numbered, titled record. A repo accumulates a folder of these, each one decision, read in minutes.
## Status: accepted
Decisions have a lifecycle - proposed, accepted, superseded. Status tells a future reader whether this is still the current call.
## Context
Two harnesses overlap; we must ship one.
The situation, briefly. Enough for someone absent from the room to understand the forces in play.
## Decision
Base = harness A (in production); port replay from B.
The actual choice, in one line. This is what people will quote back - so make it unambiguous.
## Consequences
B's team contributes ports via merge requests.
What follows from the choice - including how the other team stays involved. Honest about trade-offs, so nobody feels a cost was hidden.

Scale delivery: the tenant-contributor model

Once there is one harness, demand for features usually outpaces the small core team. The move that scales is the tenant-contributor model: the teams that use the harness also contribute to it. A tenant needs a feature, writes it, and raises a merge request; the core team reviews and merges, producing a new version. This aligns contributors with the value they need and multiplies delivery beyond what the core team could write alone. It works best once a tenant's requirements are clear; during the discovery phase, a newcomer first learns the platform through the backlog before contributing freely. And the architecture has to permit it - a stable skills middleware or tool interface is the seam that lets many hands add capabilities without stepping on each other, exactly the kind of pluggable layer you saw dissected in the best-known harness.

Careful

The failure mode here is never technical - it is a team feeling steamrolled because "their" platform lost. Hear every perspective, make the losing team's best ideas visibly win by porting them, and let them contribute to the unified result. A merge that is engineered perfectly but leaves half the people resentful has not actually succeeded.

In the wild

You will find an adr/ or docs/decisions/ folder, a platform-comparison spreadsheet, and commit messages like "port replay from the other harness." An internal platform that accepts merge requests from the product teams that use it is the tenant-contributor model in the flesh, and a monorepo consolidation is often the "new home repo" where the unified harness lives. When a large organisation says "we're merging the two agent platforms," the meeting is about base, ports, and who contributes - precisely this chapter, and a fitting place to close the course.

Key takeaways

  • Merging two harnesses is a decision problem before a code problem: whose base, which features survive, and how to merge without wasting effort or bruising pride.
  • Build on the harness already in production with real users - the lower-risk base - then port the best features from the other and refactor, rather than rewriting from scratch.
  • Make each port a win-win: a feature missing here but present there and demanded now (like replay) both ships value and advances the merge.
  • Record decisions in short ADRs (context, decision, consequences) so the choice is transparent and not re-argued whenever someone new arrives.
  • Scale delivery with the tenant-contributor model - the teams that use the harness raise merge requests the core team reviews - enabled by a stable skills/middleware seam.
  • The hardest part is human: hear every perspective, let the other team's best ideas visibly win, and a unified harness in one home repo becomes something everyone owns.
