Merge requests, end to end

The complete ritual, one screen at a time: open the merge request, gather your reviewers, work the threads, wait for green — and then press the most satisfying button in software.

~35 minChapter 100 of 123
The story

A busy restaurant keeps one master cookbook. Every dish the kitchen serves comes from it, so nobody — not even the head chef — writes in it directly. When a line cook improves the dal recipe, she photocopies the page, makes her changes on the copy, and clips it to the front of the book with a note: "Reduced the cream, added kasuri methi. Please taste before this goes official."

That evening the sous chef tastes it. He scribbles two notes in the margin — "salt is high", "try 20 minutes, not 25". She adjusts, he tastes again, and this time he initials the card. Only now is the new page pasted into the master book. Her photocopy goes in the bin; the book remains the single, trusted source of truth.

The clipped card — the copy, the note, the margin conversation, the initials, the pasting-in, the bin — is a merge request. This chapter walks the whole ritual, screen by screen, exactly the way you will live it in a working team.

One object, two jobs

Quick refresher from the Git chapter: you never work on main directly. You make a branch — a private copy of the project's timeline — commit your work there, and git push it to the server. Your branch now exists on GitLab, but it is still a photocopy clipped to nothing. The team's main has not changed at all.

A merge request (MR) is the formal ask that closes that gap: "please merge my branch into main". But it is really two things stapled together. First, it is a diff — the precise set of line-by-line changes between your branch and the target. Second, it is a conversation wrapped around that diff: comments pinned to specific lines, approvals, a pipeline verdict, and finally a merge. GitHub calls the same object a pull request; the ritual is identical, only the labels differ.

Hold on to that framing. Everything on the MR screen — every button, badge, and checkbox — serves either the diff or the conversation.

Screen one: the push that starts everything

The ritual begins in your terminal, and GitLab meets you there. The moment you push a new branch, the server prints a gift into your push output:

Those remote: lines are GitLab talking back through Git. Cmd-click the link and you land directly on the New merge request form with your branch pre-selected. (If you missed it, no harm: GitLab also shows a "Create merge request" banner on the project page for recently pushed branches, and there is always the Merge requests section in the left sidebar you met in the previous chapter's tour.)

Now step through the form itself, field by field:

1. Where from, where to

Source brancheval/tone-check — your work

Target branchmain — where it wants to live

The top of the form states the whole transaction: merge the source branch (yours) into the target branch (usually main). GitLab pre-fills both from your push link. Glance anyway — targeting the wrong branch is a classic Monday mistake, and everything downstream (the diff, the pipeline) depends on this pair.

2. The title — and the Draft: switch

TitleDraft: Add tone evaluator to the nightly suite
Description
Reviewer · Assignee

Write the title like a commit subject: short, imperative, specific — "Add tone evaluator to the nightly suite", not "eval changes v2 final". Starting the title with Draft: flags the MR as a work in progress: everyone can look, CI still runs, but the Merge button is locked until you remove the prefix (there is a one-click "Mark as ready" for that later).

3. The description — pick a template

Title
DescriptionChoose a template: default ↓
Reviewer · Assignee

Most teams keep description templates — pre-written outlines stored in the repo under .gitlab/merge_request_templates/ — and the form offers them in a dropdown. Pick one and fill in the blanks. A good description is a courtesy to the reviewer: what changed, why, and how to check it. We will dissect one in a moment.

4. Reviewer and assignee — two different jobs

Title · Description
Reviewerthe senior teammate you agreed with
Assigneeyou — you own landing this

The reviewer is the person you are asking to read the diff — they get a to-do item and their approval is what you are waiting for. The assignee is whoever is responsible for driving the MR to completion — almost always you, the author. Assigning the MR to your reviewer is a common beginner slip; it quietly tells GitLab they own finishing your work.

5. Create merge request

Create merge requestyour MR gets a number: !214

Press the button and the MR is born with a number like !214 (the exclamation mark means "merge request", the way #87 means "issue"). From here on, the MR page is home: the conversation on the Overview tab, the diff on the Changes tab, and the pipeline verdict pinned to the merge widget.

The description, dissected

Here is a filled-in description for our example MR — adding a tone evaluator to a nightly eval suite, the kind of change you will genuinely ship alongside data scientists. Click the annotated lines:

Description of !214 — following .gitlab/merge_request_templates/default.md
## What this MR does
The headline section. One or two sentences, plain language. Busy reviewers read this and decide how much attention the diff deserves — earn that attention honestly.
Adds a tone evaluator to the nightly suite so brand-voice
regressions fail the pipeline instead of reaching users.
## Why
The motivation, with a link. A reviewer should never have to ask "why does this exist?" — that question in a thread means your description failed.
Closes #87 (tone drift flagged after the March incident).
Closes #87 is magic text: when this MR merges, GitLab automatically closes issue 87 and links the two forever. Traceability for free.
## How to review
The kindest section you can write. Tell the reviewer where to start and what is boring: it shrinks their job from "read everything suspiciously" to "check the one interesting decision".
Start with scorer.py — everything else is wiring.
## Evidence
Proof it works: eval numbers, a screenshot, a log excerpt. For data-science MRs this section carries the most weight — reviewers trust measurements over adjectives.
Nightly run on this branch: 96.4% pass (main: 96.1%).
## Checklist
Markdown checkboxes render as real, clickable boxes on the MR page. Teams use them as a lightweight pre-flight ritual — tick them truthfully.
- [x] Tests added and green locally
- [x] No secrets or client names in the diff
Worth its own box on every template. A merge request is often visible to the whole company — and diffs live forever in history.

Asking for review like a colleague, not a supplicant

Setting the Reviewer field creates a to-do, but a to-do is not a conversation. In healthy teams you follow it with a short, human message — and there is a craft to writing one that gets picked up fast:

a review request that gets picked up fast
Hi Priya — when you have ~20 minutes today or tomorrow, could you
review !214? It adds the tone evaluator to the nightly suite.

The only interesting file is scorer.py; the rest is wiring.
No rush before Thursday's release cut. Happy to walk you through
it on a quick call if that's faster for you.

Notice the five moving parts: what it is, an honest size estimate, where to focus, a real (not fake-urgent) deadline, and an easy out. What you never do: ping every senior person hoping someone bites, mark a half-finished MR as ready to "get in the queue", or chase after two hours. If a review has not happened in a day or so, one polite nudge — mentioning the deadline, not their slowness — is fair game.

Tip

Small MRs get reviewed fast; big ones rot. A 150-line MR often lands the same day. A 1,500-line MR sits for a week and then gets a shallow "looks fine". If your change is growing, split it — reviewers reward you with speed and depth.

The conversation: threads, suggestions, resolution

Now the reviewer opens the Changes tab and reads your diff. When something needs discussing, they do not email you — they start a thread: a comment pinned to a specific line of the diff, carrying an unresolved / resolved state. Threads are the actual review. Here is one, played out to the end:

1. A thread appears on line 42

Diff, scorer.py line 42threshold = 0.7
Reviewer thread — Unresolved"Where does 0.7 come from? Our ad-hoc runs used 0.75."

The comment is anchored to the exact line, so the discussion stays next to the evidence. The MR now shows "1 unresolved thread" — and most projects are configured to block merging while any thread is unresolved.

2. The reviewer attaches a suggestion

Suggested change- threshold = 0.7 → + threshold = 0.75
Apply suggestiona button, right there in the thread

For small fixes, reviewers can go beyond commentary: a suggestion is a proposed edit rendered inline as a mini-diff, with an Apply suggestion button attached. It saves the "please change X to Y" round-trip entirely.

3. You reply — and apply

Your reply"Good catch — 0.75 matches the calibration run. Applying."
Apply suggestionGitLab creates a real commit on eval/tone-check for you

Clicking Apply is not cosmetic: GitLab commits the change to your source branch on your behalf, and the MR updates instantly. For bigger fixes you edit locally and git push to the same branch — every new push flows into the open MR automatically. No new MR, ever, for the same piece of work.

4. Resolve the thread

Thread resolved0 unresolved threads remain

Once the fix is in, someone clicks Resolve thread. Etiquette varies by team: some say the author resolves after fixing, others reserve it for whoever opened the thread. The safe universal rule — never resolve a thread you have neither fixed nor answered. Resolving to make a question disappear is the fastest way to lose a reviewer's trust.

5. The approval lands

Approved1 of 1 approvals — Priya pressed Approve

Satisfied, the reviewer presses Approve. An approval is a formal, recorded thumbs-up; projects can require one, two, or role-specific approvals (say, one from the code owners of evals/) before the Merge button unlocks. Note the new commits from step 3 also re-triggered the pipeline — which brings us to the last gate.

Taking feedback without ego

A page of red comments on your first real MR stings. Every engineer remembers that feeling — and the good ones learned the same few moves. First, read the whole review before replying to any single thread; comments often connect, and answering one in isolation makes you defend a position the reviewer already softened three comments later. Second, thank precisely: "good catch — that would have broken the Friday run" costs nothing and tells the reviewer their effort landed. Third, sort every comment into defect or preference. Defects you fix, gratefully. Preferences ("I'd name this score_tone") you concede cheaply — the karma is worth more than the name.

And when you genuinely disagree? Push back once, in the thread, with evidence: a measurement, a doc link, a failing case. If one written round does not settle it, take it to a fifteen-minute call and post the conclusion back to the thread for the record. What never works: relitigating across nine replies, quietly ignoring the comment, or treating the review as a verdict on you. The review is about the diff. You are not the diff — you are the person who gets better because someone read your work carefully, for free.

Careful

The asymmetry to remember: reviewers are spending their focus on your work. Respond to every thread — even just "done" or "agreed, follow-up in #91" — so nothing dangles. A trail of unanswered threads reads as carelessness, and carelessness is the one reputation that follows you between projects.

The merge widget: green, squash, merge

At the bottom of every MR sits the merge widget — the box that aggregates every gate into one verdict. It watches four things: the Draft flag, the pipeline, unresolved threads, and approvals. Watch it change state:

1. Pipeline running — merge blocked

Pipeline: runningtests · lint · nightly evals — for your latest commit
Mergeblocked: pipeline must succeed

Every push triggers the project's CI pipeline — the automated test and check suite you will meet in depth two chapters from now. Well-run projects enable "pipelines must succeed", so the Merge button stays dead while CI is running or red. The machine gets a vote, and its vote cannot be charmed.

2. Red pipeline — fix and push, that is all

Pipeline: failedtest_scorer.py — 1 failure
Mergestill blocked

A red pipeline is not a crisis and needs no new MR. Click into the failed job, read the log, fix locally, push to the same branch. The MR picks up the commit, the pipeline re-runs, the widget re-evaluates. This loop — push, watch, fix — is half of professional life.

3. Everything green — decisions before the button

Pipeline passed · Approvals 1/1 · Threads 0 unresolved
[x] Delete source branch  [x] Squash commits
Merge

Two checkboxes sit above the newly alive Merge button. Delete source branch: bin the photocopy after pasting — the commits will live on main, so nothing is lost. Squash commits: fold your branch's seven messy commits ("wip", "fix typo", "actually fix") into one clean, well-messaged commit on main. Many teams default both to on.

4. Press it

Mergedone squash commit on main · eval/tone-check deleted · issue #87 closed
→
mainnow contains the tone evaluator

The MR flips to Merged — a permanent record anyone can revisit years later to see what changed, why, who approved, and what the pipeline said. Your branch is gone, main moved forward, and Closes #87 quietly did its job. Locally, run git checkout main && git pull and start the next branch fresh.

Note

If the pipeline is still running but everything else is settled, you do not have to babysit it: Set to auto-merge tells GitLab to merge automatically the moment the pipeline passes — and to stand down if it fails. Ending the day by setting three MRs to auto-merge is a small, real pleasure.

The vocabulary, one flip at a time

Eight terms carry the whole ritual. Flip until each back feels obvious:

In the wild

On a data-science team, the MR page is where models actually ship: you will see MRs titled "Bump reranker to v3", with eval tables pasted as evidence and a thread war over one threshold. In chat you will hear the dialect — "can someone look at !214?", "LGTM" (looks good to me), "nit:" prefixing a take-it-or-leave-it preference, "resolve when done". And increasingly, the author of the MR is not human: coding agents push branches and open merge requests too, which makes this ritual — review threads, required approvals, a pipeline that cannot be charmed — the safety layer that lets teams accept an agent's work with the same scrutiny as anyone else's. The ritual is the point.

Check yourself

1. You want a senior data scientist to review your MR. Which field do you set — and where do you put yourself?

Reviewer = "please read this"; assignee = "responsible for landing it". You own landing your own MR, so you stay assignee. Assigning it to the reviewer quietly hands them your job.

2. Your MR title starts with Draft:. What does that actually change?

Draft is a merge lock plus a social signal, nothing more. The pipeline still runs and early feedback is welcome — it just cannot land on main by accident while you are still cooking.

3. The merge widget says "Merge blocked: pipeline must succeed" and the pipeline is red. What is the professional move?

An open MR tracks its source branch: every push flows in and re-runs the pipeline. New MRs for the same work just scatter the conversation — and overriding red pipelines is how Friday incidents are born.

4. The reviewer left a suggestion and you click "Apply suggestion". What happens?

Apply is a genuine write: the suggested edit becomes a commit on the branch, the MR updates, and the pipeline re-runs. Remember to git pull before your next local edit, or your branch will be one commit behind itself.

5. You tick "Squash commits" and "Delete source branch", then merge. What does main's history show — and did deleting the branch lose anything?

Squash folds "wip", "fix typo", and friends into a single well-messaged commit on the target branch. The source branch was only the photocopy — once the page is pasted into the master book, the copy can safely go in the bin.

Key takeaways

  • A merge request is two things stapled together: a diff (source branch into target branch) and a conversation around it (threads, approvals, pipeline). GitHub's pull request is the same ritual with different labels.
  • The ritual starts in your terminal — the push output hands you the create-MR link. Title like a commit subject, prefix Draft: while cooking, and fill the team's description template honestly, including "how to review".
  • Reviewer and assignee are different jobs: the reviewer reads and approves; the assignee (you) is responsible for landing it.
  • Threads are the real review. Reply to every one, apply or push fixes to the same branch, and never resolve a thread you have neither fixed nor answered.
  • The Merge button unlocks only when every gate agrees: not a draft, pipeline green, threads resolved, approvals in. Squash keeps main's history clean; deleting the source branch loses nothing.
  • Ask for review with size, focus, and a real deadline; take feedback as data about the diff, not a verdict on you. Fix defects gratefully, concede preferences cheaply, push back once — with evidence.
