The capability-matrix method

A demo persuades; a matrix decides. This chapter gives you the repeatable method for comparing any two harnesses — areas, sub-rows, evidence, colour — and for steering a room full of strong opinions to one logged decision.

~30 minChapter 96 of 133
The story

Your family is choosing between two flats, and the discussion is going nowhere. Your cousin loves flat A because the balcony "feels right". Your uncle loves flat B because the broker was convincing. Every Sunday the same argument, louder each week.

So you show up to the next viewing with a printed checklist. Down the left side: the areas that actually matter — plumbing, wiring, natural light, society paperwork, commute. Under each area, specific checks: turn every tap on and time the pressure. Plug a charger into every socket. Ask for the occupancy certificate and read it. Next to each check, three stickers: green, amber, red — and a rule: no green sticker unless someone in the family personally tested it. "The broker said so" earns amber at best.

Then the family meeting changes shape. Ten minutes per area. Curtain colours go on a "later" list. Anyone who wants to flip a sticker has to bring proof, not volume. And the final choice gets written on the back page, with reasons — so when your uncle asks in six months "why did we pick B?", nobody has to remember. The page does.

That checklist has a name in engineering: a capability matrix. This chapter teaches you to build one and to run the meeting.

Why smart rooms make bad harness decisions

In the previous chapter you built the demand side: the non-negotiable requirements a serious enterprise puts on any agent platform — durability, auditability, security, identity. And in the contenders you met the supply side: the harnesses competing to run your agents. This chapter is the bridge between the two — the method for holding supply up against demand without fooling yourself.

Left to themselves, harness comparisons fail in three predictable ways. First, demo capture: whichever framework gave the slickest twenty-minute demo wins, even though a demo exercises maybe five per cent of what production needs. Second, the loudest-voice problem: the most senior or most persistent engineer's favourite wins, and everyone else quietly disengages. Third, vibes scoring: "I heard it doesn't scale", "their docs look great", "someone on a forum said the checkpointing is flaky" — claims nobody verified, steering a decision that will bind the team for years.

The capability matrix — a structured table with capabilities as rows, candidate harnesses as columns, and an evidence-backed colour in every cell — exists to defeat all three. It is not paperwork. It is the difference between choosing a platform and being sold one.

Step one: define the areas

An area is a major territory of capability — a chapter heading for your rows. You have met every one of these territories earlier in the course, which is exactly why you are ready for this chapter now. A well-tested starting set has nine:

AreaWhat it coversOne sub-row you might write
Agent patternsWhich shapes of agent it supports — the plain ReAct loop, subagents, planner-executor, custom graphs"Can a subagent run with a smaller model than its parent?"
ToolsHow tools are defined, discovered and executed — including native tool calling and MCP support"Can we attach a remote MCP server over streamable HTTP?"
StateWhat survives a crash — checkpointing, resume, replay"Kill the process mid-run; does the run resume from the last step?"
SecurityBlast-radius control — sandboxing, tool allow-lists, guardrails on inputs and outputs"Can a tool be restricted to read-only for one agent but not another?"
Identity and auditWho ran what, as whom — per-user credentials, permission propagation, tamper-evident audit trail"Does the audit log record the human on whose behalf the agent acted?"
ObservabilitySeeing inside a run — traces, tool inputs/outputs, token and cost accounting"Can we see the exact prompt sent on turn 7 of a failed run?"
ServingRunning it for real users — streaming, concurrency, horizontal scale-out, deployment story"What happens to in-flight runs during a rolling deploy?"
MemoryKnowledge across sessions — long-term memory, stores, retrieval"Can memory be scoped per user rather than global?"
EvalsProving behaviour — agent evals, regression suites, trace-based scoring"Can we replay a recorded trace against a new prompt version?"

Nine areas is a default, not a law. A team building read-only research agents might shrink Security to three rows; a team handling payments will expand Identity and audit into a dozen. The point of naming areas first is coverage: without them, the matrix silently becomes a list of whatever features the loudest advocate already likes.

Step two: break areas into sub-rows — seeded from reality

A sub-row is one atomic, testable question. "Good state management" is not a sub-row — it is a mood. "Kill the process mid-run and the run resumes from the last completed step" is a sub-row: specific enough that a one-day spike can answer it, and specific enough that two engineers cannot both be right while disagreeing.

Where do sub-rows come from? This is the step most teams get wrong, so here is the rule: seed the rows from what your production users actually use — not from the candidates' feature pages. If you write rows by reading marketing sites, you end up scoring each harness on the features it chose to brag about, which is like judging the two flats on the photos each broker chose to send.

Instead, mine reality:

  • Real transcripts and traces. Pull a month of runs from the agents your teams already operate. Which capabilities actually fired? If every production agent uses human-in-the-loop interrupts daily, that is a heavyweight row. If nobody has ever launched a ten-agent swarm, that gets one polite row, not a section.
  • Support tickets and incident reports. Every past outage is a sub-row: "recovers when the model API times out mid-tool-call" earns its place because it once cost you a weekend. The failure-modes chapter is a ready-made quarry.
  • The requirements sheet. Every non-negotiable from bank-grade requirements becomes at least one row, usually several.

A healthy matrix for a serious platform decision lands between forty and eighty sub-rows. Fewer, and you are probably still at the mood level; many more, and you are cataloguing rather than deciding.

Tip

Write every sub-row as a question a spike could answer, and put the verb early: "Resumes after a process kill?", "Restricts a tool per-agent?", "Replays a recorded trace?". If you cannot imagine the test, the row is not finished.

Step three: score with evidence, not vibes

Every cell gets a colour, and the colour is capped by the evidence behind it. The evidence ladder has three rungs — and the bottom rung does not count:

  • Vibes — a blog post, a conference chat, a memory of a tweet. Worth writing down as a lead to investigate. Worth zero as a score.
  • Docs — the capability is explicitly documented by the maintainers, with an API you can point at. This earns amber at best: documented is a claim, not a fact. Docs drift, and "supported" sometimes means "there is an open issue about it".
  • Tested — someone on your team ran it, in a spike repo, and the artefact (script, trace, screenshot of the failure) is linked from the cell. Only tested evidence can turn a cell green — or, just as valuably, red.

The colour states then read at a glance: green means tested and it works; amber means partial, docs-only, or works-with-a-workaround; red means tested and it fails, or the capability simply does not exist; grey means not yet assessed — and grey is an honest colour, far better than a guessed green. A matrix with some grey cells and a plan is rigorous; a matrix that is all green after one afternoon is fiction.

Careful

The most dangerous cell in any matrix is a green that came from documentation. It looks identical to a real green, and it fails in month three of the build, when switching costs are at their peak. Enforce the rule mechanically: no repro artefact linked, no green.

Now do what the room would do. Change the Serving weight to 5 and watch whether the winner flips — that is a sensitivity check, and a decision that survives reasonable re-weighting is a safe one. Two things are worth noticing in the output. First, Aviary's "horizontal scale-out" cell scored zero not because Aviary cannot scale, but because the only evidence anyone had was a conference-corridor conversation. The method does not punish the harness; it punishes the unverified claim, and it prints exactly which cells need a spike before the score can be trusted. Second, Foundry leads even on the raw unweighted scores, so the weights are not what manufacture the win — what they do is anchor the decision to the areas this team's workload actually lives in, chiefly State, where Foundry's advantage is largest and the agreed weight is heaviest. The ordering still matters: those weights were set before scoring, and weights chosen after the scores are visible are just vibes wearing a spreadsheet.

Driving the room to a decision

A perfect matrix can still die in a bad meeting. Facilitation is part of the method, not an afterthought, and it rests on five habits:

  • Circulate first, meet second. The matrix goes out days before the meeting. The meeting is for challenges and the decision, not for reading a fifty-row table aloud.
  • Timebox per area. Ten minutes for Agent patterns, ten for State, and a visible clock. Timeboxes are not rudeness; they are fairness — without them, the first two areas get an hour and Identity and audit gets ninety seconds at the door.
  • Park the rabbit holes. When the room discovers a genuinely interesting tangent — "wait, could we write our own checkpointer?" — it goes to the parking lot, a visible list of deferred questions, each with an owner. Parked is not dismissed: the list is reviewed at the end, and items either become spikes or get consciously dropped.
  • Only evidence moves a colour. The single most powerful sentence in the room: "happy to change it — show me the repro." This one rule converts seniority contests into engineering. A challenge without evidence becomes a parked spike; a challenge with evidence changes the cell on the spot, gratefully.
  • One named decider, one written log. Consensus is lovely and rare. Agree up front who decides if the room splits — then record the outcome in a decision log: the choice, the scores it rested on, the dissent it overrode, and the concrete triggers that would reopen it.

A decision-log entry is short — that is what makes people actually write it:

decisions/2026-07-adopt-foundry.yaml
decision: Adopt Foundry for the payments-agent programme
date: 2026-07-02
options_considered: [Aviary, Foundry]
weighted_scores: {Aviary: 25, Foundry: 40}   # max 48; matrix v1.3 attached
decisive_evidence:
  - "Spike S-114: killed Foundry mid-run, resumed from step 9 (trace linked)"
  - "Spike S-117: Aviary run lost after process kill; no durable backend"
dissent:
  - "Aviary's time-to-first-agent (2 days vs 9) is real; prefer it for
     low-risk internal tools"
revisit_when:
  - "Aviary ships durable checkpoints (their issue #212)"
  - "Six months elapse, whichever comes first"

The log is what turns a decision into an asset. Without it, the argument reopens every time a new engineer joins or a vendor ships a shiny release, and it reopens from zero. With it, the conversation starts at "has a revisit trigger fired?" — a five-minute check instead of a five-week relitigation.

Note

The matrix is a snapshot, so date it and version it. Harnesses move fast: a red cell in January may be green by June. That is not a flaw in the method — it is the reason the decision log carries revisit triggers instead of pretending the answer is permanent.

In the wild

You will meet this artefact under many names: "evaluation matrix", "framework bake-off", "scorecard", "trade study", or an architecture decision record (ADR) with a comparison table inside. In a repository it usually lives as a spreadsheet link plus a decisions/ folder of markdown or YAML files; in a merge request you will see spikes named after cells — "spike: prove checkpoint resume on candidate B". When a big engineering blog announces "why we chose X", the credible ones publish the matrix and the spike repos; the less credible ones publish adjectives. And when an enterprise harness team asks you to review their comparison, your first question now writes itself: which of these greens were tested, and where are the artefacts?

Check yourself

1. A cell in the matrix says a harness supports durable checkpointing, citing the official documentation. Under the evidence ladder, what is the strongest colour that cell can hold?

Docs earn amber at best. Documentation drifts, and "supported" can hide caveats that only surface when you run it. Green requires a tested repro with a linkable artefact — and a docs-born green is the most dangerous cell in the matrix because it fails in month three, when switching is most expensive.

2. Why does the method insist on seeding sub-rows from production traces, incidents and requirements — rather than from the candidate harnesses' feature pages?

Rows written from marketing pages tilt the whole comparison toward the vendors' strengths — like judging two flats on the photos each broker chose to send. Seeding from real usage, real failures and real requirements makes the matrix measure your demand, not their supply.

3. In the review meeting, a senior engineer says a red cell is "definitely wrong — I'm sure it supports that". What does the method say happens next?

Only evidence moves a colour — "happy to change it, show me the repro." Confidence without evidence becomes a parking-lot spike with an owner. This single rule is what converts a seniority contest into engineering, and it protects the senior engineer too: if they are right, the spike proves it permanently.

4. Six months after adopting a harness, a new team member argues the choice was wrong. What does the decision log change about that conversation?

The log records the scores, the dissent and the concrete conditions that would reopen the decision. If a trigger has fired — say, the losing harness shipped the missing capability — the question reopens legitimately and quickly. If not, the reasoning is right there to read. Either way, nobody argues from memory.

5. In the Aviary-vs-Foundry example, why did Aviary score zero on horizontal scale-out even though nobody proved it cannot scale?

The zero punishes the unverified claim, not the harness. The scorer prints exactly which cells were voided, turning each one into a to-do: run the spike, attach the artefact, and the points come back — as a green or an honest red.

Key takeaways

  • A capability matrix is areas → sub-rows → evidence-backed colours: nine areas (agent patterns, tools, state, security, identity/audit, observability, serving, memory, evals) broken into forty to eighty atomic, testable questions.
  • Seed the sub-rows from what production users actually use — real traces, real incidents, real requirements — never from the candidates' feature pages.
  • The evidence ladder caps every colour: vibes score zero, docs earn amber at best, and only a tested repro with a linkable artefact turns a cell green or red. Grey ("not yet assessed") is an honest colour.
  • Agree the area weights before scoring, then sensitivity-check the winner by nudging them — a decision that flips under reasonable re-weighting is not a decision yet.
  • Facilitate deliberately: circulate the matrix first, timebox each area, park rabbit holes visibly with owners, and let only evidence change a colour.
  • End with one named decider and a written decision log — choice, scores, dissent, revisit triggers — so the decision compounds instead of reopening from zero every quarter.
