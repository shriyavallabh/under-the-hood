Async: why agent code says await

Every agent codebase is littered with the words async and await. By the end of this chapter they will read like plain English — because they describe something you have watched a good cook do a thousand times.

~35 minChapter 40 of 133
The story

Watch the lone chef at a busy dhaba on a Sunday morning. Three orders come in at once: dal, rice, and an omelette. A bad chef would cook them one at a time — put the rice on, then stand there staring at the pot for ten minutes while it boils, then start the dal. The queue outside would riot.

The real chef does something smarter. Rice goes on the flame — and the moment the water is doing the work, she turns away and starts chopping onions for the omelette. Dal goes into the pressure cooker — and while it hisses, she cracks the eggs. When the cooker whistles, she comes back to it, exactly where she left off. One pair of hands, three dishes in flight, and not one second spent watching water boil.

Notice what she is not doing: she has not hired two more chefs, and she is never doing two things with her hands at the same instant. She is simply refusing to be idle while she waits. That refusal has a name in Python: async. The pot of boiling water is a network call to an LLM. The chef is the event loop. And the moment she says "this pot needs ten minutes on its own — I'm free until it whistles"? That is the word await.

You already know a function that can pause

The previous chapter gave you the strangest idea in Python so far: a generator is a function that can pause mid-execution at a yield, hand something out, and later resume from exactly that line with all its local variables intact. A normal function is a sprint from top to bottom; a generator is a walk with benches along the way. If that idea has settled in, this chapter is downhill: an async function is the same pausable machinery, plus a scheduler that decides who runs while somebody is sitting on a bench. Under the hood, Python's async system was literally built out of generator plumbing. You are not learning a new trick — you are meeting the same trick wearing a chef's apron.

Here is the whole chapter in one sentence: async lets one Python program keep many slow operations in flight at once, by pausing whichever function is waiting and running another one in the meantime. Everything else is vocabulary and syntax.

Two kinds of slow: waiting versus working

Before the syntax, one distinction that decides whether async helps you at all. Programs are slow for two very different reasons.

A task is I/O-bound when the computer spends its time waiting on the outside world — a network reply, a disk read, a database answer. I/O stands for input/output, and the defining feature is that your CPU is doing essentially nothing during the wait. This is the pot of rice: once it is on the flame, standing next to it adds no value.

A task is CPU-bound when the computer spends its time actually computing — crunching a spreadsheet of a million rows, resizing images, training a model. This is chopping onions: it needs the chef's hands the whole time, and no amount of clever scheduling makes one pair of hands chop faster.

Async helps with waiting, not with working. It does not add chefs; it stops the chef standing idle. Now look at where the time in an LLM call actually goes — the numbers below are round and illustrative, but the proportions are honest:

This is why LLM calls are the textbook case for async. Your machine sends a few kilobytes of JSON, then sits on its hands for two, ten, sometimes thirty seconds while distant GPUs do the heavy lifting. Make three independent model calls one at a time and you are the bad chef staring at the pot — thirty seconds of doing nothing, in instalments.

The event loop is the chef

So who decides which paused function resumes, and when? A small piece of machinery called the event loop — a scheduler that keeps a list of paused functions, watches for their waits to finish (a network reply arriving, a timer expiring), and resumes whichever one is ready. It is the chef's brain: one worker, a mental list of dishes in flight, and a rule — never stand idle while a pot is boiling.

The functions the event loop juggles are called coroutines — functions declared with async def that can pause at every await and resume later, exactly like a generator pausing at yield. Step through one Sunday-morning service to see the whole system move:

Note

Async is concurrency, not parallelism. There is still exactly one chef — one thread, doing one thing at any instant. The speed-up comes entirely from overlapping the waiting. That is also why async does nothing for CPU-bound work: if all three dishes were pure chopping, one pair of hands would take just as long in any order. (For genuinely parallel CPU-bound computation Python offers processes — multiprocessing, which sidesteps the GIL — a different tool for a different problem, and rarely needed in agent code.)

The syntax: three rules and you can read anything

Here is the smallest complete async program, in the shape you will see in real repositories:

smallest_async.py
import asyncio

async def main():
    print("kitchen open")
    await asyncio.sleep(1)     # a stand-in for any slow wait
    print("kitchen closed")

asyncio.run(main())            # opens the kitchen: starts the event loop

Three rules govern everything you just read:

Rule 1 — async def declares, it does not hurry. Writing async def main(): makes main a coroutine function. Nothing about it is faster; it has simply been given permission to pause.

Rule 2 — calling it does not run it. This is the rule that trips everyone, so prove it to yourself right now. Just as calling a generator function hands you a generator object without running any code, calling a coroutine function hands you a coroutine object — a dish ticket, not a dish. Only await (or the event loop) actually cooks it:

Rule 3 — await only lives inside async def, and someone must start the loop. A plain script cannot pause at top level, so the entry point is always the one synchronous line asyncio.run(main()) — it builds the event loop, runs main to completion, and shuts the kitchen. Every async program you will ever read is a pyramid: one asyncio.run at the bottom, async def functions awaiting each other above it.

Tip

Sharp-eyed readers will notice the runnable box above awaited at top level with no asyncio.run in sight. That works here because this page — like a Jupyter notebook — already has an event loop running, so top-level await is allowed. In a plain .py file it is a SyntaxError. If you have ever pasted agent code into a notebook and it worked, then failed as a script: this is why.

asyncio.gather: the whole point in one function

One coroutine awaiting one call gains you nothing — a chef with a single order might as well watch the pot. The payoff arrives when several independent waits can overlap, and the tool for that is asyncio.gather — give it several coroutines and it starts them all, overlaps their waits, and returns the results in the order you passed them in (not the order they finished).

Why LLM calls are async's favourite customer

Every property of a model call is the property async was invented for. The waits are enormous — seconds, not milliseconds. The calls are often independent — judging five candidate answers, embedding a hundred chunks for retrieval, fanning a task out to subagents — so they can genuinely overlap. And agent servers must serve many users at once: while user A's request waits on a model, the same event loop serves users B and C. This is why the ecosystem went async from top to bottom.

Here is what the pattern looks like with a real SDK instead of a fake — the same fan-out shape, dressed for production. Click the annotated lines:

clause_review.py — three independent model calls, concurrently

from anthropic import AsyncAnthropic

Identical setup to the sync client from your first API call — it reads the API key from the environment. The client object is created synchronously; only its network methods are async.

A coroutine function: one question in, one answer out. Small async functions like this are the bricks agent codebases are built from.

The await marks the spot where this coroutine parks — the whole network round-trip and the model's thinking happen "off screen" while the event loop runs other work. The method and arguments are exactly the ones you already know; only the word await is new.

After the await resolves, the coroutine resumes here with the response in hand and returns plain text. From the inside, an async function feels exactly like a normal one — do a thing, get a result, carry on.
async def main():
questions = [
"Summarise this contract clause in one line: ...",
"List the risks hiding in this clause: ...",
"Suggest a friendlier rewording: ...",
]
answers = await asyncio.gather(*(ask(q) for q in questions))
The fan-out. The generator expression builds three coroutine objects — three tickets — and the * unpacks them as separate arguments. gather starts all three; total time is the slowest call, not the sum. Results come back in question order, so the zip below is safe.
for q, a in zip(questions, answers):
print(q[:40], "->", a[:60])
asyncio.run(main())
The single synchronous line at the bottom of the pyramid: build the event loop, run main to completion, close the kitchen. In a web server or a notebook a loop already exists, so you await instead — but in a script, this line is the on switch.

Reading async code without fear

Now the promise in this chapter's title. When you open a real agent file and it bristles with async, use these three habits.

Habit 1: mentally delete the keywords. Here is the secret nobody tells you — async code means exactly the same thing as the code with async and await deleted. Same order of steps, same values, same top-to-bottom logic; the keywords change only the scheduling — who else may run during the waits. So on a first read, ignore them and follow the logic. On a second pass, read each await as a pin saying "slow wait here — the loop serves others during this line".

Habit 2: find the pyramid's base. Somewhere there is one asyncio.run(...) — or a framework (a web server, an MCP runtime) playing that role invisibly. Everything above it is coroutines awaiting coroutines. Once you spot the base, the file has a shape.

Habit 3: know the three red flags. Nearly every async bug you will ever meet in an agent log is one of these:

Careful

The missing await. result = ask(question) without await gives you a coroutine object, not an answer — a dish ticket stapled to the order board, never cooked. Python whispers RuntimeWarning: coroutine 'ask' was never awaited in the logs and your "result" breaks downstream in confusing ways. The blocking call. time.sleep(5) — or any slow, plain synchronous call — inside an async function is the chef staring at the pot: it has no await, so the loop cannot switch away, and every coroutine in the program freezes for those five seconds. The async-aware twin (await asyncio.sleep(5), an async HTTP client, an async database driver) is the fix. The CPU hog. Ten seconds of pure number-crunching inside a coroutine blocks the loop just as badly — async overlaps waiting, and chopping onions is not waiting.

In the wild

Open any modern agent repository and async is everywhere. Web frameworks like FastAPI expect async def route handlers, so an agent's API endpoints nearly always are. The MCP Python SDK runs servers on async plumbing, and tool functions are routinely declared async def. LangChain names the async twin of every method by prefixing an a — invoke / ainvoke, stream / astream — so when a colleague's merge request "switches to ainvoke", they are moving a call onto the event loop. Fan-out code — judging candidates, embedding batches, parallel subagents — is where you will spot asyncio.gather. And in the logs, RuntimeWarning: coroutine ... was never awaited plus the review comment "this blocks the event loop" are the two async ailments you can now diagnose on sight. The next chapter adds the last construct you need — async for, which is how token-by-token streaming arrives — and the agent loop you will build in Act V slots straight into this machinery.

Check yourself

1. A coroutine reaches await client.messages.create(...). What happens at that instant?

await is the chef turning away from the boiling pot: this coroutine is bookmarked and parked, the loop serves whoever else is ready, and the coroutine resumes with the result when the wait completes. One thread, no freezing — that is the whole trick.

2. You write answer = ask("Summarise this") where ask is an async def function — and forget the await. What lands in answer?

Calling a coroutine function only creates the coroutine, exactly as calling a generator function only creates the generator. Nothing runs until it is awaited — hence the log line "coroutine 'ask' was never awaited" and a downstream crash when code expects text and finds a ticket.

3. Three independent LLM calls take about 10 seconds each. Roughly how long does await asyncio.gather(call1, call2, call3) take?

gather starts all three and lets their waiting overlap, so the total is roughly the longest single call. It cannot beat the slowest call — async removes queued-up idleness, it does not make any individual pot boil faster.

4. Why is an LLM API call such a perfect fit for async?

A model call is extreme I/O-bound work: a sliver of local effort, then seconds of pure waiting. Async changes nothing on the provider's side — it just lets your one thread do other useful things during the wait.

5. A tired developer puts time.sleep(5) inside an async def tool. What actually happens?

time.sleep has no await, so the loop never gets a chance to switch away — the chef stands staring at the pot while every other dish burns. Blocking calls inside async code are the classic "our agent server went unresponsive" bug; the fix is the async twin, await asyncio.sleep(5).

Key takeaways

  • Async is the dhaba chef's rule: one worker, many dishes, never idle while a pot boils. Concurrency by overlapping waits — not parallelism, and no extra chefs.
  • It only pays off for I/O-bound work — waiting on networks, disks, and above all LLM calls, which are seconds of idle waiting wrapped around a sliver of local work. CPU-bound chopping gains nothing.
  • async def makes a pausable coroutine (generator machinery in an apron); calling it makes a ticket, only await cooks it; asyncio.run(main()) is the one synchronous line that opens the kitchen.
  • asyncio.gather is the payoff: start several independent calls, overlap their waits, get results in order — total time tracks the slowest call, not the sum.
  • To read async code fearlessly: mentally delete the keywords and the logic reads as ordinary top-to-bottom code; each await is just a pin marking "slow wait here, others may run".
  • The three bugs to spot in the wild: a missing await (ticket, never cooked), a blocking call in a coroutine (chef staring at the pot, whole loop frozen), and CPU-heavy work hogging the loop.
