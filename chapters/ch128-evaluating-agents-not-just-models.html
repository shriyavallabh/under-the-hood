Evaluating agents, not just models

You tweaked a prompt and it "feels" better. Feelings are not evidence. Evals are unit tests for behaviour you cannot assert with == - and judging a whole agent is harder than judging one answer.

~35 minChapter 128 of 133
The story

A restaurant hires a new chef and the owner tastes one dish. "Delicious - hired." For a month the reviews are fine, then they slide. What happened? The chef was brilliant at the dish the owner tasted and mediocre at the other forty on the menu, which nobody ever checked. The owner made a decision from a sample of one and a gut feeling, and the whole kitchen paid for it.

You do this to your agent every time you change a prompt and eyeball a single reply. It looks better, so you ship it. But that one tweak might have quietly broken five cases you did not think to try. There is no compiler error for "the agent is now worse at refunds," and no test that == can write, because the output is different every run. The discipline that replaces the owner's single taste-test is evaluation: a menu of cases, run every time, scored the same way, so "it feels better" becomes "it went from 71 to 88 on the suite - and here are the three cases that regressed."

What an eval actually is

An evaluation - "eval" for short - is scoring your system's outputs against expectations over a fixed dataset. It is a test, but for probabilistic behaviour: instead of asserting one exact value, you run many cases and measure how well the outputs match what you wanted. Wire that suite into your pipeline and a change that drops the score can block a merge, exactly like a failing unit test - a habit you already met in the pipelines chapter.

The first big distinction decides how hard your job is.

Why judging an agent is harder than judging a model

Model-eval scores one input to one output: was this answer right, was this label correct. You can grade it with an exact match or a reference. Agent-eval scores a whole trajectory - the full path the agent took through the loop. Did it choose the right tool? In the right order? Did it notice a tool error and recover, or barrel on? Did it reach the goal at all? The final answer can look fine while the path was a disaster - three wasted tool calls, a wrong turn it happened to recover from. And because the agent is stochastic, the same input can take a different path next run. Judging that is genuinely harder, which is why - honestly, as of mid-2026 - agent-trajectory eval tooling is still maturing, and several platforms that began with model-eval are only now extending toward full agent evaluation.

How you grade, when there is no single right answer

Evaluators come in a ladder of sophistication. Exact-match and heuristics work when there is a crisp answer (a number, a label, a required substring). Reference-based scoring compares against a known-good answer with a similarity measure. And LLM-as-judge hands the output to a model with a rubric - "score 0 to 1 for whether this answer is grounded, polite, and complete" - which scales to fuzzy, open-ended replies that no == could grade. Human evaluation remains the gold standard, but it is slow and costly, so you use it to calibrate the cheaper judges, not to grade every run.

RAG has its own metrics

When your agent retrieves documents and answers from them, the failure modes are specific, so the metrics are too. A popular open toolkit (Ragas) provides the standard ones - verify the exact names against current docs, but the shapes are stable: faithfulness (is the answer grounded in the retrieved context, or hallucinated?), answer relevance (does it actually address the question?), and context precision / recall (did retrieval fetch the right material, and enough of it?). Faithfulness is the one people reach for first, because it is the direct hallucination alarm: a fluent, confident answer that the retrieved documents do not support scores low, and that low score is what saves you.

The harness that holds it together

Around the loop sits an experiment-tracking platform (an MLflow-style tool): it logs each run with its parameters and metrics, keeps a leaderboard so you can compare versions, and plugs into your pipeline so a regression blocks the merge. A strategic pattern many teams land on is one platform for both experiment tracking and evaluation, with pluggable metric libraries - so a plain accuracy eval and a RAG-metrics eval (the Ragas-style scores) live and compare in the same place, instead of two disconnected tools. One home for "how good is it, across every version we have tried."

In the wild

You will see an evals/ folder with a dataset and a runner, an eval job wired into CI so a merge request shows a score delta, and review comments like "faithfulness dropped 8 points - what changed in retrieval?" You will meet MLflow runs on a leaderboard and LLM-as-judge prompts checked into the repo. When a team at a large bank debates "is model-eval enough, or do we need full agent-eval?", they are living this chapter: grading the final answer is easy, grading the whole trajectory is the frontier.

Check yourself

1. Why is "I changed the prompt and the reply looks better" not enough to ship?

Like the owner tasting one dish, a single reply hides regressions across the rest of the menu. An eval runs many cases, scored the same way, so improvements and regressions both show up.

2. What makes agent-eval harder than model-eval?

Model-eval grades one input to one output. Agent-eval must judge the entire path through the loop, which can differ run to run and can be wrong even when the final line looks right.

3. What does the RAG metric "faithfulness" measure?

Faithfulness is the direct hallucination alarm: a confident answer the retrieved documents do not support scores low, flagging that the model invented it.

4. What is an "LLM-as-judge"?

LLM-as-judge scores fuzzy, open-ended outputs that == cannot grade. It is cheap and scalable, but because the judge can be wrong, you calibrate it against a sample of human judgements.

Key takeaways

  • An eval is a test for probabilistic behaviour: run a fixed dataset, score the outputs, and let a drop block a merge - "it feels better" becomes a measured delta.
  • Model-eval scores one input to one output; agent-eval scores a whole trajectory (tool choices, order, recovery) and is much harder because the path matters and is stochastic.
  • Grade with a ladder: exact-match and heuristics for crisp answers, references for similarity, and LLM-as-judge for fuzzy ones - with human eval as the calibration gold standard.
  • RAG has its own metrics - faithfulness (grounded vs hallucinated), answer relevance, context precision/recall - and faithfulness is the first-reach hallucination alarm.
  • An eval harness is just a golden set, an agent, a scorer, and a loop - you ran one - wrapped by experiment tracking that leaderboards versions.
  • A strong pattern is one platform for experiment tracking and evaluation with pluggable metric libraries, so plain and RAG-style evals compare in one place.
