LLMs vs deterministic models

Your colleagues' models live in PKL files and give the same number every single time; your models talk. Same word, totally different animals — this chapter is the map between them, plus the exact sentences to say when a meeting muddles the two.

~25 minChapter 31 of 123
The story

The office lobby has two ways to get a coffee. On the left, a vending machine: press B4 and exactly the same cup drops out — same volume, same temperature, same price, in nine seconds, ten thousand times in a row. If it breaks, everyone knows within a minute, because nothing comes out. On the right, a barista: you can say "something warm, not too sweet, I've had a long day" and receive something wonderful that no button could produce. But order the same thing twice and you get two slightly different drinks. She is slower, costs more per cup, and every so often she confidently hands over a lavender oat-milk creation nobody asked for.

Nobody in that lobby argues about which one is "better". The queue that wants a predictable espresso, nine seconds per person, goes left. The person holding a vague craving goes right. And the smartest order in the building uses both: the barista listens to your rambling, translates it into "that's a B4" — and the machine then makes it, identically, every time.

Hold that lobby in your mind. It is this entire chapter.

One word, two completely different animals

You have now met both animals. In what a model actually is you learned that a model is a function whose behaviour was learned from data rather than typed by a programmer. In the classical toolkit you met sklearn and XGBoost — stacks of tiny learned if-statements that score rows of numbers — and in PKL files you saw how those get pickled into a jar and shipped. Then transformers and tokens, embeddings, and context windows introduced the other creature: a giant next-token predictor that reads and writes language.

Both get called "model" in meetings, and that one overloaded word causes real, expensive confusion. So let us make the split precise. A deterministic model is one where the same input produces the same output, every single time, forever — the vending machine. XGBoost, logistic regression, a decision tree: at prediction time these are fixed arithmetic. An LLM is stochastic — it contains randomness by design: it predicts a probability for every possible next token and then samples one, so the same prompt can legitimately come back worded differently. The barista.

That one design difference fans out into five practical ones. Here they are at a glance; the rest of the chapter walks each row.

Determinism and reproducibility: the replay test

Here is the test a bank's auditors actually run: take a decision the system made last March, feed the same inputs in again, and check you get the same answer. A gradient-boosted model passes trivially — it is a frozen bundle of numbers in a PKL file, versioned like any artifact, and prediction is pure arithmetic. Same row in, same score out, this year, next year, in court if necessary.

An LLM does not naturally pass it. Sampling is controlled by a dial called temperature — high means pick adventurously among likely next tokens; 0 means always pick the single likeliest one. So temperature 0 makes it deterministic? Mostly — but do not promise this in a meeting. Providers do not guarantee bit-identical outputs even at temperature 0 (floating-point arithmetic and request batching on their servers can nudge results), and the hosted model itself gets updated under you. Temperature 0 tames the randomness; it does not turn the barista into a vending machine.

Explainability: "why did you decline me?"

Banking regulation has a blunt requirement hiding behind polite language: when a system declines a customer, someone must be able to say which factors drove that specific decision. This property is called explainability — pointing at the real inputs that moved the real output, per decision, not a story invented afterwards.

Classical models are good at this. A gradient-boosted scorer can emit reason codes — the top factors behind each individual score, such as "EMI above 40% of income; employment history under 3 years". These come from the model's actual mechanism (which features moved the score, and by how much), and compliance teams have accepted them across decades of scorecard history.

An LLM cannot do this honestly. Its "decision" is smeared across billions of learned weights, with no per-decision ledger of which ones mattered. Here is the trap that catches smart people: you can ask an LLM "why did you say that?" and it will answer beautifully. But recall from the transformer chapter what it is actually doing — predicting plausible next tokens. The explanation is generated the same way the answer was: a plausible narrative about the answer, not a trace of it. Researchers call this post-hoc rationalisation. It reads like an audit trail and is nothing of the kind.

Careful

"The model explained its reasoning" means two entirely different things depending on the animal. For the scorer it means mechanism: these inputs moved the number. For the LLM it means prose: a fluent story that may or may not match what happened inside. Regulators accept the first. Treat the second as a draft written by a persuasive intern.

Cost and latency: the overnight-batch test

Whenever someone proposes "let's just use the LLM for that", run the overnight-batch test in your head. A mid-sized bank re-scores millions of things per night: card transactions for fraud, accounts for limit reviews, portfolios for risk. Inference — running a trained model to get an answer, as opposed to training it — has wildly different economics for the two animals.

Do the arithmetic once and you will never forget it. Ten million decisions at a millisecond each is under three hours on one modest machine, for pennies of electricity. Ten million LLM calls at two seconds each is over two hundred days of sequential compute — you can parallelise the time, but not the bill, because every call is metered. These are illustrative round numbers, but the ratio is the durable lesson: per decision, the two animals differ by around a thousand times in both time and money. This is why "why don't we replace the fraud model with GPT?" gets the reaction it gets from the data science team. Not defensiveness — arithmetic.

Failure modes: how each animal breaks

Both animals fail — but in opposite styles, and each style needs its own monitoring.

The classical model fails like a machine. Its big enemy is drift — the world changes while the training data stays frozen, so the model quietly gets worse: a fraud model trained before a new scam pattern simply does not know it exists. It is also brittle at the edges — a category it never saw in training gets a bad number without complaint. But notice the shape: these failures are quiet, statistical, and measurable. Dashboards catch them — accuracy this month versus last, score distributions shifting — which is exactly what your colleagues' monitoring notebooks do.

The LLM fails like a person. Its signature failure is hallucination — stating something false with total fluency and confidence; not a lie, since there is no intent, just next-token machinery producing plausible text that happens to be wrong. Add format drift (you asked for JSON, it returned JSON-with-a-friendly-preamble), sensitivity to phrasing, and the nondeterminism you watched above, which makes bugs maddening to reproduce. The dangerous part is the shape: LLM failures arrive fluent and confident, indistinguishable at a glance from successes. No dashboard of averages catches one confidently invented account number.

One sentence to keep: the classical model fails quietly and measurably; the LLM fails fluently and confidently. Quiet failures need statistical monitoring; confident failures need verification — checking each output against a source of truth before anything downstream trusts it.

So when should a bank use which?

Three questions sort almost every case. First: is the input structured numbers or language? Columns of amounts and ratios are the scorer's native food; letters, emails, transcripts, and contracts are the LLM's. Second: is this decision repeated at scale? Millions of near-identical decisions per day point at the vending machine; a handful of bespoke syntheses point at the barista. Third: will someone have to explain each individual decision later — to a customer, an auditor, a regulator? If yes, the deciding step must be the animal that produces real reason codes.

Credit scoring, fraud detection, limit setting: structured inputs, massive repetition, regulated explanations — deterministic model, full stop. Summarising a complaint thread, drafting a briefing, answering "what does this clause mean?": language in, language out, low volume, a human reads the result — LLM territory.

But the answer that wins in production — and in meetings — is usually both. A bank's most valuable information arrives as language wrapped around numbers: a loan application letter, a scanned statement, an income document. The LLM is the first system in history that can reliably read that wrapping. So let it do exactly that job, and only that job: the LLM reads the document into clean structured fields, and the deterministic model scores the fields and makes the decision. How to say it in a meeting

You will meet this confusion in real conference rooms, usually within a week of someone seeing an impressive demo. Here is language that holds up — accurate enough for the data science team, plain enough for everyone else.

When someone saysYou can say
"Why not just use the LLM for credit scoring?""Scoring is a repeated, regulated decision on structured data — we need the identical answer on replay and a reason code for every decision. That is the classical model's home turf. The LLM earns its keep reading the documents that feed the scorer."
"But the LLM got the demo case right.""A demo is one run; production is millions of runs a night. Per decision the two differ by roughly a thousand times in cost and latency — let's price it per decision before we decide."
"Which model is more accurate?""They sit different exams. The scorer is graded on labelled history; the LLM is graded on extraction quality and hallucination rate. Let's measure each on its own exam instead of comparing across them."
"Can't we get the best of both?""Yes — that is the hybrid. The LLM reads the paperwork into clean fields; the deterministic model scores them and carries the audit trail. Language at the edges, determinism in the middle."

And if you only get one sentence before the meeting moves on, spend it on the three sorting questions: is the input numbers or language, is the decision repeated at scale, and who has to explain each decision afterwards? Whoever asks those three questions out loud usually ends up owning the architecture discussion — because they are the questions both camps recognise as the real ones.

In the wild

You will see the two animals living side by side in a bank's repositories. The data science side has a models/ folder of .pkl files, feature pipelines, and retraining notebooks; the platform side has a prompts/ folder, an LLM gateway config, and merge requests saying "set temperature to 0 for the extraction step" or "add schema validation on the LLM output" — both of which you can now translate: taming nondeterminism, and guarding the hallucination failure mode. Listen for the word "model" in stand-ups too: to a data scientist it usually means the PKL; to the platform team it means the LLM endpoint. And when the risk office says "model governance", they mean the decades-old validation regime built for deterministic scorecards — which is precisely why putting an LLM in the regulated deciding seat, rather than the reading seat, sets off every alarm they have.

Check yourself

1. A regulator asks why one specific applicant was declined last March. Which setup answers cleanly?

The scorer's reasons come from its actual mechanism and its replay is exact. An LLM's "explanation" is generated the same way its answer was — plausible next tokens — so it is a narrative about the decision, not a trace of it.

2. Why can the same prompt to an LLM come back worded differently on two runs?

LLMs are stochastic by design: they predict probabilities for every possible next token and sample one. Temperature 0 tames this by always taking the likeliest token — but providers still do not guarantee bit-identical replay.

3. The bank needs to re-score 10 million card transactions for fraud tonight. What is the deciding factor?

Structured inputs, massive repetition: a classical scorer does the batch in hours for pennies, while millions of metered, seconds-long LLM calls blow both the clock and the budget. The gap is arithmetic, not opinion.

4. In the hybrid pattern, what is the correct division of labour?

Language at the edges, determinism in the middle: the LLM does the one job classical ML never could — reading messy language — while the regulated, repeated, must-be-explainable decision stays on the replayable animal.

5. "The classical model fails quietly; the LLM fails confidently." What does that mean for monitoring?

Drift shows up as shifting distributions and sliding accuracy — statistics catch it. A hallucination is a single fluent, confident, wrong output that looks exactly like a success, so it must be caught by checking the output itself before anything downstream trusts it.

Key takeaways

  • "Model" names two different animals: a deterministic function that scores structured numbers (XGBoost and friends, shipped as PKL files) and a stochastic next-token predictor of language (the LLM). Never let the shared word blur them.
  • Deterministic models replay exactly and emit per-decision reason codes from their real mechanism — which is why regulated, repeated decisions like credit and fraud stay on them.
  • LLMs sample: same prompt, different words. Temperature 0 tames the randomness but guarantees neither bit-identical replay nor explainability — an LLM's "why" is a plausible story, not an audit trail.
  • Per decision, the two animals differ by roughly a thousand times in cost and latency; the overnight-batch test kills most "just use the LLM for scoring" ideas on arithmetic alone.
  • They fail in opposite styles — classical models drift quietly and measurably, LLMs hallucinate fluently and confidently — so each needs its own kind of monitoring.
  • The winning production pattern is the hybrid: the LLM reads messy documents into clean fields and drafts the outgoing letter; the deterministic model scores and decides in between. Language at the edges, determinism in the middle.
  • In a meeting, sort any proposal with three questions: numbers or language? repeated at scale? who explains each decision later? Then you are ready for the model landscape.
