Context engineering

Chapter 48's survival tricks, promoted to a named discipline — the one that lets Claude Code work for days inside a window that fills up in hours.

The story

Two trekkers start up the same Himalayan trail. The first packs everything that might conceivably matter: the full guidebook, maps for the whole region, a week of food, spare everything. Eighteen kilos. By noon he is exhausted, and when the weather turns he cannot find the rain cover — it is buried under things he will never use.

The second trekker is a working guide, and her pack is small. She photocopied only today's pages of the guidebook — tomorrow's pages wait at the next lodge. When the route looks doubtful, she sends a porter to scout the pass; he walks four hours and returns with one sentence: "Snow above the ridge, take the east fork." Wrappers leave her pack the moment the food is eaten. And every evening she rewrites the day into three lines in a small diary — then burns her scratch notes.

She is not carrying less knowledge. She is carrying references, summaries, and exactly today's payload — because on a mountain, weight is a budget and every gram must earn its place. Her pack is a context window. Her habits have a name: context engineering.

The trick that earned a name

First, the promised recap. In memory and the context problem you learned that an LLM is stateless — the history list your loop re-sends every turn is the agent's entire world. That list grows until it hits the context window, and harnesses survive with three moves: truncation (chop the oldest), sliding windows (keep the last N, pin the important bits), and compaction (summarise the middle, keep recent turns verbatim). That chapter taught survival — how not to crash.

This chapter is what the field built on top of survival. Around 2025 the practice acquired a name, a manifesto, and a playbook. Context engineering is the discipline of curating, at every turn, the set of tokens the model sees — what enters the window, what gets summarised, what gets evicted, what never enters at all. The anchor text is Anthropic's engineering post "Effective context engineering for AI agents", which frames it as the successor to prompt engineering and gives the discipline its one-line creed: find the smallest possible set of high-signal tokens that makes the desired outcome likely.

Prompt engineering asks: how do I word one instruction well? Context engineering asks a logistics question: of everything that could go in front of the model this turn — system prompt, tool definitions, history, tool results, retrieved files, notes — what actually should?

The attention budget

Why does this deserve the word "engineering"? Because of a physical constraint. Inside a transformer (chapter 29, plainly), every token attends to every other token — the relationships to track grow roughly with the square of context length, and models are trained mostly on shorter sequences. The practical result is context rot — as the token count climbs, the model's ability to recall and use any one fact in the context measurably drops. You met its most famous symptom in chapter 48: "lost in the middle", where facts buried mid-transcript get recalled worse than facts at the edges.

So the window is not a warehouse. It is an attention budget — a finite pool of the model's focus, and every token you add makes a withdrawal, whether or not that token helps. A 190,000-token transcript that "still fits" in a 200,000-token window is a model paying attention to everything a little and to nothing well. Hence the discipline's creed: curate, not cram. Past a point, more context is less thinking.

That last tile is the whole problem. A serious task — refactor a subsystem, chase a bug across services — generates far more text than any window holds: files read, tests run, logs scanned, dead ends explored. Context engineering's job is to run a multi-day marathon of work through a fixed-size window.

The playbook, in five moves

The entire discipline, as practised inside Claude Code and its peers, comes down to five named moves. Flip each card until the definitions feel obvious, then we take them one at a time:

Move 1 — compaction, now with taste

You already know compaction's mechanics from chapter 48: summarise the old middle, keep the tail verbatim. The discipline adds the part that separates a good harness from a naive one: what the summary must keep. When Claude Code compacts — manually via /compact, or automatically as the window nears its limit — the summariser is instructed to preserve the load-bearing facts: architectural decisions taken, bugs found and not yet fixed, the current state of the task, the next steps. What goes first is bulk already spent: repeated tool outputs, files quoted in full, dead-end explorations.

The cost: a summary is lossy, and whatever the summariser judged unimportant is gone. That is why compaction is the last resort in the playbook — the remaining four moves all exist to postpone it.

Move 2 — tool-result pruning

Look at any long agent transcript and ask: which tokens are dead weight? Almost always, old tool results. The 2,000-line test log from this morning mattered enormously for one turn; forty turns later its lesson is extracted, but the log still sits in the window, spending attention on every call. Tool results are the safest eviction because they are re-fetchable: the world still contains that file, that log, that query result. So the harness replaces the payload with a stub — "output of read_file('tests.log'), pruned; re-run to fetch" — keeping the reference, dropping the bulk. Anthropic's post describes this tool-result clearing as the lightweight cousin of compaction: cheap, low-risk, the first thing to reach for.

Move 3 — just-in-time retrieval

The moves so far clean up a window after it fills. The sharper discipline is to stop filling it in the first place. Early LLM apps preloaded: gather everything relevant, stuff it in, then ask — you saw the industrial version in the RAG chapter, where documents are embedded ahead of time and injected at query time. Just-in-time retrieval is the agentic alternative: the context holds lightweight references — file paths, folder listings, saved queries — and the agent pulls actual payloads through tools only at the moment of need, one layer of detail at a time (that layering is progressive disclosure: the folder, then the file, then the section).

This is visibly how Claude Code treats your codebase. It does not ingest the repository at session start. It greps for a symbol, reads the three files that matter, often just the relevant line ranges — a senior engineer navigating by ls and search rather than memorising the repo. The reference ("there is a billing/ folder with a tax.py in it") costs tens of tokens; the payload costs tens of thousands, and is fetched only when it earns its place:

Mixing is normal: many production agents preload a small always-relevant core (the project memory file, a schema) and fetch everything else just in time.

Move 4 — sub-agent isolation

Some questions are token bombs. "Which of these 40 modules touches the payments flow?" is a legitimate question whose honest answer requires reading tens of thousands of tokens — almost all worthless the moment the answer is known. The engineered move, which you met mechanically in the subagents chapter, is isolation: spawn a helper agent whose context window starts empty, let it burn fifty thousand tokens exploring, and take back a one-paragraph answer. The exploration happened in a disposable window; only the distilled result — often one or two thousand tokens — enters the context that must survive the week. In Claude Code this is the Task tool dispatching a sub-agent; in deepagents, the built-in task tool. The porter walks four hours; the guide's pack gains one sentence.

Move 5 — structured note-taking

The final move concedes something the other four cannot fix: any token inside the window can be lost — pruned, slid off, or flattened by a summary. So the discipline keeps its most precious state outside the window, in files. The agent writes as it works — a todo list it re-reads and updates, a notes file of decisions and findings, a progress log — using ordinary file tools (deepagents made the pattern structural with its virtual filesystem; Claude Code simply writes real files in your repo). Notes on disk cost zero attention until re-read, survive every compaction, and can be pulled back in — just in time — when needed. This is the guide's diary — and a note that survives the session is the beginning of long-term memory, exactly where the next chapter picks up.

Run the discipline yourself

Time to feel the physics. Below, the same 40-turn job runs twice against a deliberately tiny 2,000-token window (small makes the drama visible). The first run is the naive transcript from your ReAct loop — append everything, forever. The second adds three of this chapter's moves: tool-result pruning, compaction, and structured note-taking. As ever, the model and tools are scripted stand-ins; the context management is the real thing. Press Run.

Tip

Experiment before reading on. Set KEEP_RESULTS = 999 to switch pruning off and watch compaction fire earlier and more often. Shrink WINDOW to 1000 and see the engineered run compact repeatedly yet still finish. Every knob here has a direct twin in a production harness.

The two moves, line by line

The engineered run's whole advantage lives in about ten lines. Read them the way a harness author would:

engineered_context.py — the part that does the engineering
notes.append(f"- section {i}: key figure captured")
Structured note-taking. This list stands in for a file on disk — never counted by used(msgs), because it is not in the window. No prune or compact can touch it.
results = [m for m in msgs if m.startswith("TOOL RESULT")]
Find every tool payload still sitting in the window, oldest first. A list comprehension (ch8) doing triage.
for old in results[:-KEEP_RESULTS]:
Everything except the newest three is evicted. Recent results stay verbatim because the model is probably still using them — recency is the cheap heuristic real harnesses use too.
msgs[msgs.index(old)] = f"[result {n} pruned - re-read section {n} if needed]"
The prune itself: a 200-token payload becomes a 12-token stub that keeps the reference. Nothing is lost that cannot be re-fetched.
if used(msgs) > 0.8 * WINDOW:
The trigger sits at 80%, not 100%. Harnesses act early: the model needs room to write its reply, and context rot degrades quality long before the hard limit rejects the call.
head, tail = msgs[:2], msgs[-4:]
Pin the head (system prompt and original task — chapter 48's hard-won lesson about what must never fall off) and keep the freshest turns verbatim.
summary = (f"SUMMARY of turns 1-{i}: sections read in order; ...")
A string literal plays the summariser here. In Claude Code this is a real LLM call instructed on what to preserve: decisions, open bugs, current state, next steps. The quality of that prompt is the quality of the agent's memory.
msgs = head + [summary] + tail
The rebuilt window: pinned head, one summary, recent tail. To the stateless model, this tidy transcript simply is the world — same power, same danger as in chapter 48.

Step back and notice what you just did. In chapter 48 these were emergency responses to an overflowing list. Here they are policies, running every turn, tuned and cooperating: notes make compaction safe, and pruning postpones it. That shift — from coping to curating — is what earned the practice its name. The money follows the same curve: every token evicted is a token you stop re-paying on every subsequent call, the mechanism behind the token bill.

Note

None of these moves is free. Pruning bets the agent will not need the old payload; compaction is lossy and adds an LLM call; sub-agents cost latency and their own tokens; just-in-time retrieval trades preloading's predictability for exploration at runtime. Context engineering is choosing, deliberately, which costs to pay — because the failure it prevents, a concussed or crashed agent, is worse than every one of those prices.

In the wild

You will see this discipline daily once you know its face. In Claude Code: the context indicator ticking down, the "compacting conversation" pause, a /compact typed before a big task, sub-agents dispatched for open-ended searches, the agent grepping instead of reading whole folders. In framework code: deepagents ships the pattern as built-ins (write_todos, virtual file tools, subagents via its task tool), and LangChain-family stacks hang summarisation and trimming off middleware around the model call. In merge requests, "fetch it JIT instead of preloading", "the agent's notes file", or "summarise tool outputs over 5K tokens" are all this chapter. And when a teammate says "the agent got dumber after lunch", you now have a differential diagnosis: context rot versus a bad compaction — check the window, then check the summary.

Check yourself

1. What separates context engineering from prompt engineering?

Prompt engineering is writing; context engineering is logistics — the turn-by-turn decision of what the model sees at all. The system prompt is one item in the rucksack.

2. A fact sits in a transcript that is 190K tokens long, comfortably inside a 200K window. Why might the agent still miss it?

Nothing is dropped and nothing expires — the model attends to everything a little, and to nothing well. "Fits in the window" and "gets the model's attention" are different claims; the gap between them is why the discipline exists.

3. Why are old tool results the first tokens a harness evicts?

Eviction is safest when reversible. A pruned tool result can be re-run; a deleted user decision cannot. Keeping the stub ("this was read; re-run to fetch") preserves the map while dropping the territory.

4. Your agent must scan 40 modules to answer one question. What does sub-agent isolation buy you?

Cheaper models and parallelism are optional bonuses; the guaranteed win is contamination control. The scout's window dies with the scout — the main context gains a paragraph, not a library.

5. After an aggressive auto-compaction, which of these is guaranteed to survive intact?

Anything inside the window can be flattened into the summary. Files live outside it — untouchable by compaction, re-readable on demand. That is why disciplined harnesses write notes as they go.

Key takeaways

  • Context engineering is the named discipline of curating, every turn, the smallest set of high-signal tokens that gets the job done — the successor to prompt engineering, anchored by Anthropic's "effective context engineering" playbook.
  • The window is an attention budget, not a warehouse: every token spends focus, and past a point more context means worse thinking (context rot, lost-in-the-middle). Curate, do not cram.
  • Five moves make up the playbook: compaction (summarise old turns, keep decisions and open issues), tool-result pruning (stub out re-fetchable payloads first), just-in-time retrieval (carry references, fetch payloads when needed), sub-agent isolation (burn exploration tokens in a disposable window), and structured note-taking (state in files, outside the window).
  • Claude Code runs all five at once — grep-then-read navigation, Task-tool scouts, stale-result clearing, auto-compact near the limit, notes and todos on disk — which is how a days-long session survives a fixed window.
  • Every move has a cost (lossiness, re-fetches, extra calls); the discipline is choosing those costs deliberately because a crashed or concussed agent costs more.
  • Chapter 48 taught you to survive a full window; this chapter taught you to govern one. Notes that outlive the session are the bridge to what comes next: long-term memory.
