Temperature and friends

Every reply an LLM has ever given you was built one dice roll at a time. Temperature, top_p, max_tokens and stop are the four house rules of that dice game — and once you can see what each one actually does, the mystery knobs on every API call become tools you set on purpose.

The story

Your favourite ice-cream parlour has a Surprise Me counter. The kitchen keeps a popularity sheet — vanilla 50%, chocolate 30%, mango 12%, pistachio 7%, plus one experimental "roasted chilli" tub almost nobody orders — and the server picks your flavour by drawing from a raffle drum where popular flavours get more tickets.

On the counter sits a dial. Turned all the way down, the server ignores the drum and hands you the bestseller, every time. In the middle, tickets match the popularity sheet. Cranked up, extra tickets go in for the unpopular tubs — and one day in fifty, you get roasted chilli. A house rule is taped to the till: "never draw from flavours outside the top sellers". Your cup has a fixed size, so scooping stops when it is full, finished sundae or not. And the moment the scoop hits the wafer, everything stops.

That dial is temperature. The house rule is top_p. The cup is max_tokens. The wafer is a stop sequence. This chapter is that counter, in Python.

The machine you met last chapter is a dice game

In your first API call you sent a list of messages to a model and got text back — one request, one response, plus a usage receipt counting tokens in and out. What that chapter treated as a black box, this one opens up: the parameters you passed alongside your messages all act on one specific moment inside the model, and it is a moment you already understand.

Recall from the transformers chapter that an LLM does exactly one thing: given the text so far, it predicts the next token. The detail that matters today: it does not output a token. It outputs a score for every token it knows — all hundred-thousand-ish at once. "tea" scores high, "coffee" slightly lower, "gravel" deeply negative. Those raw scores are called logits — numbers meaning "how much the model likes each candidate". A bit of maths called softmax converts the scores into probabilities summing to 100%, turning the score sheet into a weighted dice. Then a separate, dumb-as-rocks piece of code called the sampler rolls that dice, picks one token, appends it, and the cycle repeats.

Every knob in this chapter lives in the gap between "scores are ready" and "dice gets rolled" — or in the loop around it. None of them make the model smarter or dumber. The kitchen never changes; only the picking rules do.

The vocabulary you need

Six terms carry the rest of the chapter. Flip until boring:

Temperature: reshaping the dice

The mechanism in one sentence: before the scores become probabilities, every score is divided by the temperature. That is genuinely all it is. Dividing by a small number widens the gaps between candidates, so after softmax the favourite hogs almost all the probability. Dividing by a large number squashes the scores together, so the underdogs get real chances. At temperature 0, providers skip the dice altogether and take the top score outright (dividing by zero is illegal, so zero means "greedy": no randomness requested).

Temperature reshapes preferences; it cannot invent better ones.

top_p: trimming the tail

Temperature has an awkward side effect. Raise it for livelier writing and you do not just boost the interesting mid-ranked candidates — you also boost the genuine garbage in the far tail. In a hundred-thousand-token vocabulary, thousands of tokens are each individually near-impossible but collectively add up to a real slice of probability. High temperature widens exactly that slice, which is where word salad comes from.

top_p — also called nucleus sampling, "sampling from the core" — deletes the tail before the roll. Sort candidates by probability, keep candidates from the top until the kept pile covers top_p of the total (0.9 means "the top 90% worth"), throw away the rest, rescale the survivors to 100%, roll among them only. It is the manager's rule at the counter: whatever the dial says, never draw a flavour outside the top sellers.

Notice what this is not: it is not "keep 90% of the tokens". When the model is confident, two candidates might cover 90% on their own; when it is torn, thirty survive. The cut adapts to how sure the model is — which is why top_p aged better than its simpler cousin top_k, which keeps a fixed count of k candidates no matter what.

Note

Temperature and top_p both shape the same dice, so providers advise tuning one or the other, not both — on Claude 4-generation models, sending both is actually rejected as an error. A common pairing you will see in configs: default temperature with top_p: 0.9, or a tuned temperature with top_p left alone at 1.0 (1.0 keeps everything — the rule switched off).

max_tokens: a budget, not a target

The loop runs once per token, and each turn costs real money — you saw the per-token billing in your first API call. max_tokens is the hard ceiling on how many turns the loop may take: the cup size at the counter. Scooping stops when the cup is full, mid-sundae if necessary — no warning, no graceful wrap-up. The reply is simply cut, often mid-sentence, and arrives with stop_reason: "max_tokens" so you can tell.

The word people trip over is target. Setting max_tokens=4000 does not make replies longer — the model never writes towards the number. A one-word answer under a 4,000-token budget is still one word; the budget only decides where the axe falls if the model rambles. That is why Anthropic makes it a required parameter: an unbounded token loop is an unbounded bill, so you must state your worst case. Two symptoms say it is set too low: replies ending mid-thought, and JSON that fails to parse because the closing braces got axed — check stop_reason before you blame the model.

stop: the tripwire

Sometimes you know the exact text that means "we are done" better than the model does. Stop sequences are strings you hand over with the request — stop_sequences=["\n\nCustomer:"] on Anthropic's API, stop on OpenAI's — and the loop watches for them on every token. The instant the text would contain one, generation halts, you get everything up to but not including the match, and stop_reason is "stop_sequence" (Anthropic even tells you which one fired). It is the wafer at the bottom of the cup: hit it, stop scooping.

Classic uses: simulating one side of a dialogue and cutting the model off the moment it starts writing the other speaker's turn; forcing single-line answers by stopping at "\n"; ending at a delimiter like "---" in a templated document. The tokens you never generate are tokens you never pay for — a stop sequence is the cheapest guardrail on the menu.

Careful

The knobs are quietly disappearing from the newest frontier models. Reasoning-first models (Claude's Opus 4.7 and later, Sonnet 5, and similar "thinking" models) have removed temperature, top_p and top_k — sending them returns a 400 error, because the model manages its own sampling while it reasons. On those models you steer with prompts and effort settings instead. Everything in this chapter still matters: max_tokens and stop sequences remain universal, most models in production today keep all four knobs, and understanding the dice roll is exactly what lets you read a migration note like "sampling parameters removed" without blinking.

So — 0, 0.7, or 1?

There is no magic number, but there are honest defaults. The question to ask is: does this task have one right answer, or many good ones?

SettingCharacter of the diceReach for it when
0No dice — top token always winsExtraction, classification, yes/no decisions, structured output, agent tool-calling — anything where two runs should ideally agree
0.2 – 0.5Sharpened, with a pulseSupport replies, summaries, business writing, code suggestions — reliable but not robotic
0.7 – 1.0The model's own preferences, more or less honouredChat, marketing copy, storytelling, "give me ten ideas" — you WANT different answers on every run
Above 1.0 (where allowed)Flattened — underdogs promotedBrainstorming and name generators, usually with top_p as a seatbelt. Expect occasional gravel

One production heuristic worth keeping: pipelines and agents run cold, humans-in-the-loop run warm. When a model's output feeds another program, surprise is a bug. When it feeds a person, a little surprise is the product.

The temperature=0 myth

Now for the caveat that catches nearly everyone. It is tempting to conclude: temperature 0 removes the dice, so the same prompt must give the same answer every time. In practice, it does not — not quite. Outputs become far more repeatable, but identical runs can still occasionally diverge, and the reasons live below the sampler.

Floating-point maths is not perfectly orderly on parallel hardware. GPUs add thousands of numbers in whatever order finishes fastest, and floating-point addition gives minutely different results in different orders. When two tokens are in a near-tie, a rounding wobble can flip which one is "top" — and greedy decoding faithfully picks the flipped winner, changing every token after it. Your request shares a batch with strangers. Providers process many requests together, and the batch's shape changes the exact arithmetic your request goes through — the same prompt at 9 a.m. and 9 p.m. may not run the same numbers. Add routing differences inside mixture-of-experts models and routine infrastructure updates, and bit-for-bit determinism quietly evaporates.

Say it precisely: temperature 0 removes the randomness you injected; it does not remove the noise of the machine. This is one more instance of the theme from LLMs versus deterministic code — treat model output as likely-but-not-guaranteed, even at zero. If a workflow breaks when two runs differ, do not lean on temperature alone: pin model versions, log outputs, validate structure, and design the surrounding code to tolerate variation. Some providers offer a seed parameter — treat it as "best effort", not a contract.

In the wild

You will meet these knobs everywhere. In framework code: LangChain's init_chat_model("...", temperature=0) opens almost every agent example, because agents run cold. In config files: a YAML block with temperature, max_tokens and stop under a model name is the giveaway that a file configures LLM calls. In incident channels: "the JSON came back truncated" resolves to stop_reason: max_tokens and a one-line budget bump nine times out of ten. And in merge requests: a diff that deletes temperature lines during a model upgrade is not sloppiness — it is a migration to a reasoning model that rejects the parameter.

Check yourself

1. You set temperature=0, run the identical prompt twice, and get two slightly different answers. What is the most likely explanation?

Temperature 0 means greedy decoding — no dice — but the scores themselves are computed on parallel hardware where arithmetic order varies with batching. A near-tie can flip, and one flipped token changes everything after it. Less variance, not zero variance.

2. You set max_tokens=2000 for a task whose answer is one sentence. What happens?

A budget, not a target. The model never sees max_tokens as a goal; the loop just refuses to run more than 2,000 turns. Short answers stay short, and billing follows actual output.

3. What does top_p=0.9 actually keep in the draw?

The nucleus rule cuts by cumulative probability mass, not count — so the surviving pile is tiny when the model is confident and larger when it is torn. That adaptive cut is the whole point.

4. You pass stop_sequences=["\n\nCustomer:"] and the model's text reaches that string. What comes back?

Stop sequences halt generation the moment the match would appear; the matched text is excluded from the reply, and stop_reason tells you the tripwire fired rather than a natural finish.

5. You are extracting invoice fields into JSON for an automated pipeline. Which dial?

When output feeds another program, surprise is a bug. Extraction has a correct answer, so you want the sharpest dice available — plus structure validation, because as question 1 showed, even zero is not a guarantee.

Key takeaways

  • An LLM builds every reply one token at a time: score all candidates, convert to a dice, roll, append, repeat. All four knobs act on that loop — none change the model itself.
  • Temperature reshapes the dice by dividing the scores: low sharpens towards the favourite, high flattens towards the underdogs, 0 skips the dice entirely (greedy decoding).
  • top_p trims the tail — keep the smallest top group covering p of the probability, delete the rest, rescale, roll. Tune temperature or top_p, not both.
  • max_tokens is a budget, not a target: it never lengthens a reply, only caps the loop (and the bill), cutting mid-sentence with stop_reason: "max_tokens".
  • Stop sequences are tripwires: generation halts before your string, the match is excluded, and tokens you never generate are tokens you never pay for.
  • Temperature 0 reduces variance but does not guarantee identical outputs — floating-point order, batching and infrastructure still wobble near-ties. Design pipelines to tolerate it.
  • Cold for pipelines and agents, warm for humans — and always read stop_reason. One of its values, tool_use, is the whole next chapter.
