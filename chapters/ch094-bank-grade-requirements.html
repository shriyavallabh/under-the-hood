Bank-grade requirements

"Enterprise-ready" is not a feature list. It is the ability to answer, for every action your agent ever took: who did it, with whose permission, and how do you know. You will leave with a checklist for real evaluations.

~30 minChapter 95 of 123
The story

On her first morning at a bank branch, a new employee named Meera receives three things before she may touch anything. First, an ID badge — it opens exactly the doors her role permits, and the vault door does not care how persuasive she is. Second, the logbook: every cash movement is written in ink, in a book with numbered pages, countersigned by a colleague. You cannot tear a page out without the gap showing. Third, a rule: any transfer above fifty thousand needs a supervisor's second signature. No exceptions.

Nobody thinks Meera is a criminal. The branch works this way because one day an inspector with the legal power to fine the bank or revoke its licence will walk in and ask: who did what, with whose permission, and how do you know? The badge, the logbook, and the second signature exist so that question always has an answer.

Now replace Meera with an AI agent. Every rule stays. That, in one paragraph, is what "bank-grade" means.

"Enterprise-ready" is a claim about evidence, not features

In the previous chapter you weighed LangGraph against its alternatives on engineering grounds. This chapter adds the other axis — the one that decides whether a harness is even allowed into the building when a regulator (a government body with legal power to inspect, fine, and shut down a business) is part of daily life. A demo has to work once, in front of friendly people. A regulated deployment has to survive a hostile question, years later, about something that happened at 2 a.m. on a Tuesday.

So here is the honest definition. A system is enterprise-ready when it can answer three questions for every action it has ever taken:

  1. Who did it? Not "the agent" — which human's authority was it acting under?
  2. With whose permission? Was that human entitled to the action, and if it was consequential, who approved it?
  3. How do you know? Where is the record, and can you prove nobody edited it afterwards?

Everything below is one of those three questions wearing work clothes: the badge, the logbook, the second signature, the building — and the replaceable teller.

The badge: on-behalf-of identity

The biggest difference between a hobby agent and a bank-grade one is whose name is on the badge. A service account is a machine identity — a login that belongs to software rather than a person. The tempting shortcut is one powerful service account that does everything for everyone. Enterprises call this the "god account" anti-pattern, and it fails the auditor's first question instantly: when the agent moves money, the record says the agent did it. Which human's authority was behind it? Unknowable.

The bank-grade answer is on-behalf-of identity: the agent acts as the user who asked, carrying that user's identity with every request, so it can never do anything the user themselves could not. When Asha from operations asks for a report, the agent queries the database as Asha — if her role cannot see salary columns, neither can her agent. In practice this runs on delegation tokens — the OAuth family even has a flow named "on-behalf-of" — carrying the user's signed identity downstream.

Identity answers "who is asking". An entitlement answers "what may this person do" — the machine-readable version of Meera's badge opening some doors and not others. Entitlements are enforced at the tool boundary, in code — never in the prompt. You built that boundary yourself in the ReAct chapter: the model can only reach functions in the TOOLS dict your loop is willing to run. A system-prompt sentence saying "please do not access payroll" is a request to a text generator; an entitlement lookup that refuses the call is a wall — the guardrails chapter's rule that prompts persuade, code enforces. Regulators only accept walls.

Careful

Watch for the quiet version of the god account: users are authenticated at the front door, but every tool underneath shares one privileged database credential. If any layer widens to "the agent can do everything", the badge above it is decoration. MCP security has the same rule for tool servers: the server enforces, the client asks.

The logbook: an immutable audit trail

You already keep logs — the observability chapter was all about traces and spans for debugging. An audit trail is a different animal with a different customer. Debug logs serve engineers; they get rotated and deleted when disk fills up. An audit trail serves the inspector: an append-only record of every consequential event, kept for years, that can prove it has not been edited.

Immutable means write-once: records can be added, never changed or removed. Two mechanisms make that credible. Storage-level: WORM storage ("write once, read many" — storage that refuses edits, like the numbered pages of Meera's logbook). Cryptographic: a hash chain, where each record includes a fingerprint of the record before it, so editing any old entry breaks every fingerprint after it — the gap always shows. You will build one in Python a few scrolls down.

What goes in the book matters as much as the binding. A defensible record captures who asked, what the model was shown, what it proposed, what the entitlement check decided, who approved, and what actually executed. Note proposed — bank-grade systems audit intent even when it is blocked. "The agent tried to access payroll and was denied" is exactly the line an inspector wants to find: it proves the walls are load-bearing.

The second signature: human approval gates

Some actions are cheap to reverse; some are not. Reading a balance is harmless. Sending a payment, closing an account, or emailing a customer is consequential — once it happens, it happened. Meera's rule applies unchanged: consequential actions need a second signature. Enterprises call the pattern maker-checker — one party proposes (the maker), a different party with authority approves (the checker). The agent is always the maker, never the checker.

You have already met the machinery. In the human-in-the-loop chapter, LangGraph's interrupt() froze a graph mid-run and Command(resume=...) woke it with the human's verdict; checkpointing makes the pause durable, so an approval can arrive days later and the run resumes where it stopped. Bank-grade platforms wrap that machinery in three extra rules:

  • The approver must have authority. A gate approved by "whoever clicks first" is theatre. Entitlements apply to approvers too.
  • The approval is itself audited. Who approved, when, and what exactly they saw when they signed.
  • Gates are tiered, not universal. If every trivial read demands a signature, humans start rubber-stamping and the gate protects nothing. Classify actions by consequence and gate the tier that deserves it.

The building: data residency and what leaves the boundary

Meera's branch has one more quiet rule: customer files do not leave the building. The agent equivalent is easy to miss. Every time your harness calls a hosted model API, the entire prompt — the user's question plus every document, record, and tool result you stuffed into context — travels to the vendor's servers. Data residency is the legal requirement that certain data stays within a given country or region; several jurisdictions impose it on financial and personal data (the EU's data-protection regime and India's payment-data rules are two public examples).

So a bank-grade evaluation asks precisely: what leaves the boundary, to where, and what does the vendor keep? The usual answers, in increasing strictness: region-pinned API endpoints, private deployments in the company's own cloud tenancy, on-premises models for the most sensitive work, plus redaction layers that strip personal identifiers first. And separate from where: a written zero-data-retention agreement — a contract that the vendor does not store your prompts or train on them. If a vendor cannot state their retention terms in one clear sentence, that is your answer.

The model on the risk register

Here is a comforting fact: banks did not meet risky models for the first time in 2023. Credit-scoring, fraud, and trading models have been regulated for decades under formal supervisory guidance on model risk management — the discipline of treating every model as a thing that can be wrong, and building process around that fact. An LLM entering a bank does not get a shrug; it joins an existing register, with four standing obligations:

  • Inventory and versioning. Which model, which exact version, in which workflow. "The API silently upgraded" is not an acceptable sentence — versions are pinned and changes are deliberate.
  • Validation before use. Documented evidence the model performs acceptably on the intended task — for LLM agents, an evaluation suite with recorded results, not a vibe check.
  • Ongoing monitoring. Quality drifts; usage drifts. Someone watches the metrics after go-live, and thresholds trigger review.
  • Documented limitations. Known failure modes — the ones you catalogued in the failure-modes chapter — written where the auditor can find them, mitigations beside them.

One phrase to remember: evidence over confidence. Model risk management is the paperwork shape of humility.

The replaceable teller: vendor and model swap-ability

The branch does not close when a teller resigns. A bank-grade platform must survive its model vendor raising prices, changing terms, or disappearing — some regulators explicitly examine exit plans for critical third-party dependencies (concentration risk is the formal name). Lock-in creeps in through prompts hand-tuned to one model's quirks, proprietary vendor features, fine-tuned models locked to one cloud, and tool-calling formats that differ across providers.

The defences are all things you have met. An abstraction layer at the model call — LangChain's init_chat_model exists precisely so the model is a config string, not an architecture. Open protocols at the tool layer — MCP means your tools survive a model swap untouched. And the quiet hero: your evaluation suite is your portability insurance. Swapping vendors is terrifying when "does it still work?" is opinion, and routine when it is a test run. The eval suite from model risk management is the same artifact that makes the exit plan real — one investment, two regulatory boxes.

Check yourself

1. Why is running an agent on one powerful shared service account an anti-pattern in a bank?

The god account fails two questions at once: "who did it?" becomes unanswerable, and the agent inherits powers its user never had. On-behalf-of identity fixes both.

2. What separates an audit trail from the observability logs you already keep?

Different customer, different guarantees. Debug logs can be truncated tomorrow; an audit trail must prove, years later, that a record was never edited — including records of blocked actions.

3. Where must the "may this user do this?" check live, and why?

Anything expressed only in the prompt can be talked around. An entitlement lookup that refuses the call is a wall — and regulators only accept walls.

4. Your harness calls a hosted model API. What actually leaves your data boundary?

Context is cargo: whatever you loaded into the prompt travels with it — so residency reviews ask what goes, to which region, and what is retained.

5. What single artifact does the most work for BOTH model risk management and vendor swap-ability?

Evals are the validation evidence before go-live, the monitoring baseline after, and the re-certification that makes vendor swaps routine. One investment, two boxes.

Key takeaways

  • "Enterprise-ready" means being able to answer three questions for every action ever taken: who did it, with whose permission, and how do you know.
  • On-behalf-of identity makes the agent exactly as powerful as the human it serves; entitlements are enforced in code at the tool boundary — prompts persuade, walls enforce.
  • An audit trail is not debug logging: append-only, tamper-evident (WORM, hash chains), kept for years, recording blocked proposals and approvals too.
  • Consequential actions get maker-checker gates — durable pauses where an authorised human signs, and the signature is itself audited.
  • Every hosted-API call ships your whole prompt across the boundary; ask what leaves, to which region, and what the vendor retains — in writing.
  • LLMs join the bank's existing model-risk register: pinned versions, validation before use, monitoring after, documented limitations.
  • Your eval suite is both the model-risk evidence and the exit plan; the next chapter builds these rows into a full scoring method.
