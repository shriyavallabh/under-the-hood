AI-assisted engineering, done right

The same assistant can make you dramatically better or quietly hollow. The difference is one rule and two working modes — and by the end of this chapter they will be muscle memory.

~25 minChapter 107 of 135
The story

Two friends move to a new city the same month, and both need to learn to drive. The first hires a chauffeur. Every morning the chauffeur glides him through traffic while he answers email in the back seat. It feels like winning. A year later he still cannot drive — and the day the chauffeur calls in sick, he cannot get to the one meeting that matters.

The second hires a driving instructor. She sits in the driver's seat from day one. The instructor beside her explains the clutch, lets her stall the car in an empty lot, quizzes her before every roundabout — "who has right of way here?" — and grabs the wheel only when something is genuinely about to go wrong. Six months in, she drives anywhere alone, and the instructor's role has shrunk to the occasional consult on unfamiliar roads.

Same city, same traffic, same amount of help. The difference was never the helper — it was who held the wheel. Your AI assistant will happily be the chauffeur or the instructor. It becomes whichever one you ask for. This chapter is about asking for the instructor.

Two ways to hold the same tool

You have spent this whole act becoming a working engineer: committing with git, opening merge requests, reading diffs like a reviewer, and letting tests gate your merges. AI assistance sits on top of all of it — and it comes in two fundamentally different shapes.

Ghostwriter mode is when you describe an outcome, the AI produces the whole change, and you paste it in without really reading it — the work ships, but the understanding never enters your head. It is the chauffeur. It feels fast, and for a throwaway script it is honestly fine. But for code that lands in a shared repository under your name, ghostwriter mode has a compounding cost: every ghostwritten change widens the gap between what you have shipped and what you actually understand, and that gap always gets discovered at the worst possible moment — in a review, in an incident, in a meeting where someone asks a simple question and watches you go quiet.

The alternative is two modes that build skill instead of hiding gaps. Tutor mode is using the AI to teach you — explaining code, quizzing you, unpacking design decisions — with no code changing at all. Pair mode is building together in small steps, where the AI proposes and you dispose: you read every line, run every change, and accept nothing you cannot explain.

One rule governs everything, and it is worth memorising word for word:

Note

Never ship a change you cannot defend in review. Not "never use AI" — use it constantly. But when a reviewer asks "why did you catch the exception here and not one level up?", the answer "the AI wrote it" is not an answer anyone respects. The merge request carries your name, git blame will point at you at 2 a.m. six months from now, and the only acceptable state is that you can walk through every line and say why it is there. Whoever typed it, you own it.

Mode one: the AI as tutor

The tutor is the most underused mode, and it is pure profit: no risk, no diff, just understanding entering your head faster than any textbook could put it there. Three prompt patterns carry most of the weight.

Explain-this-code. Paste any function that confuses you and ask for a line-by-line walkthrough — but always tell the tutor what you already know, so it pitches the explanation at your level instead of at a beginner's or an expert's. You did exactly this manually in the last chapter when you mapped a strange repository; the tutor turns a forty-minute head-scratch into five minutes of dialogue.

Quiz-me. Reading an explanation feels like learning; being tested reveals whether it was. Ask the AI to quiz you — one question at a time, waiting for your answer, correcting your wording and not just your conclusion. This is the same active-recall trick this course's quizzes use, and you can point it at anything: decorators, pipeline YAML, the codebase you were assigned yesterday.

Why-is-this-designed-so. The deepest of the three. Code tells you what; it rarely tells you why. Ask: "Why a queue here instead of calling the worker directly? What would break under load if it were a direct call?" Design rationale is exactly the knowledge that separates someone who can type code from someone who can defend it — and it is the knowledge reviewers probe for.

Mode two: the AI as pair

Pair mode is where code actually changes — and where discipline matters most. The shape is borrowed from human pair programming: one small intention at a time, a visible proposal, and a navigator (you) who approves every move. Big generated changes are the failure mode; a five-hundred-line diff outruns anyone's ability to review honestly, which quietly converts pair mode back into ghostwriting. Small steps keep you inside your own headlights.

Verify like you mean it: run it, test it, read the diff

Modern AI code has a dangerous property: it looks finished. Clean names, tidy docstrings, plausible structure. Human beginners write code that looks as uncertain as it is; AI writes code whose polish is completely uncorrelated with its correctness. So verification cannot be a vibe — it has to be three mechanical habits you run every single time.

Read the diff — every line, before anything executes, predicting what it does. Run it — actually exercise the changed path, because a read-through catches logic you disagree with but not behaviour you did not anticipate (and when it breaks, you can now read the traceback yourself). Test it — run the suite, and add the edge case the AI did not think of, because a green suite only vouches for the cases it contains.

The honest-positioning rule

Everything so far happens at your desk. This last part happens in rooms — standups, reviews, design discussions — and it decides your reputation. The rule: never fake depth. When you have used AI heavily, say so plainly and pair it with what you personally verified: "I built this with AI assistance; I've read and can walk through every line; I tested the uneven-tail case by hand; the part I'm still shaky on is the backoff maths." That sentence is not a confession — it is the most credible thing an engineer can say, because it shows exactly where the solid ground ends.

The alternative — bluffing an answer about code you do not understand — has a brutal asymmetry. Honest "I'll find out and come back to you" costs you nothing and is forgotten by lunch. Being caught improvising fake depth once can cost you a year of credibility, because now every confident answer you give gets silently discounted. Data scientists and senior engineers are extremely good at detecting the bluff; the probing "can you walk me through this function?" in a review is often precisely that detector.

And when you do get caught not knowing — because you will, everyone does — you now have the perfect recovery loop: take the question back to tutor mode. "Why is this designed so?", understand it for real, and return with the actual answer. Done consistently, this converts every gap a reviewer finds into a permanent piece of your competence. That is the deeper principle under this whole chapter: convert AI leverage into real understanding. Every AI-assisted change should leave a residue — one concept, one API, one design pattern that is now yours. The ghostwritten engineer and the pair-mode engineer ship at similar speeds this quarter; by next year, one of them can architect systems and the other still cannot drive.

You already know this works, because you are the case study. You arrived at this course operating production AI systems you could not read the source of. Every chapter since has been exactly this workflow — explanation, prediction, quiz, small runnable steps — turning leverage into understanding, one concept at a time. The capstone is two chapters away, and you will build it with AI at your side. Build it in pair mode, and it will be genuinely, defensibly yours.

In the wild

You can spot both modes in any team's GitLab. Ghostwritten merge requests have a signature: huge, oddly uniform, over-commented, and an author who goes quiet when asked "why this data structure?". Pair-mode engineers ship small MRs whose descriptions say what was verified and how — and they answer review questions fluently, in seconds. Many organisations now write the rule of this chapter into policy almost word for word: use any AI you like; you own every line you merge. Some ask that AI assistance be noted in the MR description or commit message. And in reviews you will increasingly see the polite probe — "walk me through this bit?" — which is not hostility. It is the review system checking for exactly the ownership this chapter taught you to build.

Check yourself

1. Which change is acceptable to merge, according to this chapter's one rule?

The rule is about ownership, not authorship. AI can type every character, as long as your understanding stands behind every line — because the merge request, git blame, and the 2 a.m. incident call all carry your name.

2. The chunk() function in the runner looked polished and would have passed a lazy test suite. What is the lesson?

The bug (dropping the uneven tail) hid behind clean naming and a tidy docstring, and only an awkward-length input exposed it. Each verification habit catches what the other two miss — that is why it is a trio, not a pick-one.

3. Which of these is a tutor-mode prompt?

Tutor mode changes your head, not the code: explain-this-code, quiz-me, why-is-this-designed-so. The other two are ghostwriter asks — outcomes delivered with your understanding bypassed.

4. Why does pair mode insist on small steps instead of one big generated change?

Step size is what keeps your understanding in the loop. Nine lines you can hold completely in your head; five hundred lines you can only skim — and skimming is how unowned code gets merged under your name.

5. A reviewer asks why your AI-assisted change uses a queue instead of a direct call, and you genuinely do not know. Best move?

Honest positioning plus the tutor loop turns every exposed gap into permanent competence. Bluffing risks your long-term credibility on one roll of the dice, and deflecting to the AI concedes you shipped a line you cannot defend.

Key takeaways

  • The same assistant is a chauffeur or a driving instructor — your prompts decide which, and only one of them leaves you able to drive alone.
  • One rule governs all of it: never ship a change you cannot defend in review. Whoever typed it, the merge request and git blame carry your name.
  • Tutor mode builds understanding with zero risk: explain-this-code, quiz-me, why-is-this-designed-so — plus predict-then-check to test your mental model, not just fill it.
  • Pair mode is a loop with you as the gate: one small intent, one small diff, read every line, run it and test it, then commit. Big generated diffs silently turn pairing back into ghostwriting.
  • AI code looks finished whether or not it is — polish is uncorrelated with correctness, so verification is three mechanical habits: read the diff, run it, test the awkward case.
  • Never fake depth: "AI-assisted, verified X, still shaky on Y" builds trust; one caught bluff destroys it. Convert every gap into a tutor session, and AI leverage compounds into real skill.
